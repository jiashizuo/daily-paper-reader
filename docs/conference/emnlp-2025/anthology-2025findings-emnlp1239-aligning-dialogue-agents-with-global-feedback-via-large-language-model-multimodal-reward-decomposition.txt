# Aligning Dialogue Agents with Global Feedback via Large Language Model Multimodal Reward Decomposition 

Dong Won Lee 1 Hae Won Park 1 Cynthia Breazeal 1 Louis-Philippe Morency 2

MIT 1, CMU 2

dongwonl@mit.edu 

Abstract 

We propose a large language model based re-ward decomposition framework for aligning dialogue agents using only a single session-level feedback signal. We leverage the reason-ing capabilities of a frozen, pretrained large language model (LLM) to infer fine-grained local implicit rewards by decomposing global, session-level feedback. Our first text-only vari-ant prompts the LLM to perform reward de-composition using only the dialogue transcript. The second multimodal variant incorporates ad-ditional behavioral cues, such as pitch, gaze, and facial affect, expressed as natural language descriptions. These inferred turn-level rewards are distilled into a lightweight reward model, which we utilize for RL-based fine-tuning for dialogue generation. We evaluate both text-only and multimodal variants against state-of-the-art reward decomposition methods and demonstrate notable improvements in human evaluations of conversation quality, suggesting that LLMs are strong reward decomposers that obviate the need for manual reward shaping and granular human feedback. 

1 Introduction 

As dialogue agents become increasingly deployed in open-ended, long-form social interactions, the challenge of aligning model behavior with trajectory-level human preferences becomes more pressing. While large language models (LLMs) have achieved strong linguistic competence, align-ment techniques such as reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) (Ouyang et al., 2022) remain fundamentally constrained by their reliance on fine-grained, turn-level annotations. In many realistic settings, such as therapy, education, or companionship, annotators provide feedback only at the session level, reflecting their overall impres-sion of the entire interaction. This presents a major obstacle: how can one use sparse, global feedback to improve local behavior? In this paper, we first introduce LLM-GELI , a 

Large-Language Model based solution to decom-pose Global Explicit feedback into Local Implicit turn-level supervision signals that can be used for RLHF. We leverage a frozen pretrained LLM as a reward decomposition oracle. Given the full dialogue and a scalar global reward, the LLM is prompted to infer how utterances contributed to the final outcome score. The result is a turn-level reward decomposition derived purely from the LLM’s reasoning over the linguistic context. These decomposed signals are then used to train a lightweight, text-only reward function that can guide downstream policy learning. Furthermore, we introduce Multimodal-LLM-GELI , an extension of our core framework that further incorporates multimodal behavioral signals—such as facial expressions, gaze, and prosody—into the reward decomposition process. Building on recent advances in multimodal prompt-ing, we represent the listener’s nonverbal reactions as natural language descriptors and append them to each utterance in the dialogue history. This tex-tual augmentation enables the language model to interpret affective and social cues in context, allow-ing it to reason about the listener’s internal state and how it evolves throughout the conversation. By grounding decomposition in the listener’s multi-modal feedback, Multimodal-LLM-GELI produces turn-level reward estimates that more accurately re-flect the listener’s extralinguistic reactions. We evaluate our approach on the CANDOR dataset (Reece et al., 2023), which features long-form video conversations and post-session sur-vey ratings, and an out-of-distribution dataset, SODA (Kim et al., 2023a). Our results show that LLM-GELI and Multimodal-LLM-GELI outper-form prior baselines across a range of human and automatic metrics. Notably, Multimodal-LLM-GELI achieves the strongest gains on affective and socially grounded human evaluation measures

2 Related Works 

Multimodal Prompting Recent advances in mul-timodal learning highlight the growing potential of large language models (LLMs) as universal inter-faces for integrating and reasoning across vision, language, audio, and sensor modalities. Socratic Models (Zeng et al., 2022) introduced a modu-lar framework for zero-shot multimodal reasoning by composing frozen unimodal models—such as CLIP (Radford et al., 2021) and GPT-3—through language-based hypothesis exchange, eliminating the need for joint training. Building on this paradigm, MM-ReAct (Yang et al., 2023) leverages ChatGPT to orchestrate visual experts (e.g., OCR, object detectors) via prompt-based planning. Fur-thermoe, recent efforts have explored using LLMs for time-series and sensor data through language interfaces. Health-LLM (Kim et al., 2023b) and PHIA (Merrill et al., 2024) convert wearable sen-sor data into enriched natural language prompts, enabling personalized health prediction and recom-mendation in a zero-shot setting. Together, these works illustrate a growing shift toward using lan-guage as a medium for zero-shot reasoning with multimodal behavioral data. 

Reward Decomposition in Reinforcement Learn-ing Reward Decomposition, also known as Tem-poral Credit Assignment (TCA), is a fundamental concept in reinforcement learning that addresses the challenge of determining how past actions con-tribute to future outcomes. Effective TCA enables an agent to properly attribute delayed rewards to the decisions that caused them, thus improving learning efficiency and policy quality. One clas-sical approach to TCA involves manipulating the 

λ-return in temporal-difference learning, which ad-justs the balance between short-term and long-term credit assignment (Petrik and Scherrer, 2008; Jiang et al., 2015). More recent advances have reformu-lated TCA as a return decomposition problem. For instance, RUDDER (Arjona-Medina et al., 2019) redistributes rewards by measuring the predictive information gained between consecutive states, al-lowing for more precise step-wise credit assign-ment. IRCR (Gangwani et al., 2020) implements a uniform redistribution scheme, while Randomized Return Decomposition (RRD) (Ren et al., 2021) frames the problem as a Monte Carlo regression task, using least-squares estimation to infer per-step rewards from trajectory-level returns. 

LLMs for Reward Decomposition Recent work has explored using large language models (LLMs) to support reward decomposition in reinforcement learning (RL). For instance, LaRe (Qu et al., 2025) prompts an LLM to generate a latent code that is then decoded into scalar rewards which sum to the total episodic return. CALM (Pignatelli et al., 2024) employs an LLM to identify when subgoals are achieved, assigning auxiliary rewards in a zero-shot manner. In contrast, recent alignment methods like SPIN (Chen et al., 2024) and (Go et al., 2023) treat LLMs as reward functions, using them to rank or evaluate outputs via self-play or preference mod-eling—requiring dense or per-turn supervision. On the other-hand, GELI (Lee et al., 2024) is closely related to reward decomposition for dialogue mod-elling, however, it does not utilize an LLM, instead it utilizes a manually designed reward shaping func-tion. Our method, LLM-GELI, is the first to lever-age a pretrained LLM that decomposes a global end-of-dialogue reward into fine-grained, turn-level signals. Furthermore, by prompting an LLM with multimodal behavioral feedback (e.g., facial affect, prosody), we infer fine-grained turn-level rewards from sparse, trajectory-level supervision. 

3 Background: Aligning Language Models 

We consider the problem of generating conversa-tional responses using an autoregressive language 22756 model in a multi-sensory setting. We model the lan-guage generator as a dialogue agent with a policy

4 Methods: LLM-GELI 

In many long-form dialogue settings, we often lack fine-grained annotations for individual utterances. Instead, we may only have access to a single global explicit (GE) reward, a scalar score assigned at the end of an interaction that reflects the overall im-pression or outcome, such as how positively the user felt about the conversation. A key challenge is how to decompose this global reward into per-turn feedback that can train a reward model capable of assigning appropriate values to individual utter-ances. We address this challenge using a Large Language Model GELI (LLM-GELI) framework, which proceeds in two stages (1) Global Explicit (GE) Reward Decomposition using the zero-shot prompting capabilities of a pretrained LLM and (2) Local Implicit (LI) Reward Modeling.

4.2 Reward Modeling with LLM-Derived Local Implicit Rewards 

Once turn-level rewards are obtained from the LLM, they are treated as local implicit (LI) supervi-sion signals, rLI , and used to train a lightweight re-ward function rθ, which conditions only on the tex-tual dialogue context, i.e., state-action pairs (st, a t).Although the Multimodal-LLM-GELI had access to aligned multimodal cues during decomposition, the reward model rθ does not. 5 Experiments 

In this section, we evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed LLM-GELI framework for preference-based learning, namely reward modeling and lan-guage model adaptation in long-form dialogue. Therefore, our evaluation consists of two stages. First, we train reward functions using the various methods of global explicit reward decomposition, which we denote as “ GE ”, and/or local implicit reward supervision, which we denote as “ LI ”. Sec-ond, we fine-tune a dialogue language model using Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) (Schulman et al., 2017), guided by the learned reward func-tions to improve response generation. Due to com-putational restraints, all reward function training and RL fine-tuning procedures are executed with a single run. We assess each method’s impact using both automatic and human-centric metrics. 

5.1 Dataset 

We conduct our training and evaluation experi-ments using the CANDOR dataset (Reece et al., 2023), chosen for its long-form, naturalistic di-alogue structure—featuring an average of 159.4 turns, 31.3 minutes per conversation, and 17.81 words per turn—along with its scale (1,656 con-versations, over 7 million words, and 850 hours of data). CANDOR also includes aligned video recordings, enabling multimodal analysis common in face-to-face conversation corpora. We use this dataset both to train reward functions and to sample dialogue contexts for response generation. Specifi-cally, we construct disjoint splits: a held-out set of approximately 30,000 dialogue history–utterance pairs for reward model training, and a larger set of roughly 100,000 pairs for reinforcement learn-ing. The global explicit (GE) signal is derived from the post-interaction survey item: “Overall during your conversation, to what extent did you feel pos-itive feelings (e.g., good, pleasant, happy) or neg-ative feelings (e.g., bad, unpleasant, unhappy)?”. To assess generalization, we also evaluate on the 

SODA dataset (Kim et al., 2023a), a large-scale syn-thetic social dialogue corpus generated via GPT-3.5 and grounded in a structured social commonsense knowledge graph. Human evaluations indicate that SODA conversations are more consistent, natural, and specific than other human-authored datasets. It contains 1.5 million dialogues, with an average of 7.6 turns and 16.1 words per turn. 

Multimodal Features We utilize a set of 17 mul-timodal conversational features capturing prosodic, visual-social, and linguistic cues to model the affective dynamics of interactions. Audio-based prosodic features include (1) f0 (fundamental frequency, representing pitch) calculated using the parselmouth library (Jadoul et al., 2018), (2) 

intensity (voice amplitude) from a predictor trained on the RAVDESS dataset (Livingstone and Russo, 2018), (3) jitter (indicative of pitch instability from disvoice library (Dehak et al., 2007), and (4) log energy (signal power, capturing loudness) from librsoa library (McFee et al., 2020). Visual-social features extracted from the video modality include detectors such as (5) gaze (whether the speaker is looking on-screen) calculated based on white pixel value counts, (6) nod_yes , (7) nod_no (indicating head nodding behaviors) where a nod is defined as having a nod zero crossing threshold >= 2 22758 Figure 2: Comparison of reward decomposition across models on a CANDOR dialogue segment. Language Descriptors To enable effective inte-gration with language models, we convert contin-uous multimodal behavioral features, which vary over time, into discrete natural language descriptors aligned with each utterance. Since these features are sampled at a different temporal resolutions than utterances (e.g., per frame or per second), we aggre-gate the feature values over the utterance duration by computing their mean and appending them to the corresponding utterances. This formulation allows us to leverage the zero-shot multimodal reasoning capabilities of large language models, as outlined in prior work on language-as-interface paradigms. An example of such processed sample is included in the Supplementary. 

Focusing on Listener Reactions Since our goal is to decompose a global reward based on the lis-tener’s affective response, we condition the model exclusively on the multimodal features of the lis-tener (i.e., the participant who completed the post-interaction survey and provided the overall affec-tive rating). This choice centers the reward attribu-tion on the reactions and experiences of the evalua-tor rather than the speaker, naturally aligning with the perspective-taking required for accurate reward decomposition.

5.3 Fine-Tuning Language Models via Reinforcement Learning 

We adapt the LLaMA-2 model (Touvron et al., 2023) 2 using reinforcement learning with feed-back from the trained reward functions. Specifi-cally, we employ the PPO-based RLHF framework from the TRL library (von Werra et al., 2020), with LoRA (Hu et al., 2021) applied to reduce memory and compute overhead. The complete hyperparam-eter settings are provided in Appendix E. 

Evaluation of Generated Responses. We con-duct a human evaluation following prior proto-cols (Lee et al., 2022, 2024), using 9 criteria com-monly used to assess dialogue quality. A total of 300 crowd workers were recruited via Amazon Me-chanical Turk. Annotators were presented with dialogue histories and model-generated responses, and asked to identify which model best satisfied each criterion. At the end of the task, participants were asked which model they would prefer to con-verse with again. This is described in Section 6.1.

6.3 Comparison of Decomposed Rewards 

Figure 2 presents a turn-by-turn comparison of re-ward decompositions from different models on a CANDOR dialogue segment. Across the conver-sation, we observe that MM-LLM GELI (purple) consistently aligns rewards with socially mean-ingful content (e.g., self-disclosures, affirmations), while discounting low-affect or filler utterances like “Mhm” or “Okay.” In contrast, baseline methods like GELI (blue), VA (pink), and LS (orange) often misattribute reward to neutral or socially uninfor-mative turns, or fail to identify affective shifts. The RRD and IRCR models, optimized for global loss, show erratic or flat credit assignment. The key difference between MM-LLM Decomp. 

and LLM Decomp. lies in multimodal ground-ing: MM-LLM uses local visual affect (e.g., fa-cial expressions, gaze) to inform reward assign-ments, allowing it to distinguish between utterances with similar surface forms but divergent social im-pacts—something the language-only variant cannot reliably capture. In the segment where Speaker A asks, “So um are you done with school? have you finished college?”, we observe a key divergence in how models assigned rewards. Both GELI and LLM Decomp. attribute either neutral or slightly positive reward, likely based solely on the linguis-22762 tic surface (a question showing interest). However, MM-LLM Decomp. assigns a clearly negative re-ward. This contrast arises because MM-LLM in-corporates multimodal cues—such as facial expres-sion, vocal hesitancy, or disengaged affect from the listener—which may signal discomfort or a nega-tive emotional reaction to the question. 

7 Conclusion 

We introduce a new paradigm for preference-based alignment of conversational agents by offloading reward decomposition to large language models. Unlike prior methods that rely on manually engi-neered functions to interpret nonverbal cues, LLM-GELI leverages the zero-shot reasoning capabilities of pretrained LLMs to infer turn-level rewards from a single global trajectory score. Our Multimodal-LLM-GELI extension further integrates nonverbal feedback through natural language descriptors, en-abling rich, socially grounded reward modeling without requiring architectural changes or multi-modal training. Empirical results on the CANDOR dataset show that our approach outperforms the state-of-the-art in human and automatic evaluations. These findings underscore the potential of LLMs as scalable and domain-agnostic reward decom-posers, bridging the gap between sparse feedback and fine-grained behavioral alignment in real-world dialogue systems. This work opens the door to more generalizable, interpretable, and low-friction alignment methods for long-form, multimodal in-teractions. 

8 Limitations 

While LLM-GELI introduces a scalable and in-terpretable approach to reward decomposition, its effectiveness depends critically on the reasoning capabilities of the underlying large language model. Our method assumes that the pretrained LLM has sufficient social and affective knowledge to infer turn-level credit assignments from global feedback. However, these inferences are ultimately shaped by the LLM’s pretraining data, which may omit domain-specific conversational dynamics or cultur-ally situated behaviors. As such, reward assign-ments may be inconsistent or misaligned in unfa-miliar contexts. The decomposition procedure is also inherently sensitive to prompt design. Despite using struc-tured prompts grounded in dialogue and behavioral context, small changes in prompt wording or in-put formatting can lead to significant variation in the resulting reward signals. This introduces in-stability and reduces reproducibility, particularly when transferring to new domains or LLM back-bones. Addressing prompt sensitivity remains an open challenge in prompt-based credit assignment methods. For the multimodal variant, the quality of de-composition depends on the accuracy of behavioral signal extraction and its subsequent translation into natural language descriptors. While this textual ab-straction enables compatibility with language-only LLMs, it may obscure or distort the nuances of the original multimodal data—especially in cases where behavioral cues are ambiguous or conflict-ing. The resulting reward signals are thus only as reliable as the upstream perception and descriptor generation pipelines. There are risks that could arise as a result of more social, dialogue agents that can interact with people in a long-term interaction. Conversational agents could be used maliciously for deception, manipulation, and the spread of misinformation. Furthermore, conversational agents which use mul-timodal data could enhance seriousness of these issues, as models can detect subtle cues such as microexpressions to infer and manipulate the user. As a potential measure to mitigate such misuse, we plan to release our code and model weights un-der a license which prevents the use of our assets by any party that support or contribute to false imper-sonation or hate speech (Do No Harm, Nonviolent Public or Hippocratic License).

J Consistency in LLM-based Reward Decomposition 

To assess the consistency of the LLM-based reward decomposition, we queried an LLM for 10 con-versations twice with temperature 1, and categorized each turn-level reward into three classes: positive (> 0), negative/neutral ( 0), or no annotation (NaN). Treating two independently sampled LLM reward decomposition outputs, we computed agreement across these 3-class labels. The agreement was 89.10% ± 5.37%, indicating that the model consistently identifies both the presence and polarity of rewards. This supports the reliability of LLM-generated signals as stable supervisory feedback. 22787
