Abstract 4

Source-space electroencephalography (EEG) connectomics aims to estimate interactions among 5

cortical generators from sensor cross-spectra that are mixed by the head and lead field. This task is 6

difficult because marginal source covariance or coherence can retain leakage, common drive and in-7

direct mediation, whereas developmental mapping requires conditional interactions that can be es-8

timated repeatedly across large cohorts. We developed JSPACE (Joint Source-space Precision And 9

Cross-spectral Estimation), a frequency-domain inverse framework for estimating multi-frequency 10 

source precision matrices from scalp cross-spectra. JSPACE couples posterior source cross-spectral 11 

estimation with standardized precision fitting, sparse frequency-smooth anatomical regularization, 12 

stochastic active-set optimization and post-selection refitting. In simulations, its advantage was 13 

target-specific: JSPACE reduced coherence inflation and achieved the lowest imaginary-coherence 14 

and peak-frequency errors in a forward neural-mass benchmark. When the ground-truth preci-15 

sion matrix was known, it achieved the highest exact, edge-collapsed and leakage-aware support 16 

recovery. We applied JSPACE to HarMNqEEG cross-spectral data from 1,935 participants aged 17 

5.17–97.00 years, spanning 47 frequency bins and 360 cortical parcels. Affine-invariant Karcher 18 

tangent harmonization reconstructed subject-level estimates into a lifespan atlas of 360 diagonal 19 

and 64,620 source-pair age-frequency surfaces. The atlas revealed a continuous off-diagonal mor-20 

phology landscape, in which age direction, frequency preference and interaction strength varied as 21 

overlapping axes rather than discrete edge classes. In contrast, diagonal precision surfaces shared 22 

a conserved alpha-trough morphology across parcels. Representative real-precision pathways cap-23 

tured posterior parietal, sensorimotor-parietal, frontopolar and visual-parietal motifs. Delta-band 24 

gradients were moderately aligned with the sensorimotor-association (S-A) organization of cortex 25 

from childhood through late adulthood, with a candidate oldest-old deviation in the sparsest age 26 

range. JSPACE provides a scalable framework for frequency-resolved source-precision charting in 27 

lifespan EEG. 28 

Keywords: EEG source imaging; precision matrix; conditional dependence; cross-spectral analysis; 29 

graphical models; lifespan development; normative EEG 30 

# 1 Introduction 31 

Resting EEG and MEG resolve neuronal activity at millisecond time scales and are therefore well suited 32 

to frequency-resolved connectomics. However, the signals recorded at the scalp do not correspond one-33 

to-one to cortical generators. Under the forward model, each sensor cross-spectrum is a mixture of 34 

source cross-spectra projected through the head and lead field [1, 2]. As a result, field spread and 35 

source leakage can make generators appear associated even when the apparent association is driven 36 

by spatial mixing, common drive or indirect pathways [3–5]. For electrophysiological connectomics, 37

Classical and Bayesian source imaging methods provided the foundation for estimating local cortical 41 

activity from M/EEG, including minimum-norm, distributed tomographic and prior-constrained source 42 

solutions [6–8]. Frequency-domain extensions such as dynamic imaging of coherent sources made it 43 

possible to study oscillatory source power and coherence within an inverse framework [9]. In parallel, 44 

connectivity measures and corrections were developed to reduce sensitivity to spatial leakage, including 45 

the imaginary part of coherency, multivariate orthogonalization and explicit cautions about source-46 

space ghost interactions [10–12]. These strategies address important sources of bias, but they do not 47 

by themselves define a statistical model of latent source interactions. Leakage-robust measures can 48 

lose sensitivity to near-zero-lag physiological coupling, whereas two-stage workflows can carry residual 49 

source-estimation errors into downstream pairwise connectivity measures [13]. Source connectivity 50 

therefore remains an inverse statistical problem rather than a post-processing problem. 51 

This shifts the modeling target from source amplitudes alone to source cross-spectral statistics. 52 

Cross-spectral electrophysiological source imaging formalizes the estimation of latent source cross-53 

spectra under the lead-field model, and spectral structured sparse Bayesian learning has shown that 54 

structured priors can reduce distortions in this high-dimensional inverse problem [14]. Cross-spectra 55 

and covariance matrices, however, describe marginal association. They do not distinguish a direct 56 

pairwise association from dependence induced through the rest of the modeled network. Precision 57 

matrices, the inverses of covariance or cross-spectral matrices, provide the corresponding conditional-58 

dependence representation under Gaussian spectral models. Intuitively, an off-diagonal precision entry 59 

asks whether two sources remain statistically coupled after the activity of all other modeled sources 60 

has been taken into account. A zero off-diagonal entry corresponds to conditional independence in 61 

the Gaussian model, whereas covariance or coherence can remain nonzero because of common drive, 62 

indirect mediation or spatial mixing. Sparse inverse covariance estimation and joint graphical models 63 

show how regularization can recover interpretable graphs and borrow strength across related conditions 64 

[15, 16]. For M/EEG, hidden Gaussian graphical spectral models have already brought this idea into 65 

source-space inverse modeling through Hermitian graphical regularization [17]. 66 

Source-space precision estimation is not obtained by simply applying a graphical lasso after source 67 

reconstruction. The quantity available to the precision model is a posterior source moment shaped by 68 

the lead field, the residual sensor model and the regularization used in the inverse step. If this moment 69 

is passed directly to a precision fit, local spectral power differences can dominate the graph and obscure 70 

weaker conditional dependencies. Fitting each frequency independently introduces a second instability, 71 

because neighboring spectral bins carry related physiological and sampling information. Anatomical 72 

connectivity provides another constraint, but it should act as a soft source of regularization rather 73 

than imposing a hard support constraint a priori. These considerations lead to a coupled formulation 74 

in which source-moment estimation and precision fitting are linked, and in which standardization, 75 

frequency sharing and anatomical weighting are built into the estimator. 76 

The need to estimate comparable source-space conditional dependence becomes more demand-77 

ing in lifespan and normative EEG. A population atlas requires the same inverse estimator to be 78 

applied across many subjects, sites and ages. Its outputs must also remain comparable after harmo-79 

nization. qEEGt addressed this population problem for EEG source spectra through age-corrected 80 

normative statistical parametric mapping, and HarMNqEEG extended the framework to harmonized 81 

multinational EEG cross-spectral tensors [18, 19]. Recent pipelines and models, including CiftiStorm 82 

and ξ-αNET, further show that source-space EEG can be organized at large scale and connected to 83 

structural priors, lifespan modeling and reproducible workflows [20, 21]. The unresolved question is 84 

whether this population neuroinformatics framework can be extended from local spectra or specific 85 

generative-model parameters to frequency-resolved source conditional-dependence charts. 86 

Here we introduce JSPACE (Joint Source-space Precision And Cross-spectral Estimation) to ad-87 

dress this question. JSPACE couples posterior source cross-spectral estimation with multi-frequency 88 

Hermitian precision fitting. Its central modeling step is to standardize posterior source second moments 89 

before precision estimation, so that conditional-dependence structure is not driven primarily by local 90 

power differences. The precision model then combines sparse off-diagonal regularization, smoothness 91 

across neighboring frequencies and soft anatomical weighting, and the fitted precision is returned to 92 

the source scale for the next inverse update. We evaluate JSPACE in simulations that separate source-93

2.3 Simulation benchmarks 168 

The validation strategy follows from the distinction between source-spectral reconstruction and preci-169 

sion estimation. A forward neural-mass benchmark asked whether JSPACE preserved source-spectral 170 

and phase-lagged structure after forward mixing through the same lead-field representation used in 171 

the empirical analysis. This benchmark is useful for source-domain spectral behavior, but it is not a 172 

precision-graph ground truth because the simulated source cross-spectrum can be low rank when the 173 

ensemble count is smaller than the number of parcels. A second reduced-dimensional benchmark there-174 

fore generated sparse Hermitian positive-definite precision matrices with smooth frequency-varying 175 

off-diagonal trajectories. That benchmark directly evaluated precision and partial-correlation errors, 176 

exact support recovery and leakage-aware spatial support recovery. Full simulation equations and 177 

numerical settings are given in Appendix E. 178

Atlas-wide descriptive summaries were used to make the reconstructed metric bank readable at 202 

manuscript scale. For off-diagonal surfaces, normalized surface-shape features were embedded with 203

3.2 JSPACE improves recovery when source precision is the target 245 

The second benchmark matched the simulated ground truth to the estimand of JSPACE. Sparse Her-246 

mitian positive-definite precision matrices were generated with smooth frequency-varying off-diagonal 247 

trajectories and then projected through the forward model. This setting directly tested precision and 248 

normalized partial-correlation error, exact graph support recovery and leakage-aware spatial support 249 

recovery. 250 

The target-matched benchmark showed that the advantage of JSPACE was concentrated in support 251 

recovery and spatially tolerant edge localization (Figure 3). Exact edge-by-frequency recovery was 252 

difficult for all methods, but JSPACE had the highest median exact support F1 (0.1226 [0.1168, 0.1261]) 253 

and edge-collapsed F1 (0.1496 [0.1438, 0.1567]). The advantage became clearer when evaluation allowed 254 

for the spatial ambiguity expected in EEG source imaging. JSPACE achieved the highest lead-field 255 

tolerant recall for both top-3 neighborhoods (0.7356 [0.6883, 0.7566]) and top-5 neighborhoods (0.9098 256 

[0.8914, 0.9212]), and the highest PSF/CTF tolerant top-5 recall (0.9658 [0.9208, 0.9864]). It also had 257 

the smallest true-to-estimated edge distance (0.1819 [0.1728, 0.1921]) and symmetric edge distance 258 

(0.2053 [0.1993, 0.2099]). 259 

This pattern separated graph-support localization from scalar partial-correlation amplitude recov-260 

ery. JSPACE remained below the post-hoc graphical-lasso and source-workflow baselines on partial-261 

correlation error (2.297 [2.108, 2.425]), but it was not the lowest-error method for that scalar target. 262 

Thus, the precision benchmark did not identify JSPACE as uniformly best on every numerical sum-263 

mary. Instead, it showed that JSPACE was strongest for recovering the location of sparse conditional-264 

dependence structure under forward-model ambiguity, which is the validation target most closely 265 

matched to the downstream lifespan precision atlas. 266

3.4 Diagonal precision surfaces share a conserved alpha-trough architec-295 

ture 296 

The diagonal entries of the precision matrix showed a different form of organization from the off-297 

diagonal coupling surfaces. Whereas off-diagonal source pairs occupied a continuous morphology 298 

landscape, diagonal log precision was dominated by a highly reproducible spectral architecture across 299 

cortical parcels (Figure 5). Because diagonal precision represents the local conditional scale of a source 300 

after accounting for the rest of the graph, we analyzed it separately from inter-parcel coupling. 301 

The mean standardized diagonal surface showed a pronounced trough in the alpha range, embedded 302 

within a broader increase toward higher retained frequencies (Figure 5A). This pattern was not driven 303 

by a small subset of parcels. Across all 360 HCP-MMP1 parcels, each parcel surface remained strongly 304 

correlated with the atlas mean surface (median r = 0 .938, IQR 0 .905–0 .967; minimum r = 0 .772; Fig-305 

ure 5C). Frequency profiles sampled at representative ages showed the same alpha-centered depression 306 

across childhood, early adulthood, midlife and older adulthood (Figure 5B). 307 

We quantified the depth of this alpha trough as the mean of the theta and low-beta flanks minus 308 

the mean alpha-band value within each standardized parcel surface. The trough depth was positive 309 

in every parcel ( n = 360; minimum 0 .088, median 1 .096, maximum 1 .664), indicating that the alpha 310 

depression was a conserved feature of local conditional precision rather than an averaging artifact. Its 311 

magnitude nevertheless varied across cortex (Figure 5D). The largest trough depths were concentrated 312 

in bilateral posterior parietal and medial parietal parcels, with additional high values in occipital 313 

and central/sensorimotor-adjacent parcels. Shallower troughs were more evident in frontal/prefrontal, 314 

fronto-opercular and cingulate parcels. Thus, the diagonal layer combined a shared spectral motif 315 

with anatomical modulation. These results justify treating diagonal precision as the local spectral 316 

architecture of the atlas, while the next section focuses on representative off-diagonal real-precision 317 

pathways. 318

as interpretable anchors within the morphology landscape (Figure 6). These pathways were selected 323 

from atlas-wide summaries of effect energy, dominant frequency structure, age-shape class and anatom-324

The left PFm–left PGs pathway showed a posterior parietal alpha basin (Figure 6B). Its frequency 328 

profiles were predominantly negative, but the age slices revealed a change in the location of the strongest 329 

depression: the youngest profiles were most negative at the lowest retained frequencies, whereas profiles 330 

from young adulthood onward showed a deeper alpha-centered basin near 10 Hz. The age profiles 331 

showed the same frequency dependence. Low-frequency and alpha slices became less negative from 332 

childhood into adulthood and then approached a plateau, whereas the higher-frequency slices near 15 333 

and 19 Hz started near zero or slightly positive and progressively decreased toward weakly negative 334 

values in later life. 335 

The right area 1–right PGs pathway showed a sensorimotor-parietal maturation motif (Figure 6C). 336 

Across age slices, the surface retained negative low-frequency values but gained positive values in 337 

the alpha and higher retained frequencies. The age profiles were not uniformly increasing across 338 

the spectrum: the lowest-frequency slice rebounded early and remained negative, whereas theta-to-339 

high-frequency slices increased steadily from childhood into older adulthood. This pathway therefore 340 

combined a persistent low-frequency negative component with progressive strengthening at higher 341 

frequencies. 342 

The interhemispheric frontopolar pathway between left p10p and right p10p showed a different low-343 

frequency attenuation motif (Figure 6D). Early profiles were dominated by large positive low-frequency 344 

real precision, which declined markedly with age. Mid- and high-frequency slices showed smaller adult 345 

peaks, but these did not compensate for the loss of the low-frequency component. Thus, the dominant 346 

developmental feature was not a global decrease across the entire spectrum, but a selective attenuation 347 

of early low-frequency frontopolar coupling. 348 

The visual-parietal pathway between left LO1 and right V3A had the most explicit shape transition 349 

(Figure 6E). In childhood, the frequency profile contained an alpha-range trough, with lower values 350 

near 9 Hz than at the flanking frequencies. In adulthood, this trough was replaced by a positive alpha 351 

peak centered at a slightly higher frequency, around 11 Hz. The age profiles confirmed this transition: 352 

low-to-alpha slices showed inverted-U trajectories with maxima around midlife, whereas the highest-353 

frequency slice was largest at the youngest age and declined across the lifespan. This pathway therefore 354 

captured a developmental retuning from an early alpha depression to an adult alpha-centered positive 355 

coupling motif. 356 

Finally, the right PGs–right POS2 pathway showed posterior parietal attenuation with a frequency-357 

dependent timing of the peak (Figure 6F). Low-frequency slices peaked earlier, around childhood or 358 

early adolescence, whereas the alpha and higher-frequency slices peaked later, from young adulthood 359 

into mid-adulthood. By older age, all sampled frequency slices declined, and several crossed toward 360 

negative real precision. The surface therefore showed both a shift in the frequency location of the 361 

strongest positive component and a late-life attenuation of posterior parietal coupling. 362 

Taken together, the selected pathways illustrate how atlas-level age-frequency variation is expressed 363 

in anatomically distinct source pairs. The examples show that a representative real-precision surface 364 

may differ not only in overall sign or dominant band, but also in the timing of its peak, the frequency 365 

location of its trough or ridge, and whether age changes are shared across frequencies. Thus, Figure 6 366 

gives pathway-level examples of the surface motifs summarized in Figure 4. 367

The cortical maps showed how this reorganization unfolded spatially (Figure 7A). From childhood 384 

through late adulthood, positive gradient scores were concentrated mainly in frontopolar and lat-385 

eral prefrontal association parcels, including frontoparietal network (FPN)- and default-mode network 386 

(DMN)-dominant regions. Negative scores were concentrated in posterior visual-parietal regions early 387 

in life and, from young adulthood onward, increasingly in dorsal parietal, cingulate and sensorimotor-388 

adjacent parcels. After 80 years, the positive frontal/prefrontal pole became markedly compressed. 389 

By the oldest ages, the highest positive scores were weaker and shifted toward occipital and visuopari-390 

etal parcels, whereas the strongest negative scores remained concentrated in parietal-cingulate and 391 

sensorimotor-adjacent regions. 392 

Map similarity gave the same transition point. A two-phase description separated maps from 5–75 393 

years and 80–97 years. Within-phase similarity was high for both phases (mean r = 0 .847 for 5–75 394 

years and r = 0 .895 for 80–97 years), whereas between-phase similarity was lower (mean r = 0 .229). 395 

The multidimensional scaling (MDS) representation showed older-age maps displaced from the main 396 

5–75 year trajectory (Figure 7C,D). 397 

The distribution plot showed the same late-life shift from another angle (Figure 7E). Because each 398 

age-specific gradient was standardized, the vertical spread should be read as the relative ordering of 399 

parcels along the gradient, not as a global change in precision magnitude. At 5 years, positive and 400 

negative tails were approximately balanced. By young adulthood, the positive tail was more populated, 401 

consistent with a prominent association-cortex positive pole. From 80 years onward, no parcels retained 402 

strongly positive scores above +1, whereas a negative tail persisted, indicating compression of the 403 

positive pole and persistence of a parietal-cingulate negative extreme in advanced age. 404 

# 4 Discussion 405 

JSPACE was developed to estimate frequency-resolved precision matrices of latent cortical sources from 406 

scalp cross-spectra. The two simulation benchmarks tested different parts of this goal. The neural-mass 407 

benchmark evaluated source-spectral recovery after forward mixing, including power, coherence and 408 

phase-lagged structure. The precision-ground-truth benchmark instead evaluated whether a method 409 

could recover sparse conditional source relationships. JSPACE performed best for support recovery and 410 

leakage-aware localization of true precision edges, whereas amplitude-level partial-correlation recovery 411 

remained more difficult. These results support a target-specific conclusion. Source precision is not a 412 

substitute for power, covariance or coherence, but a different estimand that requires direct validation 413 

when the scientific question concerns conditional source relationships. 414 

A post-hoc source graph can be difficult to interpret when it is fitted to a source moment that 415 

has already been shaped by the lead field and inverse regularization. JSPACE reduces this problem 416 

by updating posterior source moments and precision matrices within the same forward-model-aware 417 

procedure. The standardization step limits the influence of local spectral scale on the graph. The 418 

multi-frequency penalty uses neighbouring frequency bins to stabilize estimation. The anatomical 419 

weights regularize weakly supported source pairs without fixing the final graph support. Together, 420 

these steps make the reported precision less dependent on local power differences, frequency-bin noise 421 

and hard anatomical exclusions [1, 17]. 422 

In the lifespan atlas, local diagonal terms, off-diagonal source pairs and the delta precision gradient 423 

carried complementary information. Diagonal precision showed a conserved alpha-band trough across 424 

parcels. Off-diagonal precision surfaces occupied a continuous age-frequency embedding rather than a 425 

set of sharply separated edge classes. The leading delta-band precision gradient was moderately aligned 426 

with an external sensorimotor-association reference. These levels are linked by one interpretation. 427 

JSPACE does not only estimate where source activity is large. It estimates how local and pairwise 428 

source relationships remain conditionally structured after other modeled sources have been accounted 429 

for. 430 

The diagonal alpha trough illustrates this distinction most clearly. In conventional log-spectrum 431 

or covariance surfaces, posterior alpha appears as a peak because alpha has high marginal power. In 432 

a precision matrix, the diagonal element has a different meaning. A smaller diagonal precision value 433

indicates larger residual conditional variance for that parcel under the fitted graph. The alpha trough 434 

therefore means that alpha-range source fluctuations remain relatively less predicted by the rest of the 435 

modeled source system. This does not contradict an alpha power peak. It is the conditional-precision 436 

counterpart of a strong local alpha rhythm. 437 

This interpretation is physiologically plausible because resting alpha is partly generated by local 438 

posterior and thalamo-cortical loops. Alpha can dominate marginal power while still carrying local 439 

timing and gating properties that are not fully explained by other cortical parcels. Work on alpha 440 

oscillations supports this view by linking alpha to inhibition, gating and controlled access to information 441 

[29, 30]. The posterior and sensorimotor emphasis of the trough depth also fits known alpha and mu 442 

rhythm topographies. At the same time, this interpretation should remain component-aware. Alpha-443 

band power is affected by both periodic alpha peaks and aperiodic background activity, and these 444 

components can follow different lifespan trajectories [31, 32]. 445 

The diagonal surfaces did not separate into distinct region-specific motifs. Instead, all parcels shared 446 

a similar alpha-trough pattern, although the depth of the trough varied across systems. Developmental 447 

EEG studies help interpret this stability with modulation. In early childhood, the dominant posterior 448 

rhythm can lie in theta or low-alpha frequencies. From later childhood into young adulthood, the 449 

dominant posterior peak shifts upward within the alpha range [33]. In our atlas, this means that 450 

the conserved alpha trough should not be treated as a fixed 10-Hz object throughout life. It is better 451 

viewed as an alpha-related conditional feature whose frequency location and depth can shift as posterior 452 

rhythms mature and as aging changes the balance between periodic and aperiodic spectral components. 453 

The continuous UMAP embedding gives the off-diagonal counterpart of this result. The source-pair 454 

surfaces did not form clean categories because several physiological influences can act on the same pair. 455 

Low-frequency attenuation can reflect the reduction of broad slow coordination during maturation. 456 

Alpha basins and ridges can reflect posterior rhythm timing. Later high-frequency increases can reflect 457 

maturation of long-range synchrony. Late-life weakening can reflect reduced specificity of association 458 

systems. These influences are continuous and can overlap. Similar continuum logic appears in lifespan 459 

connectome studies, where development is organized along sensorimotor-association axes rather than 460 

by isolated regional labels [28, 34, 35]. 461 

The posterior alpha-related pathways represent two variants of the same broad motif. PFm– 462 

PGs showed a negative basin whose strongest depression moved from lower retained frequencies in 463 

childhood toward the alpha range in adulthood. LO1–V3A showed a more explicit retuning, where a 464 

childhood alpha-range trough near 9 Hz became an adult positive ridge closer to 11 Hz. A trough-to-465 

ridge transition means that alpha changed from a frequency of relatively weak or negative conditional 466 

coupling to the frequency of strongest positive coupling. This pattern suggests a retuning of posterior 467 

conditional coordination, not only a rightward shift of an alpha peak. A plausible mechanism is 468 

maturation of posterior feedback timing. As conduction becomes faster and more reliable, visual 469 

and parietal systems can sustain stable coordination at a higher alpha frequency. This interpretation 470 

is consistent with source-level EEG work linking alpha frequency to conduction delay and cortical 471 

myelination, and with models showing that delay and coupling jointly shape alpha synchrony [21, 36]. 472 

The area 1–PGs pathway showed a different pattern. Its low-frequency component stayed negative 473 

or only partially rebounded, whereas theta, alpha and higher retained frequencies became increasingly 474 

positive with age. Area 1 is primary somatosensory cortex, while PGs belongs to posterior parietal 475 

association cortex. The frequency split suggests that slow somatosensory-parietal fluctuations remained 476 

conditionally distinct, while faster components became more positive during maturation. This fits the 477 

broader developmental sequence in which primary sensorimotor systems mature earlier and association 478 

pathways continue to refine later. EEG evidence also shows that neural synchrony undergoes late 479 

restructuring across adolescence, rather than following a simple monotonic increase [34, 37]. 480 

The frontopolar p10p–p10p pathway provides the main low-frequency association motif. It showed 481 

strong positive low-frequency real precision in childhood and marked attenuation with age. This can 482 

be interpreted as a shift away from broad bilateral slow coordination between homologous frontopolar 483 

parcels. Frontopolar and lateral prefrontal cortices are late-maturing transmodal systems. During 484 

development, increasing segregation of control and default-mode systems may reduce diffuse slow 485 

interhemispheric coupling. In later life, these same long-range association systems are vulnerable 486 

to disruption of default and control networks [38, 39]. The p10p–p10p surface therefore supports a 487 

bounded interpretation. Low-frequency frontopolar conditional coupling is strong early in life, becomes 488 

less dominant with maturation and may be vulnerable to later association-system aging. 489

Posterior parietal and medial parieto-occipital pathways bridge the alpha and low-frequency motifs. 490 

PGs–POS2 showed earlier low-frequency peaks and later alpha or higher-frequency peaks, followed by 491 

broad decline in older age. This timing suggests that posterior association systems may first rely on 492 

slow coordination and later express more frequency-specific feedback or integrative coupling. PGs and 493 

POS2 sit near visual, attentional and default-mode interfaces, where lifespan connectivity studies have 494 

reported distinct trajectories for precuneus and posterior cingulate systems [40, 41]. The important 495 

point is not that this single pathway proves a general rule. Rather, it gives an interpretable example of 496 

how low-frequency and alpha-range components can peak at different ages within the same posterior 497 

association system. 498 

The delta-band precision hierarchy places these low-frequency motifs at the system level. We 499 

compared it with the sensorimotor-association axis because that axis is a compact reference for cor-500 

tical hierarchy. It separates primary sensory and motor systems from transmodal frontoparietal and 501 

default-mode systems [28,42]. The moderate alignment indicates that low-frequency conditional source 502 

relationships are partly ordered along this hierarchy. The positive pole in frontopolar and lateral pre-503 

frontal parcels suggests a transmodal association component. The negative pole in posterior, parietal, 504 

cingulate and sensorimotor-adjacent parcels suggests a distinct low-frequency profile in posterior and 505 

body-related systems. The alignment is therefore meaningful, but incomplete alignment is expected 506 

because JSPACE estimates conditional spectral relationships rather than BOLD functional similarity. 507 

The oldest-old deviation should be interpreted as a candidate late-life reorganization. After 80 508 

years, the frontal positive pole became compressed, the strongest positive scores shifted toward occip-509 

ital and visuoparietal parcels and the negative pole remained prominent over parietal-cingulate and 510 

sensorimotor-adjacent cortex. A plausible mechanism is reduced timing specificity in long-range asso-511 

ciation systems. Myelin and white-matter integrity influence conduction speed and the reliability of 512 

inter-areal timing. When these properties decline, long-range frontal loops may become less able to 513 

maintain a distinct slow conditional profile. Posterior and sensorimotor-adjacent systems may then 514 

contribute more strongly to the leading low-frequency axis. Lifespan diffusion and myelin studies sup-515 

port this mechanism by showing prolonged white-matter maturation followed by age-related decline, 516 

but the sparse oldest-old sample means that the exact transition age remains uncertain [43, 44]. 517 

The atlas also extends the normative logic of qEEGt and HarMNqEEG from scalp spectral sum-518 

maries to conditional source relationships [18, 19]. In a clinical setting, a precision-based chart would 519 

ask whether an individual’s age- and site-adjusted source interactions deviate from the expected 520 

conditional-dependence structure, not only whether local spectral amplitude is abnormal. This could 521 

be useful in future studies where altered coordination, network segregation or indirect coupling is more 522 

informative than regional power alone. The present study does not test diagnostic classification, but 523 

it provides a precision-domain reference for evaluating such applications in clinical cohorts. 524 

Several boundaries constrain these interpretations. The atlas was estimated from 19-channel EEG, 525 

so source precision depends on the forward model, parcellation, residual sensor model and regulariza-526 

tion. The cohort is cross-sectional, so age effects cannot be equated with within-person developmental 527 

trajectories. The representative pathways were selected as interpretable anchors, not as an exhaustive 528 

edgewise significance set. The sign of real precision should also be interpreted as a model-derived con-529 

ditional relation, not as direct excitation or inhibition. These constraints define the appropriate use of 530 

the atlas. It is a harmonized source-precision reference that should be tested further with high-density 531 

EEG or MEG, longitudinal elderly sampling and multimodal structural or myelin-sensitive imaging. 532 

Taken together, the findings support source precision as a useful coordinate for lifespan EEG 533 

connectomics. JSPACE links source inverse modeling with conditional spectral graphs. The resulting 534 

atlas suggests a layered organization of lifespan EEG, with conserved alpha-related diagonal structure, 535 

frequency-specific off-diagonal motifs and a delta-band hierarchy that partly follows the sensorimotor-536 

association organization of cortex. This precision view complements power, covariance and coherence 537 

analyses by asking how age and frequency shape conditional relationships among cortical generators 538 

after forward mixing and indirect dependence have been modeled. 539
