# Abstract 1

During well-trained behaviors, neural population activity in motor cortex lies on a 2

low-dimensional manifold. This raises the question of how such structure constrains 3

subsequent learning. In brain–computer interface experiments in nonhuman primates, 4

perturbations aligned with this subspace induced rapid adaptation, whereas misaligned 5

perturbations induced slower adaptation. Several theoretical accounts have been pro-6

posed to explain this differential adaptation, differing in the locus of plasticity. We 7

compare these hypotheses using a minimal linear recurrent network operating at its 8

fixed point and trained by gradient descent. All candidate plasticity sites are able to 9

produce some degree of differential adaptation, whose strength depends on the variance 10 

of recurrent weights, with different sensitivities across sites. Hessian analysis reveals 11 

how misaligned perturbations reshape the loss landscape by introducing directions of 12 

shallow curvature along which gradient descent proceeds slowly. We further propose an 13 

experimental test to help distinguish the contributions of different plasticity sites during 14 

adaptation. Overall, our results identify the variance of recurrent weights as a key con-15 

trol parameter governing differential adaptation, alongside the site of plasticity. 16

Recent theoretical accounts have sought to address this question using recurrent neural 34 

network (RNN) models. A key distinction among these models concerns the locus of 35 

plasticity: whether adaptation is attributed to recurrent connections (Feulner & Clopath, 36 

2021; W¨ arnberg & Kumar, 2019), to feedback or input weights (Gurnani, Liu, & Brun-37

evaluate these candidate plasticity sites within a unified and minimal framework, and 43 

identify the conditions under which each mechanism gives rise to differential adapta-44 

tion. To isolate the contribution of each mechanism, we study each candidate plasticity 45 

site individually, and in the Discussion consider the circumstances under which they 46 

could coexist. We further show how perturbations shape the geometry of the loss land-47 

scape, influencing learning speed. 48 

To formalize adaptation to manifold-based perturbations, we develop a RNN model 49 

that captures the low-dimensional structure of neural activity and its mapping to behav-50 

ior. To enable analytical insight, we consider linear networks and analyze their fixed-51 

point dynamics. Within this framework, gradient descent serves as a normative proxy 52 

(Richards & Kording, 2023) for plasticity targeted at specific circuit components. We 53 

find that reaiming produces differential adaptation across parameter regimes, whereas 54 

input- and recurrent-weight plasticity do so only when the variance of recurrent weights 55 

is sufficiently large, a qualitative distinction that persists in nonlinear networks. In this 56 

parameter regime, plasticity can thus be more widely distributed, while still being com-57 

patible with differential adaptation. Our framework predicts that evaluating reassocia-58 

tion (M. D. Golub et al., 2018) beyond the latent variables controlling the cursor could 59 

help distinguish the contribution of reaiming from other plasticity sites during WM 60 

adaptation. 61

# 2 Methods 62 

Our model captures the key features of the BCI experiment presented in (Sadtler et al., 63 

2014), with targeted simplifications that streamline the analysis. In this experiment, 64 

neural activity was recorded from the primary motor cortex of non-human primates 65 

using invasive neural implants. This activity was decoded to control a cursor from a 66 

center position on a monitor to one of K = 8 peripheral targets evenly distributed on a 67 

circle, a configuration corresponding to the common center-out paradigm used in BCI 68 

research. A central feature of the Sadtler et al. experiment is that the cursor movement 69 

is driven by low-dimensional latent factors—defining the manifold—rather than by the 70 

full neural activity. 71 

We first introduce the network model, followed by the BCI decoder mapping neural 72 

activity to cursor position via the manifold. We then define the WM and OM perturba-73 

tions and the gradient-based learning rules governing adaptation. Finally, we describe 74 

additional analyses used to characterize learning dynamics and the resulting changes in 75 

neural activity. 76

# 3 Results 249 

We first assess the relative difficulty of WM and OM perturbations in the model and 250 

compare them with experimental results. Next, we isolate each learning parameter’s 251 

contribution to adaptation by allowing plasticity in only one parameter at a time 2, and 252 

then test whether these observations extend to nonlinear activation functions. Finally, 253 

we analyze the neural strategies underlying WM adaptation across the different plastic-254 

ity mechanisms. 255 

3.1 Relative difficulty of perturbations 256 

In (Sadtler et al., 2014), several controls ensured that OM perturbations were not arti-257 

ficially more difficult to learn due to trivial differences in scaling or effective search-258 

space dimensionality. We adopted analogous precautions. Neural activity and latent 259 

factors were z-scored, and we exhaustively searched for OM perturbations whose pre-260 

adaptation losses lay within a specified tolerance of that of the WM perturbation, which 261 

we call valid OM perturbations (Figure 1A). Consequently, the initial performance im-262 

pairments were comparable across perturbation types. 263 

Sadtler et al. (2014) also performed post hoc controls to assess the expected relative 264 

difficulty of adaptation. One such measure is the mean principal angle between the intu-265 

itive and perturbed mappings, which quantifies the deviation between their row spaces. 266

The static linear network allows a systematic characterization of adaptation to manifold-418

We test whether these observations extend to nonlinear dynamics by replacing the lin-423

models (Men´ endez et al., 2024; Gurnani et al., 2024). Equation 1 is integrated using 425 

the Euler method with initial condition ν0 = 0. When stable, the dynamics converge to 426 

an input-dependent fixed point at which the objective (Eq. 20) is evaluated and gradient 427 

descent is applied; we use T = 40 time steps. The nonlinear setting introduces addi-428 

tional constraints. Learning is less stable, requiring a higher target WM loss to ensure a 429 

successful learning-rate search. Moreover, valid OM perturbations are not obtained for 430 

all initializations; we therefore use 20 seeds, yielding 11 ± 2 (mean ± sd) successful 431 

initializations across learning conditions. Finally, because the ReLU nonlinearity can 432 

effectively silence units, we set σW

√Ntot = 1 .1 in the high-variance condition. 433 

As in the linear case, reaiming remains largely insensitive to σW , while the distribu-434 

tions of log ratios of OM and WM final losses for input-weight and recurrent-weight 435

valid OM perturbations whose final loss exceeds 10 2 times the WM final loss—using 437 

the same threshold as in Figure 5A—drops to near zero for input-weight learning and 438 

recurrent-weight learning (Figure 5B). Although this threshold is somewhat arbitrary, 439 

these results indicate that differential adaptation at these plasticity sites is reduced in 440 

the ReLU case compared to the linear case. Overall, the main insights from the linear 441 

model are preserved in this nonlinear case, albeit with attenuated quantitative separa-442

# 4 Discussion 465 

Our study compares different hypotheses about how pre-existing neural structures may 466 

constrain subsequent learning, using the site of plasticity as the key distinguishing fac-467 

tor. We find that differential adaptation can emerge across all considered plasticity sites, 468 

although the conditions under which it occurs depend on the site. Reaiming robustly 469 

produces differential adaptation across parameter regimes. In contrast, input-weight 470 

and recurrent-weight learning require a large recurrent-weight variance σW , placing the 471 

linear network near instability. These findings largely persist qualitatively in networks 472 

with nonlinear activation functions. Mechanistically, difficult OM perturbations induce 473 

shallow directions—associated with small Hessian eigenvalues—in the loss landscape, 474 

along which gradient descent proceeds slowly. Differential adaptation under reaiming 475 

is less sensitive to σW because the Hessian for WM adaptation is isotropic with large 476 

eigenvalues irrespective of the network’s operating point. 477 

After a perturbation, the network must generate activity that is both output-potent (Kaufman, 478 

Churchland, Ryu, & Shenoy, 2014) and compatible with the task objective. Previous 479 

theories have assumed that the local recurrent connectivity is not plastic on the time 480 

scale of the experiment (Sadtler et al., 2014; Zhou, Tien, Ravikumar, & Chase, 2019), 481 

and approached this problem by viewing the recurrent network together with the per-482 

turbed decoder as a controlled dynamical system (Men´ endez et al., 2024; Gurnani et 483 

al., 2024). Adaptation becomes an open-loop (Men´ endez et al., 2024) and/or feedback 484 

(Gurnani et al., 2024) control problem. In these frameworks, learning is constrained 485 

by the assumed low dimensionality of the control inputs. Such constraints limit the 486 

controllability of network states, particularly when the input magnitude is penalized 487 

(Men´ endez et al., 2024) or when perturbations require substantial reorganization of pre-488 

existing dynamical flows (Gurnani et al., 2024; Oby et al., 2025). Our results comple-489 

ment these perspectives by showing how perturbations directly reshape the optimization 490 

landscape associated with reaiming and input-weight learning. Activity regularization 491 

plays a role similar to input-norm constraints by competing with the task objective 492

In these control-theoretic accounts, low-dimensional inputs play a central role in con-497 

straining both adaptation and the intrinsic dimensionality of neural activity. However, 498 

the origin of this low-dimensional structure is typically left unspecified and merely re-499 

flects the task structure. One may instead embed the latent structure directly into the 500 

network architecture (W¨ arnberg & Kumar, 2019), but then learning mechanisms com-501 

patible with this structure may be lacking (Feulner & Clopath, 2021). Another pos-502 

sibility is that adaptation relies less on low-dimensional external inputs and more on 503 

recurrent mechanisms, since small correlated changes to local connectivity may suffice 504 

for adaptation (Feulner et al., 2022), especially at high σW . However, our analysis sug-505 

gests that recurrent-weight learning early in adaptation is associated with a complex, 506 

non-convex loss landscape and slow adaptation rates at high σW . Gurnani et al. also ar-507 

ticulate cogent arguments against it. A solution explored in (Feulner & Clopath, 2021) 508 

was to consider imperfect credit assignment during recurrent-weight learning, but it 509 

is unclear how adaptation rates are impacted, especially early during adaptation. Be-510 

yond BCI research, fast adaptation to visuomotor perturbations in arm-reaching tasks is 511 

associated with minimal changes in motor-cortical functional connectivity, potentially 512 

arguing against recurrent-weight learning as a mechanism for rapid learning (Perich, 513 

Gallego, & Miller, 2018). 514 

All theoretical accounts of Sadtler et al.’s experiments—including the present work— 515 

have relied on modeling choices that can differ from the experimental setting. A few 516 

of these choices might deserve reexamination in future work, specifically concerning 517 

manifold-based perturbations, but also center-out BCI experiments more generally. A 518 

key discrepancy is that, in actual center-out BCI tasks, animals must typically reach 519 

the target before a set time interval expires to get rewarded. This challenges the use of 520 

a mean-square loss at a precise end time as the primary objective and, more generally, 521 

the use of supervised-learning methods instead of reinforcement-learning ones in motor 522 

control (Codol, Krishna, Lajoie, & Perich, 2024). Related to this point, viewing adapta-523 

tion simply as an optimization process might disregard important confounding factors 524 

(Hennig et al., 2021). One such factor that has not been widely discussed is that, in per-525 

turbative experiments, the animals have had extensive training with the task prior to the 526 

perturbation (M. D. Golub et al., 2018) (which we partially accounted for by using the 527 

most modulated readout units for control). Highly-trained subjects have exhaustively 528 

sampled neural and task variability structures. Structure learning (or meta-learning) 529 

could thus speed up adaptation when perturbations share sufficient statistical structure 530 

with prior exposure (Braun, Aertsen, Wolpert, & Mehring, 2009; Braun, Mehring, & 531 

Wolpert, 2010; Chang, Perich, Miller, Gallego, & Clopath, 2024), without requiring 532 

substantial plasticity (Weinstein & Botvinick, 2017). However, it is unclear how such 533 

meta-learning processes would interact with the perturbation-induced changes to the 534 

optimization landscape identified here. 535
