## 摘要
理解神经动力学与潜在脑结构之间的关系，特别是后者对传导延迟的影响，仍然是神经科学的核心问题。在人类中，我们主要通过宏观尺度的神经生理传播测量来接触这些现象。在此尺度上，信号传播反映在由白质轴突传导和灰质突触及整合过程引起的可测量延迟中。

虽然特定的白质特征已明确与单轴突传导延迟相关联，但尚无清晰模型将灰质微观结构特性与大规模传播联系起来。为此，我们结合了先进的MRI微观结构建模与静息态MEG，在94名健康对照样本中将脑结构与信号传播联系起来。白质指标包括轴突直径、髓鞘化（g-ratio、髓鞘水分数）和纤维束长度，而灰质则使用SANDI模型和皮层厚度进行表征。传播延迟通过神经元雪崩框架进行量化。

我们的研究结果表明，所有白质和灰质指标与传播延迟之间存在显著关联。当尝试使用我们的白质/灰质指标时，我们能够通过线性回归模型预测高达约26%的观察延迟。我们还发现了频率特异性传播动力学与纤维束长度之间的关联。最后，通过典型相关分析，我们展示了白质/灰质指标与MEG频率特异性传播延迟之间的多变量耦合。我们的发现为组织结构与大规模神经动力学之间的联系提供了证据，支持了基于生物学的信号传播模型的发展。

## Abstract
Understanding the relationship between neural dynamics and underlying brain structure, and in particular the impact of the latter on conduction delays, remains a core question in neuroscience. In humans, we primarily access these phenomena at the macroscale through neurophysiological measures of propagation. At this scale, signal propagation is reflected in measurable delays arising from both white matter (WM) axonal conduction and grey matter (GM) synaptic and integrative processes.

While specific WM features have been explicitly linked to single-axon conduction delays, no clear models link GM microstructural properties to large-scale propagation. To this aim, we combined advanced MRI microstructural modelling with resting-state MEG to link brain structure to signal propagation in a sample of 94 healthy controls. WM metrics included axonal diameter, myelination (g-ratio, myelin water fraction), and tract length, while GM was characterised using the SANDI model and cortical thickness. Propagation delays were quantified using the neuronal avalanches framework.

Our findings indicated significant associations between all of WM and GM metrics and the propagation delays. When attempting to use our W/GM metrics we were able to predict up to [~]26% of the observed delay using linear regression modelling. We also found associations between frequency-specific propagation dynamics and tract length. Finally, using a canonical correlation analysis we demonstrated multivariate coupling between our W/GM metrics and the MEG frequency-specific propagation delays. Our findings provide evidence for the link between tissue structure and large-scale neural dynamics, supporting the development of biologically grounded models of signal propagation.
