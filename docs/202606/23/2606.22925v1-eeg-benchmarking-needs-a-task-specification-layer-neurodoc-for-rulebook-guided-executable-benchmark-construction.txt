Abstract 

Electroencephalography (EEG) foundation models increasingly rely on multi-dataset training and evaluation, yet public EEG datasets still lack a shared task specification layer that can turn heterogeneous recordings into reusable benchmark units. Existing standards organize files, metadata, and provenance, but they do not specify EEG tasks under a common language and rulebook, leaving critical task semantics scattered across papers, code, and manual interpretation. We investigate whether heterogeneous public EEG datasets can be standardized through a struc-tured task specification language paired with a shared rulebook. Our methodology represents each benchmark entry as a task document synchronized with an exe-cutable task kernel, with the rulebook defining task fields, evidence requirements, document-kernel alignment, review states, and machine-checkable constraints. Us-ing this methodology, we release a community-reviewed EEG benchmark corpus centered on 53 completed and reviewed entries with 245 task definitions spanning diverse paradigms, and we introduce NeuroDoc and NeuroAudit as the operational support layer for rulebook-guided drafting, upgrading, review, amendment, and release management. We further examine whether the resulting benchmark units can be instantiated in a shared downstream setting across four EEG foundation model backbones, providing execution-based evidence for reusable, auditable, and executable EEG benchmarking infrastructure. 

1 Introduction 

Electroencephalography (EEG) provides a non-invasive way to monitor brain activity and has been widely used to study cognitive processes, behavioral states, and neurological conditions. Because EEG data are collected under diverse experimental paradigms for different scientific and clinical purposes, public EEG datasets have emerged with substantial variation in data acquisition, annotation, and organization conventions [ 12 , 23 , 5, 19 ]. The heterogeneity has become increasingly consequential as modern EEG foundation models are trained on aggregated multi-dataset corpora and evaluated for transfer across datasets, subjects, recording setups, and downstream tasks.

Early self-supervised representation learning systems such as BENDR [ 13 ] showed that useful EEG representations could be learned from large corpora spanning beyond a single downstream benchmark. More recent models (such as LaBraM [ 12 ], CBraMod [ 22 ] and REVE [ 5]) push this trend further toward large-scale aggregated pre-training. Several of these works rely on broad dataset aggregation: LaBraM reports pre-training data drawn from around 20 datasets [ 12 ], and REVE reports pre-training across 92 datasets [ 5 ]. As large-scale dataset aggregation becomes central to EEG foundation model design, cross-dataset reuse is becoming an increasingly important practical requirement. At the same time, emerging evidence [ 24 ] suggests that performance gains from EEG foundation models depend strongly on dataset choice, evaluation protocol, and downstream task definition. Model architecture therefore remains crucial, but it is not the only factor limiting progress. In practice, researchers who want to train, adapt, or evaluate models across many public EEG datasets still face repeated and labor-intensive task-definition work. This exposes a deeper question for the field: can EEG tasks be specified under a shared language and rulebook strongly enough to support reusable benchmarking across heterogeneous datasets? Existing infrastructure addresses important adjacent parts of the problem, while task specification itself remains underspecified. BIDS [7] and EEG-BIDS [17] standardize directory organization and modality-specific metadata. Generic documentation frameworks such as Croissant [ 2] improve trans-parency, provenance tracking, and machine-readable dataset description. What remains missing is a shared specification layer for EEG tasks: a consistent way to state epoch derivation, label definition, evidence source, and executable realization for each dataset. In practice, this information is still distributed across dataset papers, supplementary notes, raw directory inspection, and code fragments, so cross-dataset EEG research continues to depend on substantial undocumented interpretation even when the raw data are publicly available. That dependence on manual interpretation is not only a technical inconvenience. In EEG, raw recordings generally cannot be freely repackaged and redistributed because secondary sharing is constrained by research ethics, participant consent, privacy regulations such as GDPR and HIPAA, and repository-specific licensing or copyright conditions associated with dataset release [4, 1]. This makes task specification a first-class scientific bottleneck: if the benchmark unit cannot be defined clearly at the task level, large-scale reuse remains fragile even when raw data are accessible. Figure 1 illustrates the resulting design goal of this project. Recent large language model (LLM)-based documentation systems can generate cards, metadata, or benchmark descriptions, but they are typically designed for generic documentation and do not incor-porate EEG-specific semantic constraints or domain review [ 14 , 10 , 21 , 9]. In EEG, task definitions often depend on paradigm-specific event interpretation, label construction, epoch configuration, and derived supervision routes. A useful solution therefore requires a structured task language, explicit domain rules, executable task logic, and evidence tracing. In this paper, we study whether a task specification language and rulebook can make heterogeneous public EEG datasets explicit, executable, and auditable enough for reusable benchmarking. Therefore, we introduce an entry -based representation in which each benchmark entry consists of a human-readable task document and a dataset-specific task kernel . 2 Related Work 

Prior work comes from three aspects: 1) EEG standards and software infrastructure for dataset organization and processing; 2) generic frameworks for dataset documentation; and 3) large language model systems for automated documentation and scientific workflows. Table 1 summarizes the design gap addressed in this paper. 

2.1 EEG Data Standards, Toolkits, and Documentation Frameworks 

Prior work has made EEG datasets easier to store, describe, benchmark, and process. BIDS [ 7]and EEG-BIDS [ 17 ] provide conventions for dataset organization and modality-specific metadata, while MNE-BIDS [ 3] connects those conventions to practical analysis workflows. HED contributes a structured language for encoding event semantics [ 20 ], and COBIDAS-MEEG provides reporting guidance for reproducible EEG and MEG studies [ 18 ]. At the toolkit level, MOABB offers a reproducible benchmarking framework for EEG-based BCIs by standardizing datasets, paradigms, pipelines, and evaluation schemes across public benchmarks [ 11 ], while EEGUnity provides unified infrastructure for loading and processing heterogeneous EEG datasets [ 19 ]. In parallel, generic dataset documentation frameworks address transparency from a different angle: Croissant [ 2] defines machine-readable metadata for machine learning-ready datasets. Overall, these efforts standardize structure, metadata, reporting, event annotation, and high-level processing interfaces. 32.2 LLM-based Documentation and Scientific Agent Systems 

Another literature explores whether documentation and scientific workflows can be partially auto-mated with large language models and tool-augmented agents. In the EEG domain, EEGAgent focuses on automated EEG analysis, with emphasis on signal-level workflows [ 25 ]. In the broader documentation space, DCA-Bench evaluates dataset curation agents [ 10 ], Pre-Meta applies retrieval-augmented generation to metadata production [ 21 ], and Auto-BenchmarkCard targets automated benchmark documentation with factual validation [ 9]. These systems show that documentation-oriented generation can be grounded, validated, and partially automated. They do not provide a shared EEG task specification language, a rulebook that governs admissible task claims, or a community review process tied to executable benchmark entries. 

3 Methodology 

3.1 Overview 

This section presents our rulebook-guided benchmark-construction methodology. We define the task specification language and entry-based representation, introduce the shared rulebook for review and machine-checkable validation, describe tools for entry generation, upgrade, and community review and provide the extensibility policy enabling corpus and rulebook evolution. 

3.2 Task Specification Language and Entry-based Representation 

Our methodology centers on a structured EEG task specification language implemented through benchmark entries. Each entry contains: a dataset Task Document (hereafter, Document ) and a dataset-specific task kernel (hereafter, Kernel ). The Document provides the human-readable layer of the specification language, and the Kernel provides the executable dataset-specific logic in a unified pipeline. • First, the Document includes Paradigm , File Scan Result , Tasks , Data Quality Notes , and Kernel Responsibility . File Scan Result stores program-derived facts such as scan metadata, event structure summaries, extracted information tables, and kernel fingerprints. Tasks stores the task specification itself, including task type, epoch method, epoch parameters, label route, target definition, and supporting evidence. Based on evidence sufficiency, we distinguish classical tasks from potential tasks. Together, these fields form the human-readable core of the task specification language. • Second, the Kernel is the executable code for the entry, injecting dataset-dependent logic into a unified processing pipeline so that the tasks described in the Document can be instantiated consistently. The entry-based representation is designed to keep these two synchronized elements aligned through generation, review, and later updates, thereby turning a dataset-specific task specification into a reusable benchmark unit. To define EEG tasks consistently across heterogeneous datasets, we decompose each task specification into four coupled layers: 1) task type, 2) epoch method, 3) label route, and 4) meta level. Epoch anchoring and label routing are independent, so a task may use event-locked, long-event-locked, or continuous windows while drawing supervision from misc or info fields. We discuss the four layers below: • Task Type. In the current specification, tasks are formulated as either classification or regression. • Epoch Method. We distinguish three cases: event-locked window, long-event-locked window, and continuous window. Event-locked windows use a discrete event as the anchor. Long-event-locked windows segment repeatedly with a specified overlap ratio until the boundary of a long event. Continuous windows segment repeatedly until the boundary of the recording session. • Label Route. We use three routes: event, misc, and info, which are also compatible with MNE [ 8]. The event route uses discrete event semantics represented through annotations or stimulation-channel events; the misc route uses continuous targets stored in MISC channels; and the info route uses file-level attributes. 4• Meta Level. We distinguish raw meta and super meta. Raw meta refers to labels or fields directly available from raw metadata, such as original annotations and event tables. Super meta requires further derivation, such as binary contrasts collapsed from multiple event classes or response labels derived from event pairing. Representative released Document cases (8 out of 53) are provided in Appendix F. Public EEG datasets involve diverse paradigms as well as cognitive, clinical, sleep, and other applied settings, no single standard paradigm taxonomy cleanly covers the full range of tasks represented in the corpus [ 11 , 18 , 19 ]. We therefore use a six-category taxonomy for corpus-level organization purposes: Complex Applied, High-level Mental, Action/Output, Sensory/Response, Biological State, and Others. 

3.3 Rulebook for Entries 

To make benchmark construction auditable and repeatable, our methodology centers on a single entry rulebook. This rulebook is the governance foundation of the task specification language and of the corpus built from it: it defines acceptable evidence, admissible task claims, document-kernel synchronization, and valid status transitions across the entry lifecycle. The full rulebook is reproduced in Appendix D. Within the rulebook, some provisions are primarily normative and reviewer-facing, whereas others are structured enough to be checked automatically. The normative side defines evidence priority, data-over-description preference, conservative downgrade behavior, implementation-grounded task acceptance, and the responsibilities of human reviewers, automated assistants, and maintainers. This machine-checkable side protects program-derived content such as scan-derived identifiers, counts, information-table values, and kernel fingerprints from later model-side rewriting, restricts which status values may be assigned automatically, and checks section structure, legal field values, task-format compatibility, scanned-event consistency, and kernel fingerprints. We operationalize it through a detector-side rule encoding summarized in Appendix E. 

3.4 NeuroDoc 3.4.1 NeuroDoc Overview 

NeuroDoc is a rulebook-guided benchmark-construction assistant that helps create and update drafts of the Document and the Kernel under the rulebook, with human review reserved for final acceptance. In the current implementation, NeuroDoc functions as an LLM-assisted, rulebook-constrained entry-construction workflow organized around two executable paths: a generation pipeline for initial entry drafting and an upgrading pipeline for revision based on human feedback. Functionally, NeuroDoc combines four capabilities: 1) context building from dataset-local and literature-side evidence, 2) constrained task synthesis under the task language and rulebook constraints, 3) kernel-oriented coding, and 4) validation against the machine-checkable subset of the rulebook. Project-scale statistics for the downloaded reference resources and the broader construction workflow are summarized in Appendix A.2 and Appendix A.1, while a small-scale quantitative characterization of NeuroDoc draft quality is summarized in Appendix B.

3.5 NeuroAudit-assisted Community Review and Amendment Workflow 

NeuroAudit is the reviewer-facing audit web user interface, supporting community review, document-level amendment, and status switch under the shared entry rulebook. Through this interface, commu-nity reviewers can audit generated entries, record amendments, and provide the feedback that drives later upgrades. The description of NeuroAudit is provided in Appendix C. Figure 3 describes the second stage of the methodology. Once an initial entry-based representation is produced, the Document can be reviewed by the community via NeuroAudit. The reviewer-facing interface allows users to make step-by-step, rule-bound modifications within a predefined framework derived from the shared rulebook. This design keeps review focused on correction, adjudication, evidence checking, and document-kernel synchronization, while avoiding unrestricted rewriting of program-derived content. The NeuroAudit workflow may lead to three document outcomes shown in Figure 3: 

wait_kernel_update , hallucination , and completed . The completed state marks entries that pass review under the current rulebook and can be released as valid benchmark entries, but this does not mean they cannot be changed in the future. Completed entries may still receive later corrections, or additional task definitions through the same review process. The hallucination 

state marks entries whose claims are not adequately supported and should not proceed as valid benchmark entries. The wait_kernel_update state captures a failure mode in which the entry remains conceptually useful, but the current Kernel requires further revision. Under the rulebook, entries in wait_kernel_update are routed to the NeuroDoc upgrade pipeline. As illustrated in Figure 3, Phase 1: build context analyzes the returned document and reviewer notes to identify the source of the conflict. Phase 2: kernel coding then revisits the coding loop and checks whether the revised Kernel still matches the synthesized tasks. 3.6 Rulebook Evolution and Extensibility 

The benchmark corpus is intended to grow over time, and the rulebook is therefore treated as extensible governance material. The community may propose extensions to controlled dimensions such as task type, epoch method, label route, target-format conventions, or other task-interface fields. Once accepted by the community, the rulebook can be updated and existing entries can be amended or expanded accordingly. When a new rule conflicts with a legacy rule, the corpus governance policy may remain temporarily accepted during a deprecation period while entries are progressively upgraded. This preserves backward compatibility for released benchmark entries and allows staged migration as the corpus evolves. 

4 Benchmark Corpus 

Having defined the methodology, we characterize the resulting benchmark corpus as an evaluation resource and demonstrate the type of benchmark infrastructure it produces. We first summarize the scale, coverage, and structural diversity of the completed corpus used in this study, and then use downstream experiments in the next subsection to examine how these released benchmark entries can be consumed under a shared task specification layer. 

4.1 Corpus Statistics 

Figure 4 summarizes the completed benchmark corpus used in this paper. Our quantitative analysis is restricted to 53 completed and reviewed benchmark entries that are treated as released benchmark units. These entries contain 245 task definitions, with a mean of 4.62 tasks per dataset document, a median of 4, and a maximum of 10. Most datasets can participate in several benchmark settings through one synchronized document-kernel pair. The completed corpus also exhibits broad topical coverage across heterogeneous EEG paradigms. At the task-definition level, the largest categories are Complex Applied (110 tasks), Action/Output (49), Biological State (38), and High-level Mental (32). The released corpus therefore spans motor, cognitive, sensory, sleep/clinical, and applied paradigms under a common representation layer. Among the 245 task definitions, 134 are currently marked as classical tasks and 111 as potential tasks. Classical tasks anchor the corpus to established literature-backed formulations, while potential tasks expand the reusable search space for later evaluation and adaptation studies. 4.2 Execution-based Validation with Four EEG Model Backbones

To do so, we use four representative EEG foundation model backbones— BENDR [ 13 ], CBraMod [ 22 ], CodeBrain [ 15 ], and LaBraM [ 12 ]—as downstream consumers under a shared task specification layer. Our aggregate analysis covers 266 runs spanning 18 datasets and 37 benchmark tasks, evaluated under two split modes (cross-subject and within-subject) across six task families: motor imagery (MI), P300, sleep staging, SSVEP/SSAEP, clinical EEG, and cognitive tasks. 5 Conclusion 

As EEG foundation models increasingly rely on large aggregated corpora, the bottleneck is no longer only dataset access, but task specification. This paper studies whether a shared task specification lan-guage and rulebook can make heterogeneous public EEG datasets explicit, executable, and auditable enough for reusable benchmarking. Our main methodological contribution is a rulebook-guided EEG task specification language implemented through benchmark entries that pair field-structured task documents with executable kernels. Our main resource contribution is a community-reviewed corpus centered on 53 completed and reviewed benchmark entries. Our main systems contribution is the NeuroDoc–NeuroAudit toolchain for drafting, review, amendment, and release management under shared governance. Together, these contributions elevate EEG task operationalization into a 9benchmark object that can be inspected, executed, and maintained across datasets without repackaging raw recordings. A current limitation is that the quantitative analysis in this paper is restricted to the 53 entries that have reached the completed-and-reviewed release state under the current rulebook. Beyond this released subset, the broader database already contains roughly 200 additional draft or unaudited documents, but these cannot yet be treated as equally reliable benchmark units until community review, amendment, and document-kernel consistency checks are completed. Future work includes extending the rulebook and corpus to broader EEG task families and datasets, and further characterizing LLM-assisted benchmark drafting and review support, including description precision, evidence grounding, kernel alignment, and the human effort required for review.

> Figure 6: Representative NeuroAudit task-review page. Figure 6 shows a representative task-review page. The interface is organized into three coordinated regions. The left column acts as the corpus navigation panel: reviewers can search datasets, filter by paradigm category, and inspect status values such as completed ,wait_manual_review , and
> wait_kernel_update .The center panel is the main structured review workspace. NeuroAudit exposes the benchmark entry through staged tabs, including meta-paradigm information, file-scan results, tasks, data-quality notes, and kernel-related content. In the task view illustrated here, reviewers can inspect and correct each task through explicit fields for task type, epoch method, label route, meta level, epoch parameters, and target definition. Classical and potential tasks are edited through the same structured template, which helps preserve Document consistency while still allowing reviewers to add evidence-backed refinements or propose additional task formulations. The same task workspace also supports assisted regeneration through the regenerate all tasks
> control. This action is designed for cases where the reviewer has already completed file scanning and event inspection, but wants to rebuild the task region more efficiently after collecting additional support references. When triggered, NeuroAudit looks up the configured local reference directory on the user’s disk, retrieves the relevant source materials, combines them with the scan-derived annotation and event information already stored in the current Document , and then calls an attached LLM interface to regenerate the entire Tasks section. In effect, the interface lets users convert newly collected references into updated task cards without restarting the full audit process from scratch. This regeneration step is especially useful for community maintenance. Reviewers often agree on the observed event structure of a dataset before they agree on the exact task decomposition, epoch design, or target construction supported by the literature. The regenerate all tasks pathway gives them a practical way to incorporate better references, refresh the task proposals, and then 16 continue manual inspection in the same structured editor. Because the regenerated tasks re-enter the same rulebook-constrained UI, the feature accelerates task-card construction while still keeping later review and amendment decisions explicit and comparable across datasets. The right column provides reviewer guidance and rulebook support. NeuroAudit presents checklist-style validation hints tied to the current review stage, including event-structure checks, information-field checks, evidence verification, and task-parameter inspection. This design helps reviewers distinguish between missing evidence, ambiguous semantics, and executable inconsistencies. In practice, it also improves reviewer consistency across datasets by turning the rulebook into a visible part of the interface. Figure 7 illustrates a more focused interaction in the task editor. NeuroAudit does not rely on reviewers to remember rulebook constraints from memory while editing task fields. Instead, it performs rulebook-guided validation over both field-level admissibility and cross-field logical consistency. When a reviewer introduces a contradiction in the task region, the interface surfaces localized error messages that explain which constraints have been violated. After the conflicting fields are corrected and the update passes validation, the task is rendered again in compact card form. This interaction pattern reduces reviewer memory load, makes document audit easier to complete, and helps different reviewers apply the same rules when forming community judgments.

D Entry Rulebook 

NeuroAudit Rulebook v1.0.0 

Part I. General Provisions Chapter 1. Purpose and Scope Article 1. Purpose 

This rulebook standardizes generation, review, revision, and re-review of EEG dataset analysis.md 

documents so that they can: 1. Provide human reviewers with consistent, explicit, actionable review standards. 2. Provide LLM review scripts with stable, programmable, traceable decision rules. 3. Provide structured implementation guidance for later kernels, EEGUnity exports, and HDF5 produc-tion. 

Article 2. Scope 

This rulebook applies to: 1. Creating new dataset documents. 2. Revising existing dataset documents. 3. Reviewing core areas such as Classical Task , Potential Task , Event Structure , and meta-data. 4. Writing later LLM review rules, prompt rules, and fallback rules. 

Article 3. Core Principles 

Document generation and review must follow these principles: 1. Truthfulness first. 2. Actual data has priority over web descriptions. 3. Conservatism has priority over guessing. 4. Traceability has priority over brevity. 5. Implementable task definitions have priority over theoretically imaginable ones. 

Chapter 2. Terms Article 4. Dataset Document 

A dataset document means the analysis.md file under a single dataset directory, or a same-structure successor version. 

Article 5. Raw Meta 

raw meta means labels, events, codes, annotations, fields, or markers that already exist in the raw data and can be read without additional derivation. Examples include: 1. trial_type , event codes, and event names in events.tsv .2. Raw codes in trigger or stim channels. 3. Discrete events in raw annotation files. 4. Directly available behavioral fields in raw tables. 

Article 6. Super Meta 

super meta means labels or targets that do not directly exist in the raw data and must be generated, mapped, folded, paired, aggregated, joined, or otherwise derived by code, rules, or human logic. Examples include: 1. Response events. 2. Binary labels folded from multiple raw classes. 3. Clinical groups derived from filenames, participant tables, or external tables. 4. Correct or incorrect, target or non-target, fast or slow, and similar labels derived by pairing multiple events. 

18 Article 7. Kernel 

kernel means the later data-processing code or data-loading logic used to parse raw data, construct labels, route labels, and generate EEGUnity-compatible output. 

Article 8. Label Route 

Historically, Label Route meant the final EEGUnity or MNE-compatible path where labels landed, not necessarily the raw storage form. Earlier values were: 1. Annotation : time-based annotations with onset , duration , and description .2. STIM : trigger or stim channel route for code values or trigger pulses. 3. MISC : misc channel route for numeric auxiliary labels, continuous quantities, or attached labels unsuitable as event names. Deprecated: Annotation , STIM , and MISC as route values are not allowed in new documents. Current legal values are event , misc , and info ; see Article 77. 

Article 9. Task 

A Task is a definition that can directly guide EEG epoching, label construction, and supervised-learning configuration. 

Article 10. Classical Task 

A Classical Task is a core task that is supported by external evidence and can be implemented from the currently public data. 

Article 11. Potential Task 

A Potential Task is supported by the data itself but lacks enough external evidence to confirm it as a core benchmark task, or is only a recommended, extended, or derived task. 

Article 12. Evidence 

Evidence means external material supporting a task, parameter choice, or document judgment. It includes: 1. The official dataset page. 2. Official documentation. 3. Papers that clearly use the dataset. 4. Other authoritative sources. 

Chapter 3. Evidence Priority and Conflict Handling Article 13. Evidence Priority 

When sources conflict, use this priority order: 1. Verifiable data files and accompanying annotation files. 2. Official dataset page, official README, or official documentation. 3. Official papers or papers that clearly use the dataset. 4. Third-party pages, blogs, or unofficial descriptions. 

Article 14. General Conflict Rule 

When sources conflict, use the higher-priority evidence and record the conflict and decision in Data Quality Notes .

Article 15. Data Priority 

When online material or paper descriptions conflict with actual data files, the actual data files prevail. The inconsistency must still be stated explicitly. 

Article 16. Conservative Handling 

If existing evidence is insufficient to confirm a field, task, or label: 1. Prefer downgrading it to Potential Task .2. Use placeholders such as Needs verification , Variable , N/A , or null when appropriate. 3. Do not fabricate missing information. 

Article 17. Implementability 

Even if a paper used the dataset, a task must not be treated as a valid Classical Task unless the public data contains labels, events, or a verifiable derivation path sufficient to implement it. 

19 Chapter 4. Roles Article 18. Administrator Responsibilities 

Administrators are responsible for: 1. Deciding dataset entry names. 2. Deciding whether to accept human revisions or rule updates in pull requests. 3. Deciding whether a kernel must be added or rewritten. 

Article 19. Human Reviewer Responsibilities 

Human reviewers are responsible for: 1. Reviewing whether a Classical Task is valid. 2. Reviewing whether fields match actual data and official material. 3. Deciding how to resolve conflicting information. 4. Filling Manual Review when appropriate. 

Article 20. LLM Review Script Responsibilities 

LLM review scripts are responsible for: 1. Pre-generating or pre-correcting documents under the rulebook. 2. Detecting likely conflicts, omissions, and invalid tasks. 3. Producing conservative, traceable suggestions without inventing raw labels. 

Part II. Specific Rules Chapter 5. Meta Area Article 21. General Field Rule 

Meta Area records identifying dataset metadata. Unless otherwise specified, fields should be concise, stable, and easy for programs to read. 

Article 22. Dataset 

1. Dataset is the dataset identifier. 2. It should follow <source_name_id> .3. Administrators decide this name. 4. Reviewers and LLMs must not proactively modify it. 

Article 23. Year 

1. Year records key years related to collection, processing, or release. 2. It does not need to distinguish collection year from release year. 3. Multiple key years may be joined with /, for example 2004/2021 .4. Continuous ranges may use -, for example 2008-2015/2017 .5. The field need not cover every historical year, but should be updated as well as trusted sources allow. 6. Do not ignore key years clearly present in the data because a web page title shows a different year. 

Article 24. URL 

1. URL should preferably point to an information page that gives access to downloads or documentation. 2. If both a direct file link and an information page exist, keep the information page. 3. If a link is dead and cannot be replaced with an authoritative information page, delete it or replace it with an accessible authoritative page. 4. The field does not need to be permanently valid, but must have practical review value at the time of review. 

Article 25. Category 

1. Category must be exactly one of: • Complex Applied 

• High-level Mental 

• Action/Output 

• Sensory/Response 

20 • Biological State 

• Others 

2. The field is normally proposed by a program or preprocessing LLM. 3. Human reviewers should correct it only when the classification is clearly wrong. 4. Do not create category values outside the six listed values. 

Article 26. Subjects 

1. Subjects is the integer number of valid subjects in the dataset. 2. If the official count differs from the actually usable count, prefer the actually usable count. 3. Record this conflict in Data Quality Notes .

Article 27. Files (Completed) 

1. Files (Completed) is the number of files parsed by EEGUnity as Completed .2. LLMs and human reviewers normally must not edit this field manually. 3. If the parser result is clearly abnormal, report that the dataset needs a rescan or kernel correction instead of manually changing the count. 

Article 28. Channels 

1. Channels is the number of channels used by files, including non-EEG channels. 2. If only a few channel counts exist and can be listed clearly, use forms such as 32/64 or 22-128 .3. If the situation is complex and should not be fully enumerated, use Variable .4. LLMs or reviewers may use the clearest representation, but must avoid ambiguity. 

Article 29. Sampling Rate 

1. Sampling Rate records the main sampling-rate information. 2. If the whole dataset has one sampling rate, write a single value. 3. If files, modalities, or phases differ, use multiple values or explanatory wording. 4. If differences are complex, use Variable and explain in Data Quality Notes .

Article 30. Manual Review 

1. Only human reviewers may fill Manual Review .2. The value may be a real name, alias, or reviewer-approved identifier. 3. Multiple reviewers should be separated by / in review order. 4. LLMs must not forge, add, or impersonate a Manual Review mark. 5. Manual Review must be inline at the end of the second meta line, after Sampling Rate , using |**Manual Review**: ... .6. A separate third-line Manual Review field is no longer allowed. 

Article 30.1. Fixed Structure 

1. The current template normally keeps only these top-level sections in this order: • ## Paradigm 

• ## File Scan Result 

• ## Tasks 

• ## Data Quality Notes 

• ## Kernel Responsibilities 

2. ## File Scan Result contains these third-level subsections in fixed order: • ### Event Structure (required) • ### MISC Label Summary (optional) • ### Info (required) 3. Do not rename fixed section headings except for explicitly optional sections listed in this rulebook. 4. STIM Label Summary is deprecated and must not be added to new documents. Discrete events from trigger or stim channels must be registered in ### Event Structure .

Article 30.2. No Extra Sections 

1. Unless maintainers explicitly request it, do not add non-template top-level sections such as ## Meta ,standalone ## References , or custom extension sections. 2. If extra explanation is necessary, prefer task Notes , Data Quality Notes , ### Kernel Notes 

under ## Kernel Responsibilities , or review records. 3. When substantially revising historical documents, remove extra sections that violate the current structure unless maintainers explicitly request otherwise. 

Article 30.3. Automated Behavior for Manual Review 

21 1. LLM stages in generation pipelines must not fill Manual Review , including placeholders such as 

Pending , TBD , or N/A .2. LLMs should output | **Manual Review**: at the end of the second meta line with nothing after the colon. If template reuse accidentally inserts a non-empty value, post-processing must clear it. 3. This field may be non-empty only when a human reviewer manually edits analysis.md ; use Article 30 for formatting. 

Chapter 6. Paradigm Area Article 31. General Paradigm Rule 

Paradigm Area should describe the dataset‘s experimental paradigm, task form, subject behavior, and recording method, and should match the actual data. 

Article 32. Consistency Requirements 

Paradigm descriptions must align with: 1. Data directories and file structure. 2. Event files. 3. Official pages and official documentation. 4. Authoritative papers that clearly use the dataset. 

Article 33. Data Priority 

When online material conflicts with data material, the data prevails. 

Article 34. No Over-Inference 

Paradigm descriptions must not turn a paper‘s experimental goal, theoretical hypothesis, or common anal-ysis style into a label task supposedly supported by the public data unless the data provides implementable events or labels. 

Article 35. Description Boundary 

Paradigm descriptions may state the original experimental intent, but must distinguish: 1. The goal of the experimental design. 2. The labels and tasks actually available in the public data. 

Chapter 7. Kernel Responsibilities Area Article 36. Purpose and Structure 

1. Kernel Responsibilities Area records the core semantic judgment for the dataset at the kernel level. 2. The section uses blockquote metadata fields. The current version keeps only Super Meta Required 

and Status , in that order. 3. Do not add other fields, custom blocks, or explanatory text in this section. 4. Historical fields such as Type 1/2/3 template text, Selected Mode , Why , Primary Label Routes ,and Web Reference Used for Decision are deprecated and should be removed during substantial revisions. 

Article 37. Super Meta Required 

1. Super Meta Required states whether the dataset has target labels that require extra derivation, pairing, folding, mapping, or cross-file joining. 2. Legal values are exactly: • Yes : at least one task in Tasks Area has Meta Level = super .• No : all tasks have Meta Level = raw .3. Do not use synonyms or case variants. 

Article 38. Deciding Super Meta Required 

1. If any task, including Classical Task or Potential Task , has Meta Level = super , Super Meta Required must be Yes .2. If all tasks have Meta Level = raw , Super Meta Required should be No .3. If this field conflicts with Tasks Area , Tasks Area prevails and this field must be corrected. 4. LLM review scripts may infer and fill this field from Tasks Area .

Article 42.1. Status 

22 1. Status marks whether the current document text is sufficient to support later kernel refactoring, EEGUnity export, or event and label reconstruction. 2. Status is a dataset-level field, not a per-task field. 3. Although Status is metadata, it must currently be written in the Kernel Responsibilities Area 

blockquote, not in a new top-level section. 4. LLM review scripts, human reviewers, or maintainers may update Status according to the latest judgment. 

Article 42.2. Legal Status Values 

Status may use only these four machine-readable values: 1. wait_kernel_update 

2. wait_manual_review 

3. completed 

4. hallucination 

Do not replace them with synonyms, natural-language phrases, or case variants. 

Article 42.2.1. Authority Boundary for completed and hallucination 

1. Only human reviewers may assign completed or hallucination . LLM review scripts must not proactively assign either value. 2. At the current stage, LLM review scripts should choose only between wait_kernel_update and 

wait_manual_review , and should default conservatively to wait_manual_review .3. If the current text is already sufficient to support existing tasks and does not expose a clear structural kernel gap, LLM review scripts should output wait_manual_review and wait for final human confirmation. 4. After human review is complete, the final status must be completed or hallucination ; it must not remain wait_manual_review or wait_kernel_update .

Article 42.2.2. Boundary for hallucination 

1. hallucination is a severe document-level defect flag for cases where the entire analysis.md 

cannot be trusted. 2. Triggering conditions include, but are not limited to: • The dataset URL is invalid and no key field can be verified. • The document describes a dataset that does not match the actual dataset. • Task definitions, Event Structure , and Paradigm have no credible basis and cannot be repaired by reasonable supplementation. • Dataset information is so incomplete that even experts cannot make a reliable judgment. 3. hallucination does not apply to local issues such as one incorrect task citation or partially inaccurate channel description. Local issues should be recorded in Data Quality Notes , and status should remain completed after human review. 4. For datasets marked hallucination , administrators will consider deleting the document or arranging a rescan. Reviewers are not required to keep repairing it. 

Article 42.3. Deciding Status 

1. wait_kernel_update means the document clearly exposes a structural semantic kernel gap: Event Structure , MISC Label Summary , Label Route , companion-file ingestion, or super meta inte-gration is not yet sufficient to support existing tasks stably. This is an intermediate state for LLM scripts or automatic scanning. 2. wait_manual_review means no clear structural kernel gap is exposed, or remaining doubts mainly require human confirmation of task validity, task retention, or final release. This is the default LLM output status. 3. completed means a human reviewer confirms that document review is finished. Known quality issues may still exist if they are recorded in Data Quality Notes and the overall document is trustworthy and ready to proceed. completed means the reviewer has done due diligence, not that the document is flawless. 4. hallucination means a human reviewer judges the whole document to have unrecoverable severe defects and that it should be handled by administrators. 5. The following must not, by themselves, justify wait_kernel_update :• Routine preprocessing, filtering, rereferencing, or downsampling. • Artifact rejection, bad-segment removal, or bad-file exclusion. • General data quality control. • Implementation details such as training parameters, epoch length, or model hyperparameters. • Guessing from outside the document that the kernel might be unfinished or unverified. 

23 6. If the document semantically explains relevant events, labels, companion files, or metadata access paths and presents tasks as currently valid, do not mark wait_kernel_update only because of implementation caution. 7. When a dataset has multiple issue types, judgment priority is: hallucination above all other values; 

wait_manual_review above wait_kernel_update ; wait_kernel_update above completed .

Article 42.4. Blockquote Position and Order 

1. In the current version, blockquote metadata under Kernel Responsibilities Area must appear in this order: • Super Meta Required 

• Status 

2. Do not create separate top-level sections or custom blocks for these fields. 3. If a maintenance script batch-rewrites Status , it should modify only the fixed line for that field and should not rewrite unrelated body text. 

Chapter 8. File Scan Result Area 

## File Scan Result summarizes the kernel scan over raw data files, including kernel fingerprint, event structure, MISC labels, and subject information. It always contains ### Event Structure and 

### Info ; ### MISC Label Summary appears only when the dataset exposes misc-route labels or scanned native misc channels. The area begins with a kernel fingerprint blockquote. 

Article 42.5. Kernel Fingerprint 

1. The start of ## File Scan Result should record the kernel fingerprint used to generate the scan result as a blockquote. 2. The reference format is:    

> > **Kernel SHA256**: ‘<full_hash>‘ > **EEGUnity Version**: ‘X.Y.Z‘ > **Scan Type**: Full

3. Kernel SHA256 is the hash of the kernel file content and is calculated by maintenance scripts; it should not be filled manually. EEGUnity Version is the version used during scanning. 4. Scan Type is Full or Sampled (N/M files) .5. If the dataset has no dataset-specific kernel, or scanning can be completed through the default/generic parser path without a dedicated kernel file, Kernel SHA256 may be N/A .6. Neurodoc maintains this blockquote automatically. Human reviewers and LLMs must not manually edit the hash value. 7. The frontend display layer treats all consecutive > lines at the beginning of ## File Scan Result 

as Kernel Fingerprint and renders them as one blockquote without parsing field names. This keeps the frontend independent of field changes. 8. Do not insert HTML comments such as <!– AUTO-KERNEL-... –> inside ### Event Structure or other subsections. All kernel-derived information must be written directly in standard Markdown. 

Article 43. Table Form 

### Event Structure must be presented as a table. The table may be empty, but emptiness has rule consequences. 

Article 44. Required Columns 

The table must contain at least: 1. Event ID 

2. Count (total) 

3. Type 

4. Meta Level 

5. Meaning 

Article 45. Consequences of an Empty Table 

When Event Structure is empty or explicitly states that no valid event labels exist: 1. Do not keep epoch_by_event_hdf5 tasks. 2. Do not keep epoch_by_long_event_hdf5 tasks. 3. Normally only epoch_by_segmentation_hdf5 may be considered. 

24 Article 46. Legal Type Values 

Type may use only: 1. event : discrete events or time spans that can be used for tasks, including stimuli, responses, state transitions, sleep stages, and other domain events. 2. admin : system, boundary, or excluded events, such as device markers, file boundaries, or motion-artifact markers. The historical short , long , and rest subdivisions are deprecated. Event duration behavior is determined by Epoch Method in Tasks Area , not repeated in Event Structure .

Article 47. admin Events 

1. admin events should remain explicitly listed in Event Structure ; do not delete them by default. 2. admin events must not normally be used as target_events for supervised-learning tasks. 3. Any exception requires strong evidence and a reason in task Notes .

Article 48. Deciding Meta Level 

1. Events that can be read directly from raw data are raw .2. Events that require pairing, folding, derivation, mapping, joining, or reconstruction are super .

Article 49. First-Pass Documentation 

On first document creation, Event Structure should normally record only raw meta . If super meta 

is not yet supported by a stable kernel, do not pretend it is a raw event. 

Article 50. Handling Mismatches with Source Material 

When Event Structure seriously disagrees with dataset documentation: 1. If task validity is not affected, warn in Data Quality Notes .2. If task validity is affected, correct event interpretation, downgrade tasks, delete tasks, or report that a rescan is needed. 3. Do not keep incorrect tasks without explaining the conflict. 

Article 51. Parser Differences 

When mismatch is likely caused by kernel parser differences: 1. If task validity is not affected, correction may be postponed. 2. If task validity is affected, clearly mark that a rescan or kernel correction is needed. 

Article 51.1. Linkage with Status 

1. If Event Structure , MISC Label Summary when present, and Tasks Area already form a suffi-ciently consistent evidence chain, do not mark wait_kernel_update only because of preprocessing, artifact rejection, quality control, or implementation details. 2. If the document is already sufficient as textual support for existing Classical Task or Potential Task entries, but final release still requires administrator or human-reviewer confirmation, LLM review scripts should temporarily mark wait_manual_review .3. If Event Structure cannot support existing tasks and the issue mainly concerns human judgment about task validity, event semantics, evidence conflict, label mapping, deletion, or downgrading, 

Status should normally be wait_manual_review .4. Status may be completed only when Event Structure , relevant tasks, route explanations, and 

MISC Label Summary when present form a sufficiently consistent and traceable implementation basis, and a human reviewer or administrator has confirmed it. 

Article 52. Context-Dependent Events 

If the same event code has different meanings across files, tasks, or experimental phases, it is a context-dependent event. For such events: 1. Do not interpret their meaning without file-level context. 2. Warn explicitly in Event Structure or Data Quality Notes .3. Without stable context rules, do not use them directly in high-confidence Classical Task entries. 

Article 53. Later Updates for super meta 

When a Classical Task depends on super meta , human reviewers are normally not required to manually rewrite Event Structure first. Such updates should be handled by later kernel and LLM automation. 

Article 53.1. Optional Notes under Event Structure 

25 1. An optional explanatory Note may appear after the Event Structure table, but is not required. 2. The note must not repeat the meanings of the Type enum values; event and admin are defined by this rulebook. 3. Use the note only when: • Raw data has parsing ambiguity, such as the same event code meaning different things in different files. • Events come from multiple sources, such as companion files and trigger channels, and their merge logic matters. • Other explanations materially affect kernel implementation. 4. Do not use the note to introduce new events that are absent from the table. 5. If a later kernel update changes event representation, first update this note and related task routes, then decide whether to rewrite the main Event Structure table. 6. When neurodoc writes new scan results, any change to the Event Structure table, including count changes or event additions or removals, clears this note automatically. If the table is unchanged, the note remains unchanged. 

Article 53.2. Companion Files and External Tables 

1. Labels, event intervals, or auxiliary fields directly present in official companion files, 

participants.tsv , behavioral tables, sleep-stage files, or other formal sidecars may normally be used as valid information sources. 2. If such information is a discrete event or time span and will be used for target_events or event-route tasks, it must first be registered in Event Structure .3. If such information is an epoch-level class, scalar, count, score, or continuous quantity, do not force it into Event Structure ; route it through MISC Label Summary or task Notes as appropriate. 4. These fields are raw only when directly readable without extra derivation. If they require pairing, mapping, normalization, cross-file joining, or rule-based reconstruction, they are super .

Article 54. General Rule 

### MISC Label Summary describes the source, meaning, and implementation of numeric or continuous auxiliary labels.

1. If any task uses misc as Label Route , ### MISC Label Summary must exist. 2. If no task uses the misc route, this subsection may be omitted. 3. Native misc channels found during scanning may be registered here for future tasks even when no current task uses them.

This section is normally generated by the kernel and LLM. Human reviewers should edit it only when it is clearly wrong, incomplete, or in conflict with task dependencies.

Article 62. Conditions for Classical Task 

A Classical Task is valid only when both conditions hold: 1. Evidence shows that external literature or an authoritative source clearly used this dataset or an identifiable subset of it. 2. The evidence clearly supports the current task objective, label definition, or supervision target.

• Epoch Method

Article 76. Human Review Handling 

When target_events and Event Structure are inconsistent, human reviewers should do one of: 1. Adjust them to real existing labels. 2. Mark in the submission notes that a rescan is needed. 3. Delete the task. 

Article 77. Label Route 

1. Label Route describes the source and storage form of the task‘s supervision signal. Legal values are: • event : supervision comes from the event stream, namely discrete events in ### Event Structure , regardless of whether raw storage was annotation or stim channel. • misc : supervision is numeric or continuous and stored in a misc channel registered in ### MISC Label Summary .• info : supervision comes from file-level subject or device properties registered in ### Info , such as sex, age, or group. 2. Label Route , target_events , and Target Definition must be consistent: • Label Route = event : Target Definition uses an Event ID -> y mapping table. • Label Route = misc : Target Definition uses two columns, MISC Channel Name and YMapping Notes .• Label Route = info : Target Definition uses two columns, Info Field and Y Mapping Notes ; see Article 77.2. 3. For misc and info routes, the label source is uniquely specified by the first column of Target Definition ; do not additionally declare Label Field .4. If a task is an exception, explain the reason in task Notes .

Article 77.1. misc Route Usage: Epoch-Level Scalars and Continuous Values 

1. The misc route stores epoch-level numeric labels, continuous quantities, or categorical labels mapped from channel values, such as response_time , ratings, density, duration, physiological indicators, or derived factor scores such as p_factor .2. Epoch anchor events, which define epoch boundaries, are independent from the supervision-signal route. Label Route = misc does not prevent Epoch Method from using event anchoring or seg-mentation. 3. misc sources are of two kinds: • Native misc: a misc channel already present in raw files, or a numeric events.tsv column such as response_time that can be directly read and filled per segmentation. Meta Level = raw .• Derived misc: a misc channel newly created by the kernel from external tables, such as 

participants.tsv or companion files, or from derivation logic. Values are filled per segmenta-tion, with subject-level values constant across all segmentations for that subject. Meta Level = super ; Article 77.8 applies. 4. One dataset may contain multiple misc channels, such as misc:p_factor , misc:externalizing ,or misc:response_time . Each task may reference only one; that channel name must appear in the first column of Target Definition .5. Tasks using this route require ### MISC Label Summary to register source, unit, alignment, and necessary derivation logic. 

Article 77.2. Target Definition Format for Label Route = info 

1. When Label Route = info , Target Definition uses this format:   

> Info Field Y Mapping Notes
> sex Male -> 0; Female -> 1

2. Values in Info Field must match field names registered in ### Info .3. If that field has Completeness = missing , partial (x/n) , or incomplete , the task cannot be a Classical Task ; it may be at most a Potential Task , and task Notes should describe missingness. 4. The info route applies only to raw file-level fields directly exported by the kernel into ### Info ,such as sex, age, device, or native group. If median split, normalization, pairing, cross-file joining, or derivation is needed, use derived misc instead; see Articles 77.1 and 77.8. 

30 5. Y Mapping Notes may freely describe how original values map to y. If y directly follows the field value, write no mapping .6. Each info route task may use only one source field; therefore Target Definition may contain only one row. 

Article 77.3. Label Field and the Single-Source Rule 

1. Label Route = event tasks do not use Label Field .2. Label Route in {misc, info} tasks also no longer declare Label Field ; the label source is specified by the first column of Target Definition .3. The first column of Target Definition must be locatable inside the document: • Label Route = misc : it appears in the Channel column of ### MISC Label Summary .• Label Route = info : it appears in the Field column of ### Info .4. For misc and info , the first column of Target Definition may contain only one unique value, and the table may contain only one row. This restriction does not apply to event tasks. 

Article 77.4. Target Definition Format Matrix 

Target Definition table columns are determined by Label Route x Task Type :               

> Label Route Task Type Header columns
> event classification | Event ID | y | Meaning |event regression | Event ID | Value | Meaning | (rare)
> misc classification | MISC Channel Name | Y Mapping Notes | misc regression | MISC Channel Name | Y Mapping Notes | info classification | Info Field | Y Mapping Notes | info regression | Info Field | Y Mapping Notes |

1. Headers and column order must exactly match this matrix. LLMs and human reviewers must not add or remove columns. 2. For event routes, every Event ID cell must come from ### Event Structure ; see Article 74. 3. For misc and info routes, Y Mapping Notes may be no mapping when y equals the raw field or channel value, or may freely describe a custom conversion. 

Article 77.5. Single-Target Task Rule 

1. Each task encodes one learning target: one task equals one supervision route and one target schema. 2. Multidimensional labels, such as psychological four-factor scores 

internalizing/externalizing/attention/p_factor , must be split into separate tasks. Each misc or info task may use only one MISC Channel Name or one Info Field .3. event tasks may contain multiple Event ID rows when those rows jointly define a valid classification or regression mapping. 4. If one external evidence item covers multiple dimensions, reuse the same evidence in each split task instead of merging tasks. 5. For misc and info , the first column of Target Definition , namely MISC Channel Name or Info Field , may contain only one unique value within the task. 6. More complex composite tasks, such as a joint label built from multiple sources, may conceptually be expressed through combinations of simpler tasks. For consistency and brevity, this document currently defines no dedicated schema for complex tasks. 

Article 77.6. Mapping Requirements for the misc Route 

1. Label Route = misc tasks may be classification or regression .2. When Task Type = classification and the label comes from a misc channel, Y Mapping Notes must clearly explain how channel values map to class labels. 3. Thresholding, binning, median split, taking the first value, aggregation, or any other conversion on 

misc must be described in Y Mapping Notes or Notes .4. If the misc channel itself is kernel-derived, Article 77.8 still applies. 

Article 77.7. Multiple-Class Requirement for classification 

1. For Task Type = classification , the y column in Target Definition must contain at least two distinct values. 

31 2. For Label Route = event , a y cell may be either one class id or a comma-separated list of class ids when one event is intentionally mapped to multiple categories. 3. If evidence or data supports only a single class, for example only a cue-onset marker with no contrast class, change the task to regression when applicable or downgrade/delete it. Do not keep a one-class classification task. 

Article 77.8. Derived Misc Implies Super Meta 

1. Any misc channel newly created by a kernel is derived data. Any task carried by it must have Meta Level = super , and Super Meta Required = Yes ; see Article 37. 2. Derived misc must be explicitly declared as derived in ### MISC Label Summary . It is recom-mended to describe derivation steps in task Notes , including source column, fill method per segmen-tation, units, and outlier handling. This recommendation is not enforced by detect.py ; it is review and implementation guidance. 3. If the source field for derivation does not exist in the dataset, including events.tsv ,

participants.tsv , or native misc channels, the task is invalid and must be deleted. 

Article 78. Meta Level 

1. Meta Level = raw means the task can be implemented from raw labels. 2. Meta Level = super means the task depends on additional derived, joined, or transformed labels. 3. If the label requires event pairing, class folding, subject-info mapping, standard-system mapping, or continuous-variable calculation, mark it super .

Article 79. Evidence 

1. Evidence presents external support for task validity or parameter choice. 2. Classical Task must provide Evidence .3. Potential Task may provide Evidence , but it is not mandatory. 4. Evidence for Classical Task must be a specific locatable and identifiable source, not a vague placeholder. 5. If a DOI is unknown but an official page, competition page, or formal literature page is locatable, write that specific source instead of a vague phrase such as DOI unknown .

Article 80. Evidence Review Focus 

Human reviewers and LLMs should focus on: 1. Whether the evidence clearly used this dataset. 2. Whether the evidence clearly supports this task rather than only a similar task. 3. Whether label definitions in the evidence match the public data. 

Article 81. Hallucination Prevention 

Because LLMs may hallucinate paper interpretations, any case that cannot be confirmed as both using this dataset and supporting the task should be downgraded or deleted rather than forced to remain a 

Classical Task .

34 E Machine-checkable Subset of the Entry Rulebook 

This appendix summarizes a programmatic encoding of machine-checkable provisions from the shared entry rulebook, as used by detect.py to check analysis.md and kernel files. Check types include section_exists , pattern , field_value , and cross_check .

NeuroDoc Semantic Rulebook 

Hard Constraints (HC) — enforced in generation pipeline; not detectable by detect.py 

HC-1. Data fields in File Scan Result (Event ID, Count, Info table values, Kernel SHA256 fingerprint) must come from kernel scan program output; LLM must not invent or alter these fields. Semantic fields (Event Type, Meaning) are generated by LLM in an isolated inference step but must be based on actual evidence from dataset files; fabrication is not allowed. 

HC-2. Event Meaning and Type inference must be based on actual evidence in the dataset (events.tsv, sidecar JSON, README, etc.). LLM may consult any context information, but must not use unrelated reference papers, domain conventions, or BCI competition code systems as inference basis (e.g., must not infer four motor-imagery classes solely because event IDs are 1-4). Once an inference result is fixed it becomes a locked value; downstream pipeline must not modify it. 

HC-3. Meta Area fields (Channels, Sampling Rate) should be cross-referenced against the locator cache CSV. LLM may fill narrative gaps but must not override program-derived values. 

HC-4. LLM or automation may only set Status to: wait_manual_review – default output when no explicit structural kernel gap is exposed wait_kernel_update – explicit structural kernel gap or scan failure "completed" and "hallucination" are reserved for human reviewers only. 

HC-5. Kernel must NOT remap original event IDs. Prefix-based enrichment is allowed (e.g., subject_attr-event_id, session_attr-event_id). Original event IDs must pass through unchanged. 

HC-6. Kernel channel remapping is only allowed when explicit evidence exists in the dataset directory (companion file, README, sidecar) mapping ambiguous/numeric channels to named channels. Add a comment in kernel citing the evidence file. 

HC-7. Full scan count should be minimized to avoid re-scanning the same dataset. The pipeline should run a consistency check before final output to ensure document content matches scan results.

This dataset includes EEG recordings anno-tated for multiple types of artifacts such as eye movements, muscle artifacts, and electrode issues, among oth-ers. The corpus has been developed to assist in the training and evaluation of artifact detection models, with its latest release (v3.0.1) offering detailed annotations for all relevant events across different channel configura-tions. The data is divided into files containing EEG signals, with annotations provided in CSV format to mark the start and stop times of specific artifact events. It consists of over 160,000 events from 310 EEG files, cover-ing 213 patients and a total of nearly 100 hours of data. This dataset is particularly useful for research involv-ing artifact detection and seizure classification in clinical EEG data. Notably, it includes specific seizure annota-tions alongside standard artifact labels, facilitating the analysis of co-occurring events. Researchers are encour-aged to cite the corresponding publications for more details on the dataset’s creation and annotation methodology.

61 G Broader Impacts and Responsible Release 

NeuroDoc is intended to improve the transparency, reproducibility, and maintainability of EEG benchmarking. By converting heterogeneous public EEG datasets into reviewed task documents and executable kernels, the resource can reduce repeated manual task engineering, make benchmark assumptions easier to inspect, and support more comparable evaluation of EEG representation models. The community-review workflow may also help expose uncertain task claims, missing evidence, and document-kernel inconsistencies that would otherwise remain implicit in downstream benchmark construction. The same properties create risks if the released benchmark interfaces are over-interpreted. Perfor-mance on the benchmark should not be treated as evidence of clinical readiness, diagnostic validity, or population-level generalization beyond the documented source datasets. The corpus inherits limitations from public EEG repositories, including uneven demographic and clinical coverage, laboratory-specific acquisition protocols, device differences, annotation practices, and access restric-tions. Because EEG and related metadata can be sensitive biomedical information, downstream users must respect the consent, privacy, licensing, and data-use terms of the original repositories. This release does not redistribute raw EEG recordings, but it may still make it easier to locate and operationalize tasks derived from sensitive human-subject data. Additional risks arise from automation. LLM-assisted drafting can introduce unsupported interpre-tations, overly broad task definitions, or subtle mismatches between a document and its executable kernel. To mitigate these risks, the released corpus is restricted to completed and reviewed entries, task claims are governed by an explicit rulebook, machine-checkable constraints protect scan-derived and kernel-derived facts, and NeuroAudit separates human review authority from automated drafting. The Croissant metadata further records responsible-use information, limitations, and provenance. Future extensions of the corpus should preserve these safeguards and should continue to distinguish reviewed release entries from draft or unaudited materials. 62
