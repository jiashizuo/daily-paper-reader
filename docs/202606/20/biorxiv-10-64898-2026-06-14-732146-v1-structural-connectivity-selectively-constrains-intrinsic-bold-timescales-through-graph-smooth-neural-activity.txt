ABSTRACT 4

Structural connectivity defines the network architecture supporting large -scale brain dynamics, yet how this 5

network constrains the temporal statistics of signals defined on it remains poorly understood. Prior work 6

has reported associa tions between intrinsic timescales of resting -state fMRI and structural connectivity 7

strength, but it is unclear which signal components primarily drive this relationship. Here, we adopt a graph 8

signal processing framework to analyze intrinsic temporal pro perties of networked brain signals. Regional 9

Blood Oxygenation Level Dependent (BOLD) activity is modeled as a graph signal supported on the 10 

structural connectome and decomposed via graph spectral filtering into low -frequency (structure -coupled) 11 

and high -frequency (structure -decoupled) components. Using diffusion MRI –derived structural 12 

connectivity and resting -state fMRI from 100 unrelated participants of the Human Connectome Project, 13 

intrinsic timescales are quantified using relative ly low -frequency power and related to node -wise structural 14 

connectivity strength while controlling for regional volume. We show that intrinsic timescales derived from 15 

structure -coupled signals exhibit robust positive associations with structural connectivity strength at both 16 

gro up and inter -individual levels, whereas structure -decoupled signals display substantially weaker 17 

coupling. Notably, slow structure -decoupled dynamics are preferentially expressed in higher -order 18 

association cortex. Graph -spectral null models further demons trate that these effects critically depend on 19 

the empirical organization of the structural network. Together, these results establish a graph -spectral 20 

interpretation of structure –timescale coupling, showing that network topology selectively constrains the 21 

temporal statistics of graph -smooth neural activity. 22

INTRODUCTION 9

Understanding how structural connectivity shapes functional brain dynamics remains a central challenge in 10 

network neuroscience (Deco et al., 2011; Fotiadis et al., 2024; Honey et al., 2009; Park & Friston, 2013; 11 

Schmitt, 2025; Sporns et al., 2002; Sporns et al., 2000) . A widely used concept in this context is structure –12 

function coupling (SFC), which quantifies the extent to which functional interactions between brain regions 13 

reflect the underlying anatomical connectivity derived from white -matter pathways (Bashirgonbadi et al., 14 

2025; Baum et al., 2020; Popp et al., 2025; Preti & Van De Ville, 2019) . Numerous neuroimaging studies 15 

have demonstrated that strong er structure –function coupling, measured at whole -brain or network scales, is 16 

associated with superior cognitive performance, including general intelligence and executive efficiency 17 

(Bashirgonbadi et al., 2025; Mišić et al., 2016; Popp et al., 2025; Preti & Van De Ville, 2019; Zimmermann 18 

et al., 2016) . These findings suggest that the alignment between structure and function plays a fundamental 19 

role in effective brain computation. 20 

Beyond static functional interactions , increasing attention has been devoted to the temporal organization 21 

of brain activity (Lurie et al., 2024; Munn et al., 2024; Northoff et al., 2025; van Es et al., 2025) . In 22 

particular, intrinsic functional timesca les have emerged as a key descriptor of how neural activity fluctuates 23 

over time. Functional timescales are commonly quantified using autocorrelation -based metrics or spectral 24 

properties of resting -state fMRI BOLD signals (Foster et al., 2016; Murray et al., 2014) . Regions with short 25 

intrinsic timescales exhibit fast, trans ient dynamics dominated by higher -frequency fluctuations, whereas 26 

regions with long timescales display slow, temporally integrated activity dominated by low -frequency 27 

components. 28 

Importantly, intrinsic timescales are not uniformly distributed across the co rtex. Converging evidence 29 

from human fMRI (Mecklenbrauck et al., 2024; Raut et al., 2020) , electrophysiology (Gao et al., 2020) ,30 

and cross -species studies (Li et al., 2025; Song et al., 2024) has revealed a hierarchical organization, in 31 

which unimodal sensory and motor cortices exhibit short timescales, while transmodal association 32 

regions —including default mode and frontoparietal networks —show progr essively longer timescales 33 

(Golesorkhi et al., 2021; Hasson et al., 2008; Huntenburg et al., 2018; Murray et al., 2014) . This hierarchy 34 

closely mirrors functional specialization: unimodal regions support rapid stimu lus -driven processing, 35 

whereas transmodal regions enable integrative and cognitively demanding operations that require 36 

information accumulation over extended temporal windows (Chaudhuri et al., 2015; Gao et al., 2020 ;37 

Honey et al., 2012; Raut et al., 2020) .38 

Several studies have linked this temporal hierarchy to regional structural connectivity strength (SC -39 

strength). Empirical evidence indicates that brain regions with stronger or denser structural connections 40 

tend t o exhibit slower functional dynamics and longer intrinsic timescales (Demirtaş et al., 2019; Liégeois 41 

et al., 2019) . From a dynamical systems perspective, increased recurrent or long -range structural coupling 42 

can stabilize neural activity and promote temporal integration, leading to slower BOLD fluctuations. These 43 

findings suggest that SC -strength provides a mechanistic substrate through which anatomical connectivity 44 

influences regional functional timescales (Fallon et al., 2020; Gao et al., 2020; Ponce -Alvarez, 2025) .45

However, SC -st rength alone does not fully explain the spatial organization of brain dynamics. The degree 1

to which structural connectivity constrains functional interactions varies systematically across the cortex. 2

Prior work has shown that structure –function coupling is strongest in unimodal sensory and motor regions, 3

where functional connectivity closely follows anatomical pathways, and weakest in transmodal association 4

cortex, where functional interactions increasingly diverge from structural constraints (Baum et al., 2020; 5

Mišić et al., 2016; Preti & Van De Ville, 2019) . This progressive decoupling has been interpreted as a 6

marker of functional flexibility, enabling higher -order cognitive processes that are less rigidly bound to 7

fixed anatomical circuits .8

Notably, this spatial gradient of structure –function coupling parallels the hierarchy of intrinsic 9

timescales. Unimodal regions combine high structure –function coupling with short timescales, reflecting 10 

fast, structure -driven processing (Vázquez -Rodríguez et al., 2019) . In contrast, transmodal regions exhibit 11 

lower coupling and longer timescales, c onsistent with slow, integrative, and context -dependent dynamics 12 

(Gao et al., 2020) . Together, these observations point to a close interaction between structural co nstraints, 13 

functional alignment, and temporal integration across the cortical hierarchy. 14 

Integrating these findings suggests that structure –function coupling may mediate the relationship between 15 

SC -strength and functional timescales. In regions with strong coupling, anatomical connectivity may 16 

directly shape temporal dynamics. Conversely, in transmodal regions with weaker coupling, long intrinsic 17 

timescales may emerge from distributed and flexible functional interactions that are only partially 18 

constrained by structural architecture. This perspective also offers a refined interpretation of prior reports 19 

linking structure –function coupling to cognitive ability. While higher global coupling has been associated 20 

with improved cognitive performance (Popp et al., 2025; Zimmermann et al., 2016) , regional specialization 21 

indicates that cognitive complexity relies on selective decoupling in the association cortex, accompanied by 22 

prolonged temporal integration. 23 

From a signal processing perspective, functional brain activity can be modeled as a graph signal defined 24 

on the str uctural connectome (Huang et al., 2018; Medaglia et al., 2018; Preti & Van De Ville, 2019) .25 

Graph signal processing (GSP) provides a principled framework for decomposing functional signals into 26 

structure -aligned (co upled) and structure -liberal (decoupled) components using graph spectral modes 27 

(Bashirgonbadi et al., 2025; Ortega et al., 2018) . Within this fra mework, structure –function coupling 28 

corresponds to signal smoothness with respect to the structural graph, while intrinsic timescales emerge 29 

from the temporal statistics of graph -filtered signals. This formulation provides a natural link between 30 

anatomical connectivity, temporal dynamics, and cognitive function. 31 

Building on this framework, the present study investigates how structural connectivity, structure –function 32 

coupling, and intrinsic functional timescales jointly shape large -scale human brain dynamic s. We test the 33 

hypothesis that intrinsic timescales derived from structure -coupled (graph low -pass) components of BOLD 34 

activity are more strongly associated with regional SC -strength than those derived from structure -35 

decoupled (graph high -pass) components. We further hypothesize that the canonical structure –timescale 36 

relationship can be largely reproduced using structure -aligned signal components alone, whereas structure -37 

decoupled activity exhibits weak dependence on anatomical connectivity. Extending these predictions, we 38 

examine whether association cortical regions display relatively longer intrinsic timescales within structure -39 

decoupled signals compared to unimodal sensory regions. Finally, we assess the specificity of these effects 40 

using null models base d on randomized graph spectral bases to determine whether observed relationships 41 

depend on the empirical organization of the structural connectome. 42

Frequency Analysis of Functional Signals 5

For each time point, regional BOLD activity was averaged within each of the 360 regions of interest, 6

yielding a graph signal defined on t he structural connectome. The HCP resting -state fMRI dataset consists 7

of 1200 time points, resulting in 1200 graph signals defined over an identical node set. To analyze 8

functional dynamics in the graph spectral domain, each graph signal was projected onto the Laplacian 9

eigenvectors using the GFT, yielding spectral coefficients that characterize the spatial frequency content of 10 

brain activity at each time point. To dissociate structure -aligned and structure -liberal components of 11 

functional activity, each gr aph signal was further decomposed into low -frequency (Coupled , 𝐱 𝐶 ) and high -12 

frequency ( Decoupled , 𝐱 𝐷 ) components using graph spectral filtering (Preti & Van De Ville, 2019) . This 13 

decomposition enables the separation of functional dynamics that ar e smooth with respect to the structural 14 

network from those that exhibit high spatial variation across the connectome. 15

DATA AND ANALYSIS METHODS 17 

DATASET 18 

The Human Connectome Project (HCP) provides high -quality multimodal neuroimaging and behavioral 19 

data fr om a large cohort of healthy adults (Van Essen et al., 2013) , offering a robu st platform for 20 

investigating large -scale brain connectivity. In this study, we analyzed data from 100 unrelated healthy 21 

participants (54% female; mean age = 29.11 ± 3.67 years) with available resting -state fMRI (rs -fMRI) and 22 

diffusion -weighted imaging (DW I). Resting -state fMRI data were preprocessed using the HCP minimal 23 

preprocessing pipeline, which includes motion correction, distortion correction, intensity normalization, 24 

and projection to the 32k_fs_LR cortical surface space (Glasser et al., 2013) . Structural connectivity 25 

matrices were derived from diffusion MRI using MRtrix3 (Tournier et al., 2019) employing anatomically 26 

constrained tractography, spherical deconvolution, and streamline filtering via SIFT2 to reduce 27 

tractography biases (Smith et al., 2015) . Conn ectivity between 360 cortical regions, defined according to 28 

the Glasser atlas, was quantified using the number of streamlines. A group -level structural connectome was 29 

constructed using distance -dependent thresholding followed by logarithmic transformation to stabilize the 30 

distribution of edge weights. Analysis code is publicly available through the project GitHub repository: 31

STRUCTURE –TIMESCALE ANALYSIS AND STATISTICAL TESTING 1

The association between intrins ic timescales and structural connectivity strength was assessed using 2

partial Spearman correlation, controlling for regional volume to account for potential confounds related to 3

parcel size. Correlation analyses were conducted at both the group level, usin g timescale measures 4

averaged across participants, and at the inter -individual level by computing correlations separately for each 5

participant. Differences between structure -coupled and structure -decoupled components were evaluated 6

using paired non -paramet ric statistical tests across participants, and effect sizes were quantified using 7

Cohen’s d. To assess the specificity of observed effects to the empirical structural connectome, null models 8

were generated by randomizing the graph Fourier basis while prese rving the Laplacian eigenvalue 9

spectrum. Observed correlation values were then compared against the corresponding null distributions to 10 

evaluate statistical significance. 11

RELATIONSHIP BETWEEN STRUCTURAL CONNECTIV ITY STRENGTH AND INT RINSIC BOLD TIMESCALES 1

We first evaluated the baseline relationship between structu ral connectivity strength and intrinsic 2

functional timescales using resting -state fMRI data from the Human Connectome Project. Structural 3

connectivity strength was defined as the sum of streamline weights per region, while intrinsic timescales 4

were quantif ied using the relative low -frequency power (RLFP) of regional BOLD signals. As shown in 5

Figure 2-A, structural connectivity strength exhibited a clear positive association with RLFP across cortical 6

regions, indicating that nodes with stronger structural em bedding tend to exhibit slower temporal dynamics. 7

To control for potential confounds related to parcel size, partial Spearman correlations were computed 8

while regressing out regional volume. At the group level, this analysis revealed a significant positive 9

association (r = 0.49, p = 9.49×e⁻²³), consistent with prior findings in both human and animal studies 10 

(Fallon et al., 2020; Sethi et al., 2017) . The spatia l distribution of intrinsic timescales is illustrated in Fig ure 11 

2-B, where RLFP values vary systematically across the cortex. Higher values are observed in association 12 

regions, while lower values are found in primary sensory and motor areas. This spatial h eterogeneity 13 

reflects the well -established cortical hierarchy of temporal processing and provides a spatial context for the 14 

observed structure –timescale relationship (Foster et al., 2016; Golesorkhi et al., 2021; Lur ie et al., 2024; 15 

Mecklenbrauck et al., 2024; Munn et al., 2024; Murray et al., 2014) . Collectively, these results reproduce 16 

the canonical association between structural connectivity strength and intrinsic functional timescales, 17 

establishing a reference po int for subsequent graph -spectral analyses. 18

STRUCTURAL CONNECTIVI TY SELECTIVELY CONST RAINS GRAPH -SMOOTH FUNCTIONAL DY NAMICS 20 

To determine whether the observed structure –timescale relationship depends on the alignment of 21 

functional activity with the structu ral graph, BOLD signals were decomposed into graph -coupled (low -22 

pass) and graph -decoupled (high -pass) components using graph spectral filtering. Intrinsic timescales were 23 

then estimated separately for each component using RLFP. As shown in Fig ure 3-A, intr insic timescales 24 

derived from the coupled (low -pass) component exhibited a strong positive association with structural 25 

connectivity strength. In contrast, the corresponding relationship for the decoupled (high -pass) component 26 

wa s substantially attenuated ( Figure 3-B), indicating reduced sensitivity of structurally misaligned activity 27 

to the underlying graph topology. This dissociation was confirmed by group -level partial Spearman 28 

correlation analysis controlling for regional volume, which revealed a signifi cant difference between the 29 

two components (Fis her Z = 4.86, p = 1.16e⁻⁰⁶; Figure 3-C). Specifically, the correlation between structural 30 

connectivity strength and RLFP was significantly higher for the graph -coupled component than for the 31 

graph -decoupled co mponent. These findings support Hypothesis H1, demonstrating that structural 32 

connectivity selectively constrains graph -smooth functional dynamics, and Hypothesis H3, indicating that 33 

this constraint is markedly reduced for graph -decoupled activity. Overall, these results show that the 34 

classical structure –timescale relationship is not a uniform property of functional signals, but is 35 

preferentially expressed in graph -smooth components aligned with the structural connectome. 36

In this study, we leveraged a graph signal processing (GSP) framework to examine how t he structural 26 

connectome constrains the temporal statistics of functional signals defined on it. By modeling resting -state 27 

BOLD activity as graph signals and decomposing them into graph -spectral components, we show that the 28 

canonical association between st ructural connectivity strength (SC -strength) and intrinsic functional 29

Within the GSP formulation, low -frequency graph components correspond to signals that are smooth 4

The strong association observed between these components and intrinsic timescales suggests that 6

structurally misaligned activity is less directly constrained by the underlying graph topology. 12 

Importantly, the separation between structure -coupled and structure -decoupled dynamics is not limited to 13 

group -level analyses, but is consistently observed at the level of individual subjects. This inter -individual 14 

consistency indicates that the preferential coupling between structural connectivity strength and graph -15 

smooth temporal dynamics reflects a stable property of networked brain signals, rather than an artifact of 16 

aggregation. This inter -individual robustness strengthens the interpretation that structural connectivity 17 

exerts a selective and reproducible influence on intrinsic neural timescales through graph -smooth activity. 18 

Our results refine the noti on of structure –function decoupling in higher -order cortex. While structure -19 

decoupled signals are often interpreted as reflecting dynamics that are released from anatomical constraints, 20 

our findings suggest a more nuanced picture. Specifically, transmodal regions exhibited significantly 21 

elevated intrinsic timescales in the decoupled component, alongside a structure –timescale relationship that 22 

differed markedly from that of the unimodal cortex. Importantly, this relationship was not abolished, but 23 

instead ma nifested as a distinct mode of structural modulation.  This observation indicates that  the 24 

transmodal  cortex does not operate in a structure -independent regime, but rather engages structural 25 

connectivity in a qualitatively different manner. In these regions , anatomical connectivity may act as an 26 

enabling scaffold that supports the emergence of slow, integrative dynamics, rather than directly 27 

constraining local temporal properties. Such a facilitative role of structure is consistent with the integrative 28 

funct ion of  transmodal  cortex and aligns with theoretical accounts in which higher -order regions exploit 29 

distributed connectivity to sustain extended temporal processing windows.  In contrast,  unimodal  regions 30 

exhibited weaker and less differentiated structure –timescale relationships in the decoupled signal, 31 

consistent with more locally driven dynamics and reduced reliance on large -scale integration. Together, 32 

these findings support a hierarchical view of structure –function interaction in which the impact of 33 

anat omical connectivity on intrinsic dynamics varies across cortical systems, rather than uniformly 34 

diminishing along the cortical hierarchy. 35 

The specificity of our findings to the empirical structural connectome was confirmed using graph -based 36 

null models. Ra ndomization of the graph Fourier basis, while preserving the spectral properties of the 37 

Laplacian, substantially reduced the observed structure –timescale relationships. This result demonstrates 38 

that the effects reported here are not a trivial consequence o f spectral filtering or low -frequency dominance 39 

in BOLD signals, but instead depend critically on the spatial organization of white -matter connectivity. 40 

Together, these findings advance our understanding of structure –function relationships by demonstrating 41 

that structural connectivity does not uniformly constrain all aspects of spontaneous neural activity. Rather, 42 

it selectively shapes the temporal properties of graph -smooth activity, while allowing for regionally 43 

specific structure -decoupled dynamics, part icularly in association cortex. This spectral dissociation 44 

provides a principled framework for reconciling anatomical constraints with functional flexibility in large -45 

scale brain dynamics. 46
