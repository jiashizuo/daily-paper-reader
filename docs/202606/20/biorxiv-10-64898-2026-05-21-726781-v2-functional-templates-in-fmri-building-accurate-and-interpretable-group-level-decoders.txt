# 1 Introduction 23 

In functional magnetic resonance imaging (fMRI), comparing activity patterns across individuals presents 24 

a fundamental challenge due to variations in both brain anatomy and function. This challenge is par-25 

ticularly acute because fMRI captures both structural differences in brain organization and individual 26 

variations in functional responses. While traditional anatomical normalization approaches address this by 27 

mapping individual brains onto standard templates through various transformations (Fonov et al., 2009), 28 

these methods do not adequately capture the full scope of functional differences between subjects (Haxby 29 

et al., 2001; Sabuncu et al., 2009). 30 

1.1 Functional Alignment: Pairwise vs. Template Strategies 31 

Functional alignment has emerged as a complementary approach to anatomical registration and spatial 32 

normalization. It offers methods for establishing correspondences between neural patterns across individ-33 

uals using fMRI maps. Current techniques generally fall into two categories: pairwise and template-based. 34 

Pairwise methods, including Procrustes Analysis (Guntupalli et al., 2016) and Optimal Transport (OT)-35 

based alignment (Bazeille et al., 2019), directly map functional responses between pairs of subjects. 36 

While effective, these methods can only decode a given target subject; therefore, they lack a unified space 37 

for group analysis. Furthermore, these methods encounter computational challenges as the number of 38 

subjects increases. 39 

Template-based approaches address these limitations by creating a shared representational space between 40 

all subjects. Methods such as Generalized Procrustes Analysis (Gower, 1975) and Hyperalignment (Gun-41 

tupalli et al., 2016) iteratively refine a common functional template across all subjects. This eliminates 42 

the need for an arbitrary reference subject and has shown promise in improving group-level analyses 43 

(Jeganathan et al., 2024). 44 

However, template-based functional alignment introduces two important challenges. First, jointly esti-45 

mating the individual transformations and the group template is computationally demanding, as each 46 

refinement of the template requires recomputing every subject-to-template transformation. Second, eval-47

Together, these costs limit the feasibility of the approach for studies with limited computational resources. 50 

The Shared Response Model (SRM) (Chen et al., 2015) represents another significant advancement, 51 

with implementations like FastSRM (Richard & Thirion, 2023) that offer computational efficiency and 52 

improved decoding performance (Bazeille et al., 2021). However, SRM’s reliance on latent space rep-53 

resentation limits interpretability. The template has no representation in voxel space, which prevents 54 

activity mapping on the cortex. In contrast, functional templates in image space maintain direct cor-55 

respondence with brain anatomy, providing interpretable composite representations of neural patterns 56 

across subjects. Given their anatomical interpretability, image-space templates are particularly valuable 57 

for neuroscientific investigations, as they can be analyzed using traditional neuroimaging approaches and 58 

compared directly with anatomical templates. 59 

1.2 Key Open Questions 60 

Despite the expected benefits of functional templates, several critical questions remain unexplored. First, 61 

there has been limited systematic comparison between template-based and pairwise alignment strategies 62 

for a popular analytic approach in functional brain imaging: decoding conditions or variables of a task 63 

(hereafter ”task decoding”). In particular, voxelwise inter-subject correlation is the most reported metric 64 

in previous studies comparing different alignment methods (Andreella & Finos, 2022; Guntupalli et al., 65 

2016, 2018; Haxby et al., 2020), yet it is highly sensitive to spatial blurring and poorly informative in task 66 

settings (Bazeille et al., 2019; Chen et al., 2015). It remains unclear whether template-based methods 67 

offer clear advantages over pairwise alignment in the case of task decoding. Second, the extent to 68 

which voxel-space functional alignment preserves brain topography–for example, the precise localization 69 

of relevant voxels on the cortex–relative to anatomical registration requires investigation. Third, including 70 

test subjects in template generation could bias generalization, as noted by Jeganathan et al., 2024 for 71 

Procrustes; such a bias needs to be evaluated across different alignment methods. 72

1.4 Related Work 100 

Hyperalignment , introduced by Haxby et al., 2011, pioneered functional alignment by constructing a 101 

shared high-dimensional representational space across subjects. Unlike generalized Procrustes analysis 102 

(GPA) (Gower, 1975), which jointly estimates all transformations in an order-invariant manner, Hyper-103 

alignment proceeds in two sequential steps and is therefore sensitive to the order in which subjects are 104 

processed. First, the method iteratively aligns each subject to a growing functional template, updating 105 

the template at each step. Second, once the template is finalized, subject-specific transformations are 106 

recomputed with respect to this reference. 107

1.4 Related Work 5The approach is typically implemented within a searchlight framework (Kriegeskorte et al., 2006), where 108 

Procrustes transformations are estimated locally on small cortical patches, and these local solutions are 109 

then aggregated into whole-brain transformation matrices. Thanks to the relatively low computational 110 

cost of Procrustes alignment, Hyperalignment is often applied in an out-of-sample manner using a leave-111 

one-subject-out strategy. In our benchmark, we rely on GPA rather than Hyperalignment to avoid the 112 

order effect inherent to Hyperalignment’s sequential procedure, as in Bazeille et al., 2021 and Jeganathan 113 

et al., 2024. 114 

Connectivity-based Hyperalignment (Guntupalli et al., 2018) extends this framework by incorporating 115 

functional connectivity information, leading to hybrid hyperalignment approaches (Busch et al., 2021) 116 

that leverage both local activation patterns and connectivity profiles. This approach is highly relevant 117 

when used in conjunction with BOLD time series data, but is outside the scope of our study as we target 118 

homogeneous sources of data, i.e, we only incorporate z-scored condition maps. 119 

The ProMises model (Andreella & Finos, 2022) recasts the Procrustes alignment problem within a 120 

statistical framework. By imposing a whole-brain anatomical prior, it overcomes the locality constraints 121 

inherent to searchlight (Guntupalli et al., 2016) and piecewise (Bazeille et al., 2021) hyperalignment, 122 

while retaining a relatively low computational cost. Although comparing our framework with ProMises 123 

would be valuable, analogous statistical reformulations do not currently exist for Optimal Transport or 124 

Ridge alignment, preventing a meaningful comparison across all methods. 125 

Integrated Alignment (Jeganathan et al., 2024) proposes that combining anatomical and functional 126 

alignment yields improved decoding performance, independent of the specific alignment method. The 127 

approach interpolates between the identity transformation on anatomically registered data and the func-128 

tional alignment solution, producing templates that jointly capture shared structure and subject-specific 129 

functional features. However, this interpolation depends on a hyperparameter that must be tuned based 130 

on an external cohort, and the method is built within the surface-based diffeomorphic registration frame-131 

work MSMSulc (Glasser et al., 2016), making it impractical to apply directly in our setting. 132 

FUGW (Fused Unbalanced Gromov–Wasserstein) (Thual et al., 2022) formulates inter-subject alignment 133 

as a quadratic Optimal Transport problem that jointly optimizes functional similarity and spatial con-134 

straints. Beyond the functional matching term similar to the formulation of Bazeille et al., 2019, FUGW 135 

incorporates a Gromov–Wasserstein objective that aligns the pairwise geodesic structure of each sub-136 

ject’s anatomy. This quadratic matching of intra-subject distances enables the model to capture complex 137 

spatial correspondences that cannot be expressed by simple pointwise or linear constraints. However, 138 

solving the Gromov–Wasserstein problem is computationally demanding and scales poorly to whole-brain 139 

volumetric data. In addition, the unbalanced formulation allows for partial mappings between subjects, 140

# 2 Materials and Methods 144 

In this section, we detail the components of our benchmark. We begin by formally defining the alignment 145 

methods used to map data between pairs of subjects (Sections 2.1 & 2.2), followed by our piecewise strat-146

2.1 Notations 151 

Inter-subject functional alignment seeks to establish voxel-wise correspondences across individual brains to 152 

improve group-level analyses. Although we operate in the volumetric setting, the mathematical framework 153 

is agnostic to the volume–surface distinction; all methods considered here can be equivalently applied on 154 

cortical surface meshes by treating vertices in place of voxels, as in Jeganathan et al., 2024. Throughout, 155 

we assume that all subjects have already been anatomically normalized to a common template space. 156 

Let p denote the number of voxels (or vertices) shared across subjects after anatomical normalization, 157 

and let n denote the number of task-related effect maps (hereafter “task maps”) used for alignment, 158 

also consistent across subjects. For each individual, we stack the n task maps into a feature matrix 159 

F ∈ Rn×p. All matrices maintain a similar ordering between tasks and voxels respectively across all 160 

subjects. We will denote by || .|| F the Frobenius norm over matrices. 161

2.2.2 Optimal Transport 173 

Optimal Transport (OT) was first introduced for inter-subject alignment in neuroimaging by Bazeille et 174 

al., 2019. Unlike Procrustes analysis, which assumes a scaling of the data and a rigid transformation (e.g 175

2.3 Piecewise Alignment Approaches 198 

Aligning whole-brain voxel data across subjects poses significant computational challenges due to the 199 

high dimensionality of neuroimaging data. Furthermore, simultaneous alignment of all voxels across the 200 

entire brain may produce erroneous correspondences between anatomically distant regions. To address 201 

these issues, we adopt a piecewise alignment approach following Bazeille et al., 2021 and Jeganathan 202 

et al., 2024. This strategy leverages a brain atlas to define anatomically coherent parcels, within which 203 

voxel-wise transformations are computed independently. 204 

We use the Schaefer atlas (Schaefer et al., 2017) for our main experiments with 400 parcels, result-205 

ing in an average of 84 voxels per parcel. Control analyses showed that the effect of the number of 206 

parcels on our conclusions is negligible (see Appendix Figure 10). This partitioning ensures localized 207 

functional alignment while maintaining computational feasibility. We provide an overview of the list of 208 

hyperparameters to fix for each method in Table 1. 209

2.6 Experimental Design 263 

2.6.1 Task-Based Decoding Analysis 264 

To compare the various alignment techniques, we implemented a between-subject classification of task 265 

conditions (illustrated in Figure 1, right panels). The evaluation protocol follows a leave-one-subject-out 266 

cross-validation strategy, as described in Bazeille et al., 2021, to assess decoding performance across 267 

individuals. 268 

However, unlike Bazeille et al., 2021, which used data from different tasks for alignment and decoding, we 269 

used the same tasks for both, splitting each subject’s task data between alignment (20%) and decoding 270 

(80%), stratified by run to ensure equal contribution from each run. This choice limits sources of 271 

variability beyond inter-subject differences, notably inter-run and inter-task variability. The data split is 272 

repeated in a nested five-fold cross-validation, and results are averaged across folds. 273 

We employ a linear support vector classifier, which has proven effective for fMRI data (Lee et al., 2010). 274 

Specifically, we use Scikit-learn ’s LinearSVC implementation (Pedregosa et al., 2011), consistent with 275 

Bazeille et al., 2021 and Jeganathan et al., 2024. 276 

2.6.2 Three Alignment Strategies 277 

We investigate three functional alignment strategies from the literature: two template-based approaches 278 

and one relying solely on pairwise alignments. We present them in the following paragraphs. 279 

Out-of-Sample Template We adapt this strategy from Jeganathan et al., 2024 (panel 1 of Figure 280 

1), where it was used to study template generalization to new individuals. We first set one subject 281 

apart as the left-out subject, compute a template from all other subjects, then align the left-out subject 282 

to this template in a pairwise fashion. This ensures that no information about the left-out subject is 283 

incorporated during template creation, unlike the in-sample template. However, performing complete 284 

LOSO cross-validation among S subjects requires generating S templates and performing S pairwise 285 

alignments, making this the most computationally intensive strategy. 286

Each alignment strategy (out-of-sample, pairwise, in-sample) is evaluated across all combinations of tasks and alignment methods (Procrustes, Optimal Transport, Ridge regression, Shared Response Model) through a nested cross-validation proce-dure. Each subject’s task data is first split between alignment and decoding groups using a 5-fold outer cross-validation loop (20% alignment data, 80% decoding data). Inside each fold, the pipeline consists of two phases: A. an alignment phase where functional alignment transformations are computed from alignment data, and B. a decoding phase where these transformations are applied to separate decoding data to perform leave-one-subject-out cross-validation, yielding one accuracy score per subject. For each task and method combination, accuracy scores are averaged across the 5 folds for each subject. The three alignment strategies differ in how the functional alignment target is constructed: 1. Out-of-sample template alignment : For each held-out test subject, a template is generated from the N-1 training subjects’ alignment data, explicitly excluding the test subject. The test subject’s decoding data is then aligned to this out-of-sample template in a pairwise fashion. The classifier is then trained on the training subjects’ aligned data and tested on the aligned test subject’s data. 2. Pairwise alignment : For each inner cross-validation fold, the left-out test subject serves as the alignment target. All N-1 training subjects’ decoding data are individually aligned to the test subject’s space. The classifier is trained on the aligned training subjects and is evaluated on the test subject in its native space. 3. In-sample tem-plate alignment : A single population template is computed from all subjects’ alignment data. Each subject’s decoding data is then projected to this template space using the computed transformations. Leave-one-subject-out cross-validation is performed in the common template space, where the classifier is trained on N-1 subjects and tested on the remaining subject. Technical implementation leverages fmralign’s parallel processing capabilities for template construction and alignment computations, while classification is performed using scikit-learn’s LinearSVC with default parameters. 

Pairwise Alignment Used in Bazeille et al., 2021, this approach (panel 2 of Figure 1) minimizes the 287 

domain shift between subjects by mapping each subject to the left-out subject. While avoiding template 288 

creation, this strategy requires S(S − 1) mappings for complete evaluation across S subjects. 289 

In-Sample Template Coined as ”In-sample Template” by Jeganathan et al., 2024, this is the most 290

These mappings align decoding data to the template space, where leave-one-subject-out (LOSO) cross-293 

validation is performed. The computational complexity in terms of alignment operations grows linearly 294

3.3 OT Templates Show the Smallest In- vs. Out-of-sample Bias 360 

To assess whether incorporating the test subject during template construction introduces a bias, po-361 

tentially inflating decoding performance, we compare in-sample and out-of-sample template generation 362 

strategies (Figure 4). 363 

This analysis directly parallels the observations of Jeganathan et al., 2024, who reported substantial bias 364 

for Procrustes-based templates. In line with their findings, we observe a significant performance increase 365 

for Procrustes in the in-sample setting on six of the seven tasks. This confirms that including the test 366 

subject in template estimation leads to overly optimistic decoding scores. Ridge behaves similarly: it 367 

demonstrates a consistent positive bias across all tasks, with magnitudes exceeding those observed for 368 

Procrustes, indicating a greater susceptibility of this method to inclusion bias. SRM follows a similar 369 

trend. However, the analysis in Appendix Figure 7 indicates that the large bias mainly arises from the 370 

chance-level performance of the out-of-sample template strategy, whereas the in-sample strategy yields 371 

decoding scores comparable to those obtained with the other methods. While Chen et al., 2015 reported 372

3.4 A Trade-Off Between Spatial Specificity and Activity Variability 384 

With the goal of interpretability in mind, we examine the computed templates and investigate differences 385 

in the resulting classifier weight distributions. Since OT, Procrustes and Ridge produce templates in 386 

image space, we investigate how they affect decoder feature localization on the cortex. 387 

Since we use linear decoders, we visualize the distribution of voxel contributions in Figure 5, presenting 388 

fold-averaged classifier weight maps for the face-adult condition of the IBC FaceBody task. Relative to 389 

anatomical alignment, Procrustes produces a template where discriminatory voxels (with higher weights 390

similar trend, though weights appear more dispersed across the cortical surface. 392 

In contrast, Optimal Transport shows greater fidelity to the weight distribution of the anatomical tem-393 

plate, with clear delineation of discriminatory voxels on the left hemisphere. In addition, the bottom row 394 

reveals a stronger spatial specificity with reduced weight spread on the cortical surface compared to the 395 

anatomical alignment. Overall, all methods appear to follow the initial topography of the anatomical 396 

average, but they differ in how information is distributed across voxels, which underlie the magnitude of 397 

the classifier weights. 398 

Moving beyond this specific, qualitative observation, we provide a quantitative analysis of the sparsity 399 

across activity maps from different tasks. We compute the ratio between the L2 and L1 norm for each 400 

aligned map and summarize the values in histograms relative to each method. These results are shown 401

We observe that both Procrustes and to a lesser extent Ridge regression exhibit higher ratios, i.e. activity 403

# 4 Discussion 407 

Let us start by highlighting a few key results of our analysis. We observe a clear performance gap between 408 

Optimal Transport and the rest of the methods which often struggle to surpass the anatomical alignment 409 

baseline. This gap can be reduced for Procrustes analysis and Ridge regression by using Pairwise or 410 

in-sample alignment. However, the Pairwise strategy comes at the penalty of providing no template, and 411 

a higher computational cost than the in-sample strategy. Furthermore, the in-sample strategy should be 412 

ruled out for Procrustes, Ridge and SRM, given the significant bias related to the inclusion of the test 413 

subject when generating the template. The much smaller bias makes the in-sample strategy acceptable 414 

for OT. Our examination of the computed templates revealed that while OT, Procrustes and Ridge 415 

follow the topography of the anatomical baseline, they exhibit contrasted activity profile, with OT better 416 

preserving the anatomical signal distribution, while Procrustes and Ridge tend to nullify larger portions 417 

of the activity on the cortex. 418 

4.1 A Cross-Validated Framework 419 

In this work, we focus on functional alignment strategies for task decoding. Our alignment/decoding 420 

framework is designed to minimize potential sources of variability beyond inter-subject differences. Other 421

et al., 2024) or external tasks (Bazeille et al., 2021), which raises two concerns. 423 

First, discrepancies between alignment and decoding data arise from differences in stimulus type (e.g., 424 

movies, resting-state, tasks) and acquisition sessions. This makes it hard to disentangle the effects of 425 

inter-subject alignment from other sources of variability on decoding performance. 426 

Second, these approaches have reduced practical applicability, as they require neuroscientists to collect 427 

additional data specifically for alignment, which is expensive. Given the cost of fMRI data collection, 428 

our 5-fold run-stratified approach makes functional alignment more accessible, as it operates on existing 429 

data without additional acquisition costs, assuming that the main task is large enough to support cross-430 

validation splits (which is typically the case in studies designed for decoding analysis). 431 

We also gathered a diverse set of tasks differing in nature, size, and origin. Although hyperalignment 432 

has been successfully applied to various movie-viewing datasets (Andreella & Finos, 2022; Haxby et al., 433 

2020), running alignment and decoding in naturalistic setting is relevant only when the amount of data 434 

is large (Thual et al., 2023). In our framework, we use a collection of various tasks and datasets with 435 

limited amount of subjects and target a comprehensive evaluation of all possible alignment methods and 436 

strategies. 437 

4.2 Optimal Transport Shows Superior Alignment Performance 438 

Our findings indicate that Optimal Transport alignment achieves the highest decoding performance across 439 

all evaluated strategies. Furthermore, at the individual-subject level, OT consistently improves decoding 440 

accuracy relative to the anatomical baseline. In contrast, other methods often perform worse than the 441 

baseline, thereby defeating the initial purpose of functional alignment. 442 

Our experiments also reveal a minimal gap between in-sample and out-of-sample performance for OT, 443 

suggesting little-to-no bias when computing a template at the population level, especially as the number 444 

of subjects becomes larger. Specifically, this demonstrates the robustness of the OT templates to the 445 

inclusion of a single new subject, and it empirically validates the use of the faster in-sample template 446 

strategy with OT, whereas other methods suffer from inflated results due to the inclusion bias. 447 

The possibility of using the in-sample template strategy also facilitates analysis since it yields a unique 448 

common space shared by all subjects, making the application of group-level statistical frameworks 449 

straightforward. In particular, our analysis of the obtained templates shows that OT is closer to the 450 

anatomical template than other methods. 451

24 4 DISCUSSION 

The robust and conservative nature of the OT template stems from the stringent constraints of the 452 

transport plans (such as mass preservation), which results in strong sparsity, i.e, only a small number of 453 

voxels contribute to the prediction of another one, and in positive coefficients in the alignment matrices 454 

(because transport plans encode probability couplings). In contrast to Procrustes rotations or Ridge 455 

regression, alignments derived from transport plans preserve small clusters of activity, maintaining the 456 

signal’s spatial specificity which drives downstream decoding performance. 457 

Finally, the computational efficiency of the in-sample template OT strategy should be underlined, as it is 458 

consistently faster than its pairwise and out-of-sample counterparts, making it highly relevant in practice. 459 

4.3 Limitations 460 

Although we sought to encompass several methods and strategies while systematically cross-validating 461 

our results to ensure robustness and generalization, several limitations constrain our framework. 462 

We use the Schaefer atlas (Schaefer et al., 2017), following Jeganathan et al., 2024. Control analyses 463 

indicate that the number of parcels does not influence our results. Future work could explore alternative 464 

atlases or, as suggested by Bazeille et al., 2021, leverage alignment-derived parcellations rather than 465 

predefined atlases. Surface-based decoding (rather than the volume-based approach used here) could 466 

further improve the correspondence between subjects and reduce mismatches. Our framework, fmralign ,467 

does not currently support searchlight hyperalignment as in Guntupalli et al., 2016. 468 

Additionally, our approach could be extended to whole-brain alignment techniques that do not rely on 469 

local parcels, such as the Promises model Andreella and Finos, 2022 or Fused Unbalanced Gromov-470 

Wasserstein Thual et al., 2022, which incorporate anatomical constraints. However, the choice and 471 

tuning of spatial priors in the former framework remain unclear in comparison to parcellated methods, 472 

and the latter is computationally prohibitive in the template setting. 473 

Finally, the implementation of pairwise approaches could be accelerated by caching invertible transfor-474 

mations, achieving complexity of S(S − 1) /2 in the number of subjects. However, we did not implement 475 

this strategy because it is not general (e.g. Ridge transformations are not invertible). This optimization 476 

could substantially benefit OT and Procrustes. Furthermore, we relied on a CPU implementation of the 477 

Sinkhorn algorithm for OT; performance could be dramatically improved using GPU acceleration (Cuturi, 478 

2013). 479
