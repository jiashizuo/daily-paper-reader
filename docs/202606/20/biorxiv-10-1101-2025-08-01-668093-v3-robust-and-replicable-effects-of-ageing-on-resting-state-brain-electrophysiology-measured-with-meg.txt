# Abstract 

Non-invasive recordings using brain electrophysiology provide credible insights into decline in neuronal functioning with age. New approaches are required to translate these results into compelling clinical metrics and meet the global challenge of preserving brain health in ageing. Changes in neuronal dynamics with ageing are observable in the power spectra of EEG and MEG recordings. Highly promising candidates for electrophysiological markers have been identified, but progress is hindered by substantial methodological variability across studies. This makes it challenging to establish a clear consensus on the frequency, location, and direction of any single reported effect within the larger body of research. We estimate a full-frequency whole-head profile of the ageing effect on eyes-closed resting-state MEG using the GLM-Spectrum. This data representation is easily sharable, facilitates meta-analyses and provides a framework for estimating statistical power of age effects for future study planning. We use this to show that the effect of age replicates across open-access MEG datasets and is robust to modelling of common covariates. Distinct components within the full frequency profile have different effect sizes, indicating that sample-size planning for ageing effects must consider the specific features of interest. The frequency profile of ageing is strongly robust to a range of common covariates and partially robust to modelling of grey matter volume. We establish that what seems a well-powered study may become underpowered when analyses target an age effect that is linearly separable from an age-relevant covariate such as grey matter volume. These results provide a pathway towards formal comparison and assessment of candidate markers for brain health in ageing.

# Highlights 

• There is a strong effect of ageing across the adult lifespan on neuronal oscillations across a range of frequencies and spatial locations.

Ageing is a major risk factor for a wide range of major pathologies of the brain [L´ opez-Ot´ ın 2

et al., 2013] including neurodegenerative disorders [Planche et al., 2022] and stroke [Ellekjær 3

et al., 1997, Feigin et al., 2014]. Changes in human brain electrophysiology are observable 4

through changes in the power spectrum of electromagnetic fields measured by EEG and 5

MEG. These approaches offer a direct perspective on temporal synchronization of neuronal 6

activity [Babiloni et al., 2020]. This synchronization depends on the integrity of network 7

connections and is known to degrade throughout the ageing process [Maest´ u and Fern´ andez, 8

2020]. Such effects have been observed in the neuronal power spectrum of both MEG 9

[Brady et al., 2020, G´ omez et al., 2013, Hoshi and Shigihara, 2020, Rempe et al., 2023, 10 

Stier et al., 2023, Osipova et al., 2005, Hughes et al., 2019] and EEG recordings [Klimesch, 11 

1999, Park et al., 2024, Turner et al., 2023, Zibrandtsen and Kjaer, 2021]. Non-invasive 12 

measures of brain electrophysiology are a highly promising approach to contribute toward 13 

the global aim of allowing more people to realise their potential across the full lifespan 14 

[World Health Organization, 2015]. 15 

Electrophysiological signatures of ageing across studies in the literature are diverse, or 16 

even conflicting. It can be challenging to reconcile differences across differing analysis 17 

choices, frequency bands, and brain regions [Donoghue et al., 2021]. For example, increasing 18 

age has a longstanding association with a reduction in posterior alpha band (7-13Hz) power 19 

[Klimesch, 1999]. Yet, the broader literature reveals that this effect varies in magnitude, and 20 

may even reverse direction, across brain regions, frequency bands, and analysis pipelines 21 

[G´ omez et al., 2013, Hoshi and Shigihara, 2020, Lodder and van Putten, 2011, Park et al., 22 

2024, Pathak et al., 2022, Quinn et al., 2024, Rempe et al., 2023, Scally et al., 2018, 23 

Zibrandtsen and Kjaer, 2021]. Credible scientific claims must be supported by evidence of 24 

replicability in new datasets and robustness to reasonable variations in analysis 25 

[Botvinik-Nezer and Wager, 2023, Nosek and Errington, 2020, Nosek et al., 2022], but our 26 

ability to integrate results across studies is hindered by the large variety of analytic choices 27 

used in the literature. This makes it difficult to synthesize findings from multiple datasets, 28 

either as a manual comparison or a formal meta-analysis. 29 

Neuroscience researchers working on MRI facilitate comparability and meta-analyses by 30 

computing and distributing whole-volume statistical maps along with results of individual 31 

studies [Gorgolewski et al., 2015]. A similar approach is possible for spectral analysis of 32 

MEG/EEG, e.g. computing and sharing full frequency and whole-head estimates of critical 33 

effects without binning results into frequency bands or regions of interest. Features such as 34 

canonical frequency bands may be a useful guide for interpretation of results, but they can 35 

oversimplify the profile of the underlying spectrum and add a source of variability between 36 

studies that can prevent aggregation of results. Reporting of whole-head and full-frequency 37 

spectra of effect estimates would make it straightforward to aggregate across studies and 38 

eventually enable identification of sub-threshold effects that may be missed in single 39 

analyses but are consistent across studies. We argue that this approach provides a 40 

generalisable foundation that can support more complex analyses that have a frequency 41 

component (such as aperiodic slopes, burst detection, and dynamic functional networks) 42 

that require more researcher degrees of freedom. 43 

Effect sizes are another essential tool for comparing and aggregating results across 44 

studies. They quantify the magnitude, or practical significance, of an estimated effect 45 

without reducing it to a hypothesis test with a binary outcome that might be dependent on 46 

practical factors such as sample size [Smith and Nichols, 2018]. Critically, effect sizes allow 47 

efficient use of data by supporting planning for future data samples through power analyses. 48 

Statistically underpowered studies are more likely to lead to incorrect conclusions and 49 

inaccurate estimates of effect sizes when all other aspects of experimental design and 50 

analysis have been conducted perfectly [Button et al., 2013, Cremers et al., 2017]. This is 51 

critical for neuroimaging analyses where different spatial, temporal or spectral components 52

## 2.1 A whole-head and full-frequency depiction of age-related change 77 

## to the neuronal power spectrum 78 

MEG data from Cam-CAN [Shafto et al., 2020] for 557 participants during eyes-closed 79 

resting-state were analysed. After data pre-processing and cleaning, a two-level 80 

GLM-Spectrum [Quinn et al., 2024] approach was used to characterise the average 81 

magnitude and the linear slope effect of age across the spectrum for each sensor. Figure 1A 82 

uses the estimated GLM-Spectrum coefficients to show the model prediction of how the 83 

spectrum changes across age. This shows a characteristic pattern that combines a variety of 84 

previously reported results into a single analysis. Importantly, these effects are continuous 85 

across the spectrum and would be challenging to objectively unpick without A full-spectrum 86 

approach. 87 

The t-statistic spectrum (Figure 1B) is the test statistic for the linear effect of age at 88 

each point in frequency for each sensor. A positive t-value indicates that power in that 89 

frequency bin at that sensor position increases with age, and vice versa for negative t-values. 90 

The statistical significance of the t-statistic spectrum of the age-effect (i.e., rejecting the 91 

hypothesis that there is no age effect) was computed using non-parametric permutations 92 

with maximum statistics to control for multiple comparisons across sensors and frequency 93 

bins. While this permutation testing was not cluster-based, contiguous clusters of significant 94 

sensors were computed post-hoc for visualisation. Figure 1B shows the t-value spectrum of 95 

the age with the largest post-hoc clusters overlaid and Figure 1C shows the effect organised 96 

into a 2D image across space (anterior to posterior) and frequency as a statistical 97 

parametric map [Litvak et al., 2011]. The 2D image representation simplifies visualisation as 98 

it does not rely on colour-coding of sensor location. Critically, both the t-statistic spectrum 99

We have completed several control analyses to support these findings. Firstly, we have 119 

explored the qualitative correspondence between alpha peak frequency and the two effects 120 

we identified within the canonical alpha range (see supplemental section A.1). Secondly, the 121 

overall pattern of findings is consistent in an equivalent source space analysis using LCMV 122 

beamforming and parcellation (see supplemental section A.2). Finally, we have repeated the 123 

analyses with and without ICA denoising and find that the overall spectral profile is very 124 

similar. Effects in low-frequencies, low-alpha and high-gamma are increased with application 125 

of ICA, whilst high-alpha and beta remain unchanged, and low-gamma effects are reduced 126 

(see supplemental section A.3). 127 

## 2.2 Effect size of age is variable across space and frequency, with 128 

## consequences for sample size planning 129 

Neuroimaging analyses, such as the results in Figure 1, typically focus on testing hypotheses 130 

about the presence of effects in brain imaging data, not estimating their magnitude. These 131 

hypothesis tests are sample-size dependent, so that practically insignificant effects may 132 

become significant in sufficiently large datasets. Effect sizes estimate the strength of a 133 

phenomenon or relationship, independently of sample size. They better reflect an effect’s 134 

importance in a clinical or behavioural context [Reddan et al., 2017] and can be used as a 135 

basis for power analysis (i.e. sample size planning) to inform future research. In this analysis, 136 

the effect size of the age regressor within the GLM-Spectrum was computed using Cohen’s 137 

F 2 [Cohen, 1988, Selya et al., 2012] for each sensor and frequency bin in the analysis. 138 

Cohen’s F 2 corresponds to the proportion of variance in the data that is uniquely explained 139 

by an explanatory variable (i.e., regressor) of interest within the context of a multiple 140 

regression model. 141 

The spectrum of effect sizes for the age regressor (Figure 3A) indicates the strength or 142 

practical impact of ageing on the spectrum. The effect size spectrum contains local peaks 143 

that correspond to the effects highlighted in section 2.1. The largest effects with local peaks 144 

at 5Hz, 8Hz, 10.5Hz, 15Hz, 36Hz, and 60 Hz have close spatial and spectral correspondence 145 

to extrema in the t-spectrum in Figure 1B. Low frequency, upper alpha and beta range 146 

results show effect sizes peaking between F 2 = 0.2 to F 2 = 0.3, indicating that ageing had 147 

a relatively large practical impact on the spectrum in these bands. In contrast, though they 148 

achieved statistical significance in the hypothesis tests (Figure 1), lower alpha and the 149

## 2.3 Quadratic effects of age 162 

The linear effect of age is a convenient and simple regression model. However it makes a 163 

strong assumption that change with age is uniform across the whole age range. A second 164 

group-level GLM was computed with an additional regressor to quantify quadratic effects of 165 

age, which have been reported in the ageing literature [G´ omez et al., 2013, Rempe et al., 166 

2023, Stier et al., 2023]. The spectrum of t-values for the quadratic age predictor (Figure 3) 167 

shows significant effects for a U-shaped change with increasing age in the low frequency 168 

(1-5Hz) range in central sensors. Significant inverted-U shaped effects are present in two 169 

frequency ranges in the beta band. A low-frequency beta effect is present in occipital and 170 

temporal sensors between 16Hz and 20Hz, whilst a second high-beta effect is present in 171 

central sensors between 24Hz and 30.5Hz. The low-frequency and low-beta effects overlap 172 

in space and frequency with linear effects, suggesting that the low-frequency change with 173 

age has both a linear increase with age and a U-shaped component, whilst the low-beta 174 

effect has a decrease with age and an inverted-U shaped component. The high-beta effect 175 

does not overlap in frequency with any of the reported linear effects. The effect sizes for 176 

quadratic effects range between Cohen’s F 2 values of 0.033 for low-frequency to 0.076 for 177 

high-beta and are generally lower than the effect sizes for linear change. 178 

## 2.4 Sample size planning for effects of age on the neuronal power 179 

## spectrum 180 

We use the 95% confidence intervals around the effect sizes to make recommendations for 181 

future sample sizes for future samples that plan to replicate these results. The observed 182 

power calculations have no bearing on the interpretation of the present results. Instead, they 183 

should be used as a general guideline for planning future studies. Table 1 gives a full 184 

summary of the peak statistics and future sample size range for the six age effects identified 185 

in Figure 1B. These results have implications for future sample planning for resting-state 186 

electrophysiology studies of ageing. Using the upper bound of the sample size estimates as a 187 

conservative estimate, the linear changes with age that have relatively large effect sizes 188 

would have well powered replications with sample sizes of around 50-60 participants. 189 

However, the smaller linear effects and all the quadratic effects would require samples of 200 190 

or more participants to have the same probability of detecting the effect if it is indeed 191 

present (Table 1). This indicates that study samples should be planned with the smallest 192 

effect of interest in mind and that ageing effects in different frequency bands may not all be 193 

well powered within the same sample. 194

## 2.5 The spectral profile of age effects on brain electrophysiology is 195 

## replicable across datasets. 196 

To explore the replicability of the ageing effect reported from the Cam-CAN dataset, the 197 

analysis was repeated on eyes-closed resting-state recordings from three smaller datasets 198 

from the MEG-UK database (https://meguk.ac.uk/database/). Details on the similarities 199 

and differences between the datasets are summarised in Table 2. Replicability was quantified 200 

by the correlation of the whole spectral profile of the age effect between datasets. We have 201 

only completed the replication analysis with the linear effects of age as the quadratic effects 202 

from Cam-CAN have smaller effect sizes that indicate that the MEG-UKI datasets may not 203 

be able to detect these effects based on their sample size. 204 

Overall, the spectral profile of the ageing effect is highly replicable across MEG datasets 205 

acquired on different systems and across different facilities. We identified high correlations 206 

between the spectral profiles of the age parameter estimates, inferential statistics, and effect 207 

sizes in each of the four datasets (Figure 4). In particular, the spectral profiles for the 208 

parameter estimates and t-statistics were strikingly similar across datasets (Pearson’s R 209 

between 0.67 and 0.97 for parameter estimates and between 0.76 and 0.92 for t-statistics), 210 

suggesting a high level of replicability. The spectral profile of effect sizes was also positively 211 

correlated across datasets but to a lesser extent (Pearson’s R between 0.53 and 0.84). 212 

Maximum and minimum t-statistics for Cam-CAN are substantially larger in magnitude than 213 

in the smaller MEG-UK datasets. This is an expected consequence of the larger sample size 214 

of Cam-CAN on null-hypothesis test statistics such as t-values. 215 

The topography of age-related change is qualitatively reproduced across all datasets 216 

(Figure 5A) with some variability in the extent and spread of effects in specific datasets. 217 

Whilst the lower frequency effects are highly consistent, there is more variability in the 218 

higher frequency effects ( > 30 Hz), which can change direction across participants 219 

(Figure 5B, C D). The smaller data samples have more variable estimates for Cohen’s F 2 220 

when compared to Cam-CAN. Whilst the interpretation of effect sizes doesn’t rely on 221

## 2.6 The robustness of the age effect to head position correction 234 

The results so far have looked at age effects on the relative spectral magnitude of each 235 

frequency compared to the sum across frequencies. Whilst the absolute spectral magnitude 236 

is simpler to interpret in terms of underlying neuronal activity it can be confounded by the 237 

position of the participant relative to the MEG sensors (some sensors will be closer to 238 

neuronal source than others), and by inter-individual variability in this position (Gross et al., 239 

2013). It is common to apply a correction or normalisation to sensor-space analyses to 240 

control for these effects, though this is an impactful decision which can change the profile of 241 

effects seen in the results. 242 

The age effect on the absolute spectrum (Figure 6) presents a different pattern of results 243 

compared to the relative spectrum (Figure 1 & 3). Briefly, the beta effect remains preserved 244 

or even enhanced compared to the relative magnitude effect, whilst the alpha effects are 245 

attenuated, and the low-frequency effect is decreased or removed altogether (Figure 6A, B). 246 

There is a reduced spatial specificity in the age effects of absolute power. The largest 247 

post-hoc cluster of contiguous statistically significance for absolute power covers a broad 248 

range of frontal and temporal sensors and stretches the whole 1-95-Hz range of the 249 

spectrum (Figure 6C) whilst the next four largest clusters have relatively homogenous 250 

spatial patterns (Figure 6C). Spatial maps are positively correlated for all pairs of 251 

frequencies (Figure 6B), indicating that absolute scaling from head position might be 252 

making a large contribution to the overall effect. 253 

A related decision involves rescaling data recordings to correct for differences in head 254 

position within (movement compensation) and between (transformation to reference 255 

position) data recordings. This is commonly applied using Signal Space Separation (SSS) 256 

with the maxfilter software [Taulu and Kajola, 2005, Taulu and Simola, 2006]. We 257 

additionally compared the age effect across absolute and relative spectral magnitude with 258 

three variants of SSS head-position correction (None, movement compensation, and 259 

movement compensation plus transformation to reference). The spectral profile of the 260 

absolute effect (without relative scaling) is relatively consistent across the three variants in 261 

SSS head position correction with the application of between recording transformation to 262 

reference head position creating the only noticeable difference (Figure Supplemental 17A, C 263 

& E). In contrast, the relative spectrum has the familiar spectral and spatial profile from 264 

Figure 1 and is unaffected by SSS head position correction (Figure Supplemental 17B, D & 265 

F). There is substantially greater spectral specificity in spatial profile of the age effect in 266 

relative power, indicated by greater variability in spatial correlations across frequency (Figure 267 

Supplemental 17G & H). 268

## 2.8 Isolating the component of the age effect on brain 305 

## electrophysiology that is distinct from global reduction in grey 306 

## matter volume. 307 

Global grey matter volume (GGMV) had the strongest mediating effect on the impact of 308 

ageing on the MEG spectrum from all the alternative covariates (Figure 7). Whilst there is 309 

overlap between age and GGMV in their effect on the MEG spectrum, each has a unique 310 

contribution that is distinct from the other. Modelling GGMV alongside age reduced the 311 

association between age and neuronal power spectra heterogeneously across different 312 

frequency bands and did not reduce the age effect to zero (Figure 8A and B). 313 

It is important to note that the correlation between age and GGMV does impact the 314 

interpretation of the GLM results, but does not prevent the model fit. We explore the model 315 

validation and diagnostics in detail in Supplemental section A.6. In brief, the model is able 316 

to separate the unique contribution of age and GGMV. However, the correlation between 317 

factors leads to an inflation in the standard error of the estimates. A hypothesis test on 318 

these estimates is valid though the inflated variance increases type-2 error by reducing our 319 

ability to detect partial effects in the data when they are truly present. 320

# 3 Discussion 339 

A whole-head and full-frequency profile of the ageing effect is characterised and shown to be 340 

replicable across datasets. Six distinct ageing effects are identified, and the combination of 341 

linear parameters across the spectrum can represent more complex features such as alpha 342 

peak frequency. The corresponding effect sizes demonstrate that age impacts the spectrum 343 

heterogeneously and that the feature of interest must be considered when sample size 344 

planning for future experiments. This effect is robust to head position correction, but the 345 

spatial profile changes substantially between relative and absolute spectral power. Finally, 346 

the age effect is differentially modified by different factors, such as grey matter volume, brain 347 

volume, head size, and physiological factors. Individual variability in grey matter volume 348 

mediates the association between age and neuronal power spectra, leading to a reduction in 349 

the estimated effect size of age across the whole spectrum. Critically, the impact of 350 

including a grey matter volume covariate differentially impacts different brain regions and 351 

frequency bands, indicating that some regions have a strong and unique signature of ageing 352 

that reflects functional/synaptic changes over and above grey matter atrophy. 353 

Overall, by analysing four independent datasets including 600 participants, we 354 

demonstrated that there is a replicable and robust effect of ageing on MEG power spectrum 355 

across the whole-head and a range of frequencies. These effects are well-powered at 356 

moderate data samples, replicable across different datasets and scanner types, and are 357 

reasonably robust to modelling additional anatomical, physiological, or acquisition factors as 358 

covariates. 359 

## 3.1 Full frequency spectra of age effects synthesise diverse results 360 

## across the literature. 361 

The profile of results across the spectrum of age effects replicates and simplifies a range of 362 

results across previous literature. This is done without pre-specification of frequency bands 363 

or sensors of interest and in a manner that is conducive to future study planning and 364 

meta-analyses. Here, we briefly review the core age effects from this analysis in relation to a 365 

representative sample of published results on the effects of healthy ageing on the resting- 366 

state EEG and MEG spectrum. 367 

Low-frequency power decreases with age . This result is broadly consistent with 368 

literature demonstrating that ’theta’ power decreases with healthy ageing [Beese et al., 369 

2017, Cummins and Finnigan, 2007, Rempe et al., 2023, Vlahou et al., 2014] and may be 370 

associated with decreases in cognitive performance [Finnigan and Robertson, 2011]. One 371 

critical difference with this literature is that the full-frequency age profile identifies a single 372 

’low frequency’ age -effect between 1-7 Hz and does not distinguish between the canonical 373 

delta (2-4 Hz) and theta (4-7 Hz) oscillations. A further difference is that we only see the 374 

low- frequency decrease in power in the relative spectrum (Figure 1). Computing the age 375 

effect from the absolute spectrum resulted in no changes across age at low frequencies 376 

(Figure 6). 377 

A combination of age-related decreases in lower frequencies with increases in higher 378 

frequencies (notably the beta range) contributes to a flattening of the aperiodic component 379 

of the power spectrum [Aggarwal and Ray, 2023, Cesnaite et al., 2023, Dave et al., 2018, 380 

Voytek et al., 2015]. This flattening is proposed to reflect an age-related increase in neural 381 

noise affecting age-related working memory performance [Voytek et al., 2015]. Future 382 

research accounting for flattening of the 1/f-type component in the full frequency spectrum 383 

approach can better separate whether the low frequency changes relate to oscillations or the 384 

aperiodic component of the spectrum. 385

Relationship between effects on alpha power and alpha individual frequency . 411

## 3.2 Observed power analysis for future study planning 464 

The observed power and sample size calculations in this paper should not be used to help 465 

interpret the present findings, or as a basis to speculate about what sample size would be 466 

required for a non-significant result to pass the significance threshold. Rather they are 467 

intended solely to guide decisions about the sample sizes of future studies in an accessible 468 

manner and to highlight the potential for this approach in analyses of neuronal power 469 

spectra. 470 

We concur with Lenth [2001] who highlight that ”Sample size planning is often 471 

important, and is nearly always difficult”. Most recommendations about sample size 472 

planning indicate that a priori or theoretically informed effect sizes should be used as 473 

observed effect sizes are influenced by sampling error [Hoenig and Heisey, 2001]. Single 474 

estimates of observed effect sizes can be extremely noisy and will not necessarily reflect the 475 

’true’ underlying population effect. Moreover, using the single observed estimate simply 476 

recapitulates the information already present in the p-value of the test [Hoenig and Heisey, 477 

2001]. 478 

As a result, we emphasise the bootstrapped 95% confidence intervals of the effect sizes 479 

to give a sense of the uncertainty around our estimates. The upper and lower bound of 480 

these confidence intervals are used in the observed power analysis as an example to guide 481 

future study planning. Until such a time when the field reaches consensus about what the 482 

smallest effect size of interest would be for electrophysiological power spectra, we propose 483 

that researchers can use the lower bound of the 95% confidence interval as the smallest 484 

effect size of interest for a replication. Working with multiple covariates and a requirement 485 

to correct for multiple comparisons both decrease the statistical power of a neuroimaging 486 

dataset [Cremers et al., 2017] so it is critical to be transparent about which effects are 487 

observable in our data. Confidence interval based sample projections from observed effect 488 

sizes must be handled with care but are a useful tool in a field where sample size plans are 489 

frequently based on resource constraints or heuristics. Projections can be supported with 490

We propose the following steps as a practical guide for researchers looking to plan a data 495

1. Define research question and identify previous results: Your research question 497 

must be defined in advance and well specified, there should be relevant data or 498 

literature that can be used to guide your decision. 499 

2. Define the smallest effect size of interest, and the decision criterion for the 500 

sample decision: It is critical to define the parameters of how you will make your 501 

sample size decision ahead of time. We recommend considering what the ’smallest 502 

effect size of interest’ [Anvari and Lakens, 2021] would be for your question. This is 503 

specific to your question and is about more than statistical significance. What effect 504 

size would indicate that there is a practically meaningful effect to for future literature 505 

to consider? 506 

3. Estimate effect sizes to inform your decision: Either by aggregating reported 507 

statistics from the literature, or by dedicated processing of previous data, compute an 508 

estimate of the effect size. Effect sizes are only estimates so it is important to 509 

compute confidence intervals around your estimate to get a measure of variability. 510 

4. Compute power/precision curves assuming these results: Using the estimated 511 

effect sizes, compute power curves [Baker et al., 2021] that visualise the relationship 512 

between effect size, statistical power and sample size. 513 

5. Select the sample size that meets your pre-defined criteria: Using your 514 

definitions from step 2, and when considering the whole power curve, make a decision 515 

about what sample size would give you a reasonable chance of replicating the effect of 516

6. Make note of any differences or deviations from this plan during your data 518 

collection: There are many practical reasons why your planned sample might not 519 

match the data acquired in practice. Such deviations from a plan are ok, but should 520 

be acknowledged and any mitigating steps explained [Lakens, 2024]. 521 

7. Document your process for inclusion in a preregistration or publication: Include 522 

details on how a sample size decision was made in your research outputs including 523 

preregistrations, preprints, and publications. These details will be useful for future 524 

researchers to understand your process and to implement their own. 525 

## 3.3 Limitations 526 

Our approach promotes an exploratory and unbiased approach to quantifying the age effect 527 

on neuronal power spectra which is intended to complement more focused analyses. This 528 

has the benefit of reducing researcher’s degrees of freedom and of being broadly 529 

generalisable. These come at the cost of a loss in specificity and in sensitivity. Our approach 530 

does not specifically quantify features derived from the power spectrum such as alpha-peak 531 

frequency or the aperiodic component of the spectrum. These features are mixed into our 532 

full spectrum estimates but not directly quantified. Thus they can be challenging to 533 

interpret from our approach. Secondly, the mass-univariate approach suffers from a potential 534 

loss in sensitivity compared to results that aggregate across spatial or spectral regions that 535 

contain consistent results. Where a region or frequency band of interest can be supported 536

## 3.5 Future extensions 592 

A clear future extension for this work is to formally incorporate estimates of within-subject 593 

variability into a mixed-effect model. These powerful models would enable modelling of both 594 

fixed effects and random effects, allowing researchers to account for variation within 595 

individuals over time and between individuals. Linear Mixed Models also provide improved 596 

approaches for handling missing observations and unbalanced designs, making them 597 

especially useful for longitudinal and hierarchical data . A second expansion of this work 598 

could use Generalised Additive Mixed Models (GAMMs) to model non-linear relationships 599 

using smooth functions while also accounting for random effects. This may allow for more 600 

realistic representations of complex patterns of change across age. Linear Mixed Modelling 601 

comes with a substantial increase in researcher degrees of freedom and can be challenging to 602 

implement and report accurately [Meteyard and Davies, 2020]. We have used fixed effects 603 

modelling in this work in line with our objectives of maintaining generalisability and 604 

minimising researcher degrees of freedom. 605 

# Conclusion 606 

Overall, we have demonstrated that ageing effects on MEG power spectra are robust and 607 

replicable across multiple datasets. Different brain regions and frequency bands respond 608 

differently to ageing, with some having a strong unique signature of ageing that is 609 

dissociable from global anatomical changes. This is a necessary step towards adoption of 610 

electrophysiological measures in a healthcare setting. 611

## 4.2 MEG Data Preprocessing 623 

All MEG data pre-processing was carried out using MNE-Python [Gramfort, 2013] and 624 

OSL-ephys [Quinn et al., 2022, van Es et al., 2025] using the OSL batch pre-processing 625 

tools. For the Cam-CAN data, we proceeded with analysis on post-maxfilter processed data 626 

provided by the Cam-CAN team. Briefly, the data were processed using AA [Cusack et al., 627 

2015] with automatic bad channel detection (limited to 7 channels) with the origin set to 628 

the centre of a sphere fitted to the individual’s Polhemus headshape points. Maxfilter 629 

signal-space separation was performed with the temporal extension enabled (temporal 630 

window of 10 second and correlation threshold of r = 0 .98 ). Head position was continuously 631 

estimated and compensated for during periods where the HPI coils were on. After maxfilter 632 

processing head positions were translated into a default head position defined as a point 633 

relative to each individual’s origin in a head coordinate frame. Data with the full maxfilter 634 

processing and with the head position translation or movement compensation were extracted 635 

from the Cam-CAN database. An equivalent pipeline was implemented in OSL-Ephys and 636 

applied to the data from the MEG-UK datasets from Oxford and Cambridge. Files from the 637 

MEG-UK Nottingham dataset were processed with third-order gradiometry applied. 638 

The data from each recording was first converted from its raw format into MNE-Python 639 

Raw data objects. The first 35 seconds of continuous data were cropped out, and the 640 

remaining data were bandpass filtered between 0.25 and 150 Hz using an order-5 641 

Butterworth filter. 50-Hz line noise was suppressed using the ZapLine method [de Cheveign´ e, 642 

2020] as implemented in the MEEGKit toolbox. Two passes of ZapLine were applied to 643 

account for minor peaks around the 50 Hz artefact. One pass was centred on 49 Hz and the 644 

second on 50 Hz. Bad segments were identified by segmenting data into 2-second chunks 645 

and using the generalised-extreme studentized deviate [Rosner, 1983] algorithm to identify 646 

outlier (bad) samples with high variance across channels. Bad segment detection was 647 

applied to both the raw time series and the differential of the time series and separately to 648 

the Magnetometers and Gradiometers. An average of 2.82% of data samples were marked 649 

as ’bad’ (standard deviation: 2.54%, min: 0.0%, max: 15.52%) equivalent to an average of 650 

15.04 seconds per recording. Bad channels were then identified using the G-ESD routine to 651 

identify outliers in the distribution of variance per channel over time. The data were then 652 

resampled to 250 Hz to reduce space on-disk and ease subsequent computations. 653

correlation with the simultaneous EOG and ECG channels. ECG artefacts were identified 658

on the sample rate of the data as implemented in the 660

## 4.5 GLM-Spectrum - First level 719 

A GLM-Spectrum [Quinn et al., 2024] was used to provide a statistical estimate of the MEG 720 

spectrum in the presence of covariates and confounds. A Short-Time Fourier Transform 721 

(STFT) was computed from the preprocessed sensor time series from each dataset using a 722 

2-second segment length, a 1-second overlap between segments, and a Hanning taper. The 723 

2-second segment length at the sample rate of 250 Hz gives a resolution of around 2 724 

frequency bins per unit Hertz in the resulting spectrum. The short-time magnitude spectrum 725 

is computed from the complex-valued STFT and the frequency bins ranging between 0.1 726 

and 100 Hz taken forward as the dependent variable in the first-level GLM-Spectrum for 727 

that dataset. 728 

The GLM design matrix for each dataset is specified with five regressors. A single 729 

constant regressor models the intercept of the data, whilst two zero-mean parametric 730 

regressors model the effect of changing heart rate and a linear trend in time throughout the 731 

recording. The fourth and fifth regressors were non-zero mean parametric regressors 732 

modelling the effect of variance in the V-EOG channel and bad segments. These final 733 

regressors acted as confound variables that removed variance that can be linearly explained 734 

by the artefact sources from the mean term. 735 

A set of simple contrasts isolated the main effect of each of the five regressors. The 736 

first-level GLM-Spectrum model parameters were estimated using the Moore-Penrose 737 

Pseudo-Inverse [Penrose, 1956] to compute beta-spectra. Finally, the beta-spectra were 738 

weighted by the contrasts to compute cope-spectra which were carried forward to the 739 

group-level analyses. 740

term, and one parametric regressor containing z-standardised participant age. The input 743

## A.1 Literature review of alpha effects 852 

Part of our motivation for this method is that variability in the methodological choices made 853 

in different publications makes it difficult to aggregate varying results across the literature. 854 

For example, though decrease in alpha peak frequency with increasing age is reported highly 855 

consistently, the effect of age on alpha power is much more variable. Table 3 shows a 856 

sample of publications over the last 20 years that report a change in alpha power with age 857 

(note that this is intended to be a representative rather than an exhaustive list). Over half 858 

of publications (9/15) report a decrease in alpha power whilst the remaining publications 859 

report an increase (2/15), both increases and decreases (1/15), a decrease but only without 860 

correcting for aperiodic slope (1/15), a quadratic effect (1/15) and no-effect (1/15). Stier 861 

et al. [2023] suggest that the choice of analysis space (sensor space or source reconstruction) 862 

might drive these differences. 863 

Critically, it is difficult to reconcile these findings with the information reported in the 864 

publications. For example, even within the 9 publications that report a decrease, there is 865 

little correspondence in the spatial location of the effect. We argue that focused approaches 866 

cannot resolve this issue alone as targeted analyses are more specific to each dataset and 867 

less generalisable. 868 

In the specific case of the mixed literature on change in alpha power with age, our results 869

## A.3 Effect of ICA denoising on the age effect 890 

The age results may be contaminated in some way by residual cardiac or ocular artefacts 891 

that are not removed during preprocessing. Though ICA denoising was applied, it is possible 892 

that some artefactual components were not identified and removed from the dataset. The 893 

results at low frequencies and in the gamma range are most likely to be directly impacted by 894 

this contamination. 895 

To explore the impact this has on our analysis, we reran the core GLM effect of age on 896 

the data with no ICA artefact rejection at all, allowing all eye movements and heart rate 897 

components to remain in the data (Figure 11). 898 

This no-ICA analysis has three differences to the original in the publication: The 899 

low-frequency decrease with age is stronger and more widespread in the analysis that 900 

removes ocular artefacts with ICA. A large negative effect is visible in both analyses, though 901 

without ICA several frontal and temporal sensors no longer show significant effects. 902 

Similarly, the effect size of the low frequency effect of age is substantially larger when ICA 903 

denoising is applied. 904 

The low alpha effect was strongly reduced in the analyses that do not remove artefacts 905 

with ICA. A large central-occipital group of sensors show an effect between 7 and 8.5Hz in 906 

the ICA analysis, but this is reduced to a single sensor at 8Hz when ICA is not computed. 907 

At high frequencies, the age effect in frontal sensors is larger with ICA and the age effect in 908 

posterior sensors is larger without ICA, though the position and frequencies of significant 909 

effects are largely unchanged. The remaining effects in the high alpha and beta ranges are 910 

unchanged by application of ICA. 911 

Overall, ICA either improves the estimation of age effects (low-frequency, low-alpha, 912 

high-gamma) or has negligible effect (high-alpha, beta). Only the posterior low-gamma 913 

effect is reduced by ICA. Together, we take this as evidence that our core results are robust 914 

to interference by eye movements and that the ICA denoising is working effectively to 915 

reduce noise in the analysis. 916

## A.6 Multicollinearity between Age and Grey Matter Volume 954 

There is a strong correlation between age and Global Grey Matter Volume (Pearson’s 955 

r=-0.75). The shared variance arising from this collinearity adds nuance to the 956 

interpretation of the results, which we explore in more detail in this section. 957 

Firstly, the sum-square residuals for the group-level model fit including both Age and 958 

GGMV are shown as a function of frequency in Figure 18. We see that the residuals broadly 959 

follow the overall distribution of variance in the data, peaking at low frequencies and in the 960 

alpha range. 961 

These frequency ranges are where the strongest signal is visible, but also the highest 962 

variability between participants. We would expect that the group model would not perform 963 

so well in the points of greatest variability. Importantly, though the residuals are relatively 964 

high in the alpha this is still in the context of a very well-performing model with R2 values 965 

of around 80% . 966 

Figure 18. Model residuals and coefficient of determination as a function of frequency for each channel show that the group model performs well. Left) The sum-square residuals of the first level models with each individual data recording (grey lines) and the overall group average (black line). Right) The R2 coefficient of determination for the group-level model showing that the model is able to describe a high amount of variability across both channels and frequencies. 

Secondly, the correlation between age and GGMV is not inherently problematic for the 967 

GLM, but it does add complexity and nuance to the interpretation of the results. Some 968 

additional model validation statistics are shown in Figure 19. 969 

The singular value spectrum of the design matrix indicates whether a design is low-rank, 970 

the smallest singular value in this case in 0.37 which indicates that there isn’t a rank 971 

deficiency which would prevent us from estimating the model. 972 

Though we can estimate the model, correlated regressors can reduce its efficiency. The 973 

variance inflation factors for the joint AGE-GGMV model are above 1 for both parametric 974 

regressors, indicating that the standard errors of their estimates are inflated. The VIF of 975 

2.35 indicates that the standard errors of this joint model are around sqrt(2.35) = 1.533 976 

times greater than they would be in a separate or uncorrelated model. Though there is no 977 

hard rule for this, the literature generally suggests that a VIF above 5 (or sometimes 10) 978 

indicates severe multicollinearity. 979 

Including additional regressors in the model can change the age estimate by ‘partialling’ 980 

out the variance that can be attributed to the other variables, and by inflating standard 981 

errors. In this specific case of GGMV, the partialled estimates are reduced heterogeneously 982 

across space and frequency, and the amount of inflation is at a tolerable level. Overall, the 983 

regression is able to separate the unique effects of age and GGMV, at the cost of this 984 

inflation in the associated standard errors 985
