Abstract 24 

As the field expands from early research into the human connectome, there has been a fast 25 

expansion in the number of analytical approaches to study resting state functional MRI (rsfMRI) 26 

data. With increasing focus on individual differences, topographical brain maps of spatial 27 

organization have emerged in addition to traditional functional connectomes. Here, we developed 28 

a deep -learning model to embed maps of network topography and faithfully translate to 29 

individualized connectomes. Results confirmed the validity of the surface vision transformer based 30 

on reconstruction accuracy (0.73±0.09) and accurate topography -to -connectome translation 31 

(0.43±0.08). Importantly, translated connectomes retained identifiability and brain -cognition 32 

associations. These findings establish a direct mapping from spatial topography to connectomes 33 

that can be used to integrate scientific insights across rsfMRI sub -fields. This is an important step 34 

towards broadening our conceptualization of the connectome and supporting a broader integration 35 

of findings to inform a complete understanding of the human connectome. 36

Introduction 42 

The human connectome was initially proposed as a way to illustrate how functional brain states 43 

emerge from their structural substrates (1). Specifically, the goal of the human connectome was to 44

white matter structural connectivity (SC) from diffusion weighted imaging with functional 46 

connectivity (FC) from resting state functional magnetic resonance imaging (rsfMRI). Over the last 47 

20 years, the human connectome has helped characterize various properties of SC such as network 48 

organization (2, 3), its role in clinical settings (4), and developmental changes (5–7). For FC, this 49 

structure -function mapping revealed large overlaps of pathways with SC across the brain (8–10 ).50 

However, not all FC connections are bound by known cortico -cortical pathways, largely due to FC 51 

representing statistical co -activation patterns across the cortex (9, 11 ). Consequently, the 52 

connectome has offered a flexible definition of FC, contributing to its rising popularity in brain -53 

wide association studies, and resulting in a rapid expansion of approaches to study FC. 54 

Generally, FC is studied using group -derived parcellations to extract time series and 55 

calculate region -by -region temporal correlations (12 ). Beyond these functional connectomes, 56 

studies have expanded the quantification of rsfMRI to derive individualized spatial topography 57 

maps (13 ). This expansion of possible brain representation (i.e., low -dimensional summary 58 

features) derived from rsfMRI scans has provided novel insights into individual differences in 59 

functional organization (14 ). However, this analytical variability also catalyzed divergent 60 

methodological silos, limiting replicability and clinical applications (15 ). Direct comparisons of 61 

results have proven helpful to map out the effects of analytical variability in related domains 62 

including fMRI preprocessing and relevance to behavior (14 , 16 ). However, leveraging direct 63 

comparisons to integrate FC findings is challenging due to fundamental mismatches in the type of 64 

brain representations (temporal connectomes vs topography maps), as well as their dimensionality 65 

and spatial boundaries. Despite these fundamental mismatches that obscure straightforward 66 

comparison, both brain representations are derived from the same rsfMRI scans and may therefore 67 

capture overlapping information. 68 

There is an important need to integrate findings regarding functional organization across 69 

different rsfMRI brain representations, such as region -to -region temporal connectomes and 70 

topography maps. Such integration of rsfMRI findings is necessary to guide correct mechanistic 71 

interpretations, gain a comprehensive understanding of the connectome, and inform best practices 72 

for analytical approaches in clinical trials. Recently, a toolbox was developed to assess spatial 73 

correspondence between group -level parcellations used to define temporal connectomes and group -74 

level topography maps (17 ). These results are useful for comparing group -level spatial parcellation 75 

definitions, potentially in combination with standardized nomenclature (18 ). However, this spatial 76 

correspondence toolbox was not designed to integrate findings regarding individual differences. 77 

Other studies have anchored comparisons of individual differences across rsfMRI brain 78 

representations based on their associations with behavior (19 –21 ). These studies showed 79 

remarkable consistency in the behaviorally -relevant information captured regardless of the 80 

parcellation and indicated 60% shared variance between temporal connectomes and topography 81 

maps (21 ). Although these results are valuable to map behaviorally relevant shared variance 82 

between brain representations, brain wide associations have been shown to capture a relatively 83 

minor portion of individual differences, such that the use of behavior to anchor comparisons has its 84 

own limitations (22 ).85 

In our prior work, we leveraged topological data analysis to identify how brain 86 

representations compare in preserving inter -subject variability features on a shared manifold (23 ),87 

revealing overlap between topography and amplitudes but dissimilarity between topography and 88

We introduce a deep learning model capable of embedding maps of network topography into a 101

connectome. Our encoder -decoder deep learning architecture (Fig. 1) builds on prior work 103 

developing the Surface Vision Transformer (SiT) that applies multi -head attention (29 ) across all 104 

vertices on any non -genus surface (30 ). Briefly, our findings validate the SiT as an appropriate 105 

encoder framework when embedding cortical surface maps, allowing us to achieve topography -to -106 

connectome translation accuracy on par with prior connectome -to -connectome translations while 107 

preserving downstream brain wide association results. These findings therefore establish an 108 

efficient direct mapping from spatial topography to connectomes of rsfMRI, providing a path to 109 

integrate insights and results across the two. 110

Results 112 

We trained and tested our rsfMRI topography -to -connectome translation framework (Fig. 1) using 113 

data from the Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) cohort with a site -based split of 114 

training (N train = 7104), validation (N val = 920), and test (N test = 624). Model performance was 115 

primarily assessed using the within -subject correlation between measured and translated outcomes 116 

after subtracting the training group mean. 117 

The result section consists of four main parts. In the first part, we validated the SiT encoder 118 

architecture by assessing reconstruction accuracy in a topography -to -topography framework for 119 

individual -specific topography maps. In the second part, we applied the validated encoder into a 120 

topography -to -connectome framework to translate from a 15 -dimensional set of ICA topography 121 

maps to a 100 -dimensional connectome. Our goal was to translate these individual -specific 122 

topography maps into corresponding individualized connectomes. In the third part, we therefore 123 

explicitly tested the preservation of individual differences by assessing to what degree the translated 124 

connectomes retained subject identifiability and brain -cognition associations. A translation model 125 

is only practically useful if it translates well to different settings. Therefore, in the fourth part, we 126 

determined the generalizability of the topography -to -connectome model to different connectome 127 

dimensionalities (100 vs 300), and parcellations (Schaefer vs Glasser). Full details on the 128 

architecture, hyperparameters, optimization, and assessments are available in the Materials and 129 

Methods. 130

reconstruction in the top 10%, median, and bottom 10% are shown in Fig. 2. High reconstruction 146

Translating from spatial topography to connectomes 154 

We combined the SiT encoder with a linear decoder to translate from topography network maps 155 

(all 15 ICA maps) to a connectome defined as the full pairwise bivariate correlations using the 100 -156 

dimensional Schaefer atlas (31 ) (Materials and Methods). Similar to the reconstruction application, 157 

we trained the model to translate demeaned connectomes to avoid regression to the group mean. 158 

Translation accuracy was assessed with the demeaned versions of both the measured upper triangle 159 

of the connectome and its translated counterpart. 160 

Translation accuracy in the held -out test dataset was on average r = 0.43±0.08. Connectome 161 

translations for representative subjects in the top 10%, median, and bottom 10% are shown in Fig. 162 

3. Other metrics of model performance are shown in supplementary Table 1, and model 163 

performance as a function of training mini -batches in training and validation data is shown in 164 

supplementary Fig. S2. Taken together, these results confirm the ability of the SiT encoder -decoder 165 

framework to translate from spatial FC topography to individualized connectomes. In the next 166 

section, we explicitly evaluated to what degree individual differences were retained after 167 

translation. 168

connectomes. Specifically, we performed two different tests of subject identifiability and two 173 

separate brain -cognition association analyses. Firstly, we assessed the average rank percentile, 174 

which compares how a measured connectome and its corresponding translation compare against all 175 

other translated connectomes. To successfully preserve connectome individual differences, any 176

Discussion 234 

In this work, we introduced a novel topography -to -connectome translator model that can map from 235 

individualized spatial maps to analogous connectomes, while preserving individual differences in 236 

finger printing and downstream brain -cognition analyses. We first validated the proposed spatial 237 

topography encoder, showing high and consistent reconstruction accuracy across brain maps, 238 

including both primary and higher association networks. When testing the full topography -to -239 

connectome translator, translated connectomes demonstrated consistent and robust preservation of 240 

measured connectomes including preserved subject identification and brain -cognition associations. 241 

Overall, this paper contributes to the connectomics field by integrating individual difference 242 

findings from spatial topography analyses of rsfMRI data with findings from FC connectomics. 243 

Our topography -to -connectome translator achieved a performance of r = 0.43 (demeaned 244 

correlation between measured and translated connectomes), well within the range of previous 245 

connectome -based translation efforts. For example, Krakencoder was optimized to map from one 246 

connectome type to another, using a many -to -many approach by embedding various kinds of 247 

connectome “flavors” and assessing reconstruction and translation accuracy among them (25 ). In 248 

their study, the accuracy for reconstructions and translations ranged r = 0.30 -0.70, except for partial 249 

FC connectomes (typically r = 0.07 -0.30). Others have attempted to translate across SC and FC by 250 

using a more complex generative model. For example, authors in (26 ) trained an adversarial 251 

generative model to translate from structural and functional connectomes, with performance 252 

accuracies typically ranging from r = 0.34 -0.43. Using our SiT encoder -decoder, translation 253 

accuracy somewhat diminished when translating to higher resolution connectomes but was stable 254 

between different atlas types (Fig. 5). The small decrease in translation accuracy for the Schaefer -255 

300 and Glasser -360 parcellations may result from having the linear decoder infer more elements 256 

for higher parcellation sizes. Additionally, translations to higher dimensional connectomes may be 257 

impacted by residual between -subject misalignment. Future work may wish to explore how 258 

improved alignment using multimodal surface matching (32 , 33 ) prior to the calculation of 259 

topography maps may impact translation accuracy. 260 

Our topography -to -connectome direction presents a different task from the connectome -to -261 

connectome translations discussed above because the source is a non -euclidean object being 262 

mapped onto a Positive Semi -Definite connectome form. In fact, our previous work suggested that 263 

spatial maps and connectomes have predominantly diverging inter -subject variability features, 264

to a non -linear mapping that directly translated from post -preprocessed dual regressed ICA brain 266

model offers an accessible opportunity for a multiverse approach without the need for reanalysis 268

(without timeseries) may illuminate important shared information between divergent analytical 270 

brain representations of rsfMRI data that could be leveraged to enrich and integrate our 271 

understanding of the resting brain. Connectomes define a binary assignment scheme where all 272 

vertices within a parcel are averaged to define the time -series for that parcel. The spatial definitions 273 

of parcels are often defined from a group -average atlas, whereas topography maps like ICA, define 274 

varying functional boundaries across the cortex and across subjects. Therefore, for any given group -275 

averaged parcel, differing functional boundaries across subjects systematically impact the parcel 276 

time series and thus establish an association between parcel timeseries and functional boundaries 277 

in ICA that can be exploited by our topography -to -connectome translators ( 35 ). 278 

Importantly, the translated connectomes retained individual difference information. 279 

Identifiability was strong, suggesting the usability of translated connectomes for finger printing 280 

(36 ). In fact, both univariate and multivariate brain -cognition associations preserved enough 281 

information regarding individual differences, to the extent that both measured and translated 282 

connectomes were strongly correlated in their univariate associations and achieved similar 283 

multivariate brain -cognition associations. These results suggest that connectome -wide individual 284 

differences were retained despite a slight drop in edge -wise individual differences. This advantage 285 

of multivariate over univariate performance may be driven by relatively higher levels of noise at 286 

the edge level and/or shared variance (e.g., redundancy of information) across the full multivariate 287 

connectome ( 37 ). 288 

Notably, translating to partial connectomes had the lowest performance of all connectome 289 

translations. This result is somewhat surprising given that partial connectomes have been shown to 290 

benefit from noise reduction ( 38 ). However, as mentioned previously, similar drops in performance 291 

were observed in the Krakencoder ( 25 ), and partial connectomes have been shown to have lower 292 

test -retest reliability ( 39 ), potentially due to instability in regularization ( 40 ). Furthermore, ICA 293 

derived topography maps represent whole brain connectivity for any given network by grouping 294 

similar vertices, which more closely resemble full correlation connectomes. Partial correlation FC 295 

connectomes, instead, regress out all other time series before calculating a pairwise edge, resulting 296 

in a sparser FC matrix. This difference may explain why translation accuracy was higher for full 297 

correlation than partial correlation connectomes. Further work is needed to integrate partial 298 

connectome results with ICA and full correlation FC connectomics. 299 

Integrating results across rsfMRI studies is crucial to inform translational research of brain 300 

diseases. To date, practical applications of rsfMRI in clinical settings are relatively limited. This 301 

dissonance between rsfMRI analyses and clinical work arises from limitations in brain wide 302 

association ( 22 ), non -standardized nomenclature ( 18 ), disagreement across analyses pipelines ( 16 ), 303 

and flexibility in choice of brain representation ( 15 ). The topography -to -connectome translator 304 

model addresses the latter issue by assuming brain representations share underlying latent features 305 

that can be used to tether them. Recently, this perspective has been expanded on to enable a 306 

“multiverse approach” without the high computational burden ( 34 ), by embedding various kinds of 307 

analysis pipelines into a lower dimensional latent space and using a deep learning approach to 308

Although our results revealed the ability to translate from topography and connectomes 314 

while retaining individual differences, there are several limitations to discuss. Firstly, deep learning 315 

models require optimization of a large number of parameters, which requires a big training dataset. 316 

Although we achieved reasonable performance using ABCD data, it is possible that we have not 317 

yet hit the ceiling of translation accuracy. In future work, we plan to expand to the UK Biobank 318 

cohort (which now includes N=100,000, enabling a more than 10 -fold increase in our current 319 

training sample) to test this hypothesis. Secondly, there are many other types of topography brain 320 

maps (additional dimensions and methods), many other connectomes, and additional sources of 321 

analytical variability (e.g., variation resulting from data acquisition and preprocessing pipelines). 322 

Although we tested the ability to translate to different connections, future work will be needed to 323 

expand the translations to a broader set of topography and connectome brain representations. 324 

Specifically, in future work, we plan to expand into a comprehensive set of ‘ NeuroTranslators’ that 325 

separately learn mappings from one topography based representation to some connectome profile, 326 

such that each separate model can be fused to reach an abstracted unifying latent space capturing 327 

underlying statistical properties of rsfMRI data that facilitate direct translation and comparison. 328 

In summary, we demonstrated the ability to faithfully translate individual FC connectomes 329 

from rsfMRI topography maps. These results broaden our conceptualization of the connectome and 330 

emphasize the relevance of rsfMRI brain representations that are not traditionally considered as 331 

‘connectomes’ to the field of connectomics. As such, we believe a broader integration of findings 332 

will be valuable to inform a complete understanding of the human connectome, not only within the 333 

context of other types of connectomes, but beyond that and onto other domains of analyses. With 334 

this in mind, our topography -to -connectome mapping seeks to resolve a goal identified in the 335 

original proposal of the human connectome, namely the creation of a publicly accessible 336 

standardized data format. In the presence of expanding analytical approaches to study rsfMRI, this 337 

standardized data format goal can be reenvisioned through comparison and integration facilitated 338 

by translation. 339

Dataset 342 

The Adolescent Brain Cognitive Development (ABCD) cohort is a publicly available dataset with 343 

a large sample size of typically developing children (N=8,648) from ages 9 - 11 recruited across 21 344 

different sites in the United States (41 –43 ). Data were acquired across three types of 3T scanners 345 

(Siemens Prisma, General Electric 750, and Philips) running standard echo -planar imaging 346 

sequence protocol (TR=800ms, TE=30ms, flip angle=52°, multiband factor=6, 2.4mm isotropic 347 

voxel resolution). ABCD data were preprocessed using the ABCD -BIDS pipelines (42 ), including 348 

standard frame -to -frame head motion correction, nonlinear alignment to standard space, Freesurfer 349 

extraction of the cortical ribbon, and formatting to grayordinate format. Data cleanup for rsfMRI 350 

furthermore followed the ‘DCAN -BOLD Preproc’ module, including regression of white matter, 351 

CSF, mean CIFTI timeseries, the six motion parameters and their volterra expansion, bandpass 352

Brain Representations . Group ICA was performed on the rsfMRI data concatenated across all 358 

subjects at a dimensionality of 15, followed by dual regression to estimate subject -specific surface 359 

network maps (27 , 28 ). The dimensionality of 15 was chosen to balance the topographical 360 

information available to models while maintaining computational feasibility of the SiT embedding. 361 

Additionally, to ensure only individual differences were embedded, each spatial brain map was 362 

normalized by the training sample mean and standard deviation for that brain map. For 363 

connectomes, each subject’s data was spatially parcellated into 100/300 regions based on the 364 

Schaefer atlas (31 ). The same process was done for the Glasser 360 atlas (44 ). Time series from 365 

each region were extracted and correlated with every other to construct a pairwise bivariate full 366 

correlation matrix. Partial correlations were produced by applying the Moore -Penrose pseudo -367 

inverse on the full correlation matrix, which is analogous to using an L2 -regularized inverse. 368

Embedding and Translating Spatial Topography Maps 370 

Both reconstruction and translation used the same SiT -based encoder paired with a linear decoder. 371 

We did not use a convolutional encoder because these have been shown to perform less well for 372 

cortical surface data ( 30 , 45 ). The reason that transformers outperform convolutional neuronal 373 

networks (CNNs) can be traced to their powerful self -attention learning mechanism ( 29 , 46 ) that 374 

encodes understanding of long -range context via modelling the co -variance of different features 375 

across the entire surface. This is necessary for modelling cognition since these processes recruit 376 

distributed networks spread over the entire cortical surface. CNNs cannot achieve this because they 377 

purposely learn highly localized filters. More recently, designs of generalizable 'foundation 378 

models' (47 , 48 ) evidences that - with enough data - transformers are now broadly seen as the 379 

architecture of choice for complex image understanding tasks. 380

Fig. 1. Schematic for ICA embeddings into latent space and linear decoders. (A) Topography -696 

to -topography models embedded the vertex space of each subject's normalized topography map 697 

(separate models for each of 15 ICA networks) into a lower dimensional triangular tessellation that 698 

formed the input to the SiT. Output of the SiT was mapped into a latent vector from which a linear 699 

transformation reconstructs triangular patches that were compared to the measured normalized 700 

topography input patches. ( B) The translation models were trained using the normalized measured 701 

topography maps of all 15 ICA networks as input. Each topography map was first resampled into 702 

the same non -overlapping triangular tessellation patches used in (A), which were combined across 703 

all 15 spatial brain maps. The models then projected these patches into a latent space and used a 704 

final linear transform to decode the upper triangle of the demeaned FC connectome, which was 705 

compared to the measured demeaned FC connectome. Importantly, the group mean was removed 706 

from the inputs to the models and the targets for reconstruction or translation to avoid regression to 707 

the mean and encourage sensitivity to individual differences. Abbreviations: Reshape (RS), Surface 708 

Vision Transformer (SiT), Linear Layer (LN). 709
