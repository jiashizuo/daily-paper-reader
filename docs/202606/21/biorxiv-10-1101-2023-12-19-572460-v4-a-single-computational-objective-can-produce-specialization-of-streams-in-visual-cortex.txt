Human visual cortex is organized into dorsal, lateral, and ventral streams. A long-standing hypothesis 11 

is that the functional organization into streams emerged to support distinct visual behaviors. Here, we 12 

compare neural network-based computational models against a massive fMRI dataset to investigate why 13 

visual streams emerge. We find that a self-supervised Topographic Deep Artificial Neural Network (TDANN), 14 

which encourages nearby units to respond similarly, better captures brain responses, as well as spatial 15

It is The copyright holder for this preprint this version posted June 20, 2026. ;https://doi.org/10.1101/2023.12.19.572460 doi: bioRxiv preprint A central question in neuroscience is why the visual system is spatially and functionally organized the way it is. 22 The human visual system is comprised of extensive neural machinery, including more than two dozen visual areas 23 organized into three anatomically distinct hierarchical processing streams, spanning the occipital, temporal, and 24 parietal lobes [1, 2]: (1) the ventral stream, which ascends from early visual cortex (EVC: V1, V2, V3) in the occipital 25 lobe to the inferior aspects of the temporal lobe; (2) the lateral stream, which ascends from EVC through lateral 26 occipito-temporal cortex to the superior temporal sulcus (STS); and (3) the dorsal stream, which projects superiorly 27 from EVC along occipito-parietal cortex. However, it remains unresolved why the visual system is organized into 28 multiple processing streams. 29 The reasons why the visual system is functionally organized into streams have been studied for decades across 30 empirical [3, 4, 5, 6], information-theoretic [1, 2], and computational [7, 8] approaches. Three key ideas have emerged 31 from these investigations. First, to support complex visual behaviors, the visual system may decompose complex 32 operations into a sequence of simpler computations distributed hierarchically across processing stages [9, 1, 2, 10, 33 11]. Second, to enable rapid and efficient processing, computations supporting independent visual behaviors, such 34 as recognizing an object (e.g., a car), determining what’s its doing (e.g., approaching), and gauging its distance, 35 are thought to be done in parallel by distinct streams [3, 4, 12, 13, 14]. At the same time, the precise computations 36 supported by each stream remain an active topic of debate, particularly for lateral and dorsal visual streams. The 37 ventral stream is hypothesized to be involved in visual recognition [3, 4] and categorization [15, 16, 17], whereas the 38 lateral stream is hypothesized to be involved in processing visual dynamics, actions, multimodal information (e.g., 39 language), and social information [12, 13, 14, 18], and the dorsal stream is hypothesized to be involved in spatial 40 processing, spatial attention, and visually guided actions [3, 4]. Third, physical constraints, such as fitting the brain 41 into the skull [19] and a need for fast processing, generate a bias for short-range connections [20, 8, 1, 2], which, 42 in turn, may encourage nearby neurons to respond similarly [1, 21, 22, 23]. Indeed, nearby neurons tend to exhibit 43 correlated responses, evident in the many topographic maps across visual cortex, ranging from retinotopic maps 44 [24, 25, 26] in which nearby neurons in cortex process nearby regions of the visual field, to clustering of neurons that 45 selectively respond to specific categories (e.g., faces [27, 28] or words [29]) in high-level visual cortex. 46 Advances in deep artificial neural networks (DANNs) over the past decade have enabled theories to be transformed 47 into concrete computational models that are trained with images and videos as inputs and tested against real neural 48 data, revolutionizing computational and theoretical neuroscience [30, 17, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38]. To date, 49 the majority of vision-DANN research has focused on understanding the function of the ventral stream, which is 50 empirically the best understood. The main insights from this research are four-fold: (i) feedforward DANNs predict 51 responses across the ventral stream, with earlier layers in DANNs matching responses in early visual cortex (e.g., 52 V1), and later layers matching high-level visual cortex (e.g., ventral temporal cortex, VTC, end of ventral stream) 53 [17, 31, 36], (ii) DANNs trained on behaviorally-relevant tasks, such as categorization for the ventral stream, better 54 predict neural responses than other DANNs [17], (iii) many DANN architectures replicate neural responses in the 55 ventral stream, albeit with numeric differences in performance [35, 39], and (iv) topographic organization within a 56 visual area can emerge through wiring-constrained training [22, 40, 41, 42, 43, 44, 45]. However, most of this work 57 has focused either on ventral cortex or on topography within a single visual area, leaving open whether the same 58 modeling ingredients can explain the broader segregation of cortex into ventral, lateral, and dorsal streams. 59 Here, we compare two computational accounts of stream organization, instantiated in different model families: 60 optimization of different streams for different candidate visual behaviors (multiple behaviors hypothesis) [3, 46, 61 47, 48, 49, 17], and learning useful visual representations under spatial constraints to minimize wiring (spatial 62 constraints hypothesis) [19, 8, 1, 2]. Although these model families are not mutually exclusive, comparing the 63 behavior and spatial constraints models allows us to test whether optimization for visual behaviors is necessary for a 64 stream-like organization to emerge. In this framework, behavior-optimized models serve as candidate computational 65 instantiations of prominent theories of stream-specific function, rather than an exhaustive test of all possible 66 behavior-based accounts. To make comparisons across model families possible, we focus on image-computable 67 models. Accordingly, broader behaviors such as navigation, visually guided action, and social interactions are 68 represented through image-computable proxies rather than full closed-loop agentic models. We first establish that 69 the empirical brain data that will be used for model comparison exhibits differentiated responses across streams 70 allowing rigorous model testing, then define the candidate model classes and evaluation framework, and finally test 71 which models best capture the functional and spatial organization into streams. 72 Finzi et al. | spacestream | 2

Distinct representational structure and category selectivity across visual streams in the Natural Scenes 74 

Dataset (NSD) 75 Before assessing the models, we tested whether the brain data we will use to test the models – the Natural Scenes 76 Dataset (NSD) [50], which measured high-resolution brain responses to 8000-10,000 of natural images in each of 77 eight individuals during a recognition memory task – shows stream-differentiated responses. Using the 515 NSD 78 images seen three times by all participants, we compute representational similarity matrices for regions of interest 79 (ROIs) spanning early, middle, and high-level visual regions, and compare the representational structure across 80 participants and ROIs. Multidimensional scaling revealed a broad hierarchical progression from early visual cortex 81 to mid-level and then high-level visual ROIs, together with a large-scale separation by stream for high-level regions 82 (Fig. 1a). High-level lateral ROIs were separated from a tight ventral cluster, which was in turn largely distinct 83 from dorsal ROIs, indicating systematic differences in representational structure across the three streams. Mapping 84 responses across these participants’ brains also finds better matches among high-level ROIs within the same stream 85 than across streams (Supplementary Fig. S2). 86 Likewise, analysis of brain responses in the same participants to another stimulus set containing controlled images 87 from 10 ecologically-relevant categories (fLoc [51]), finds significant differences in category-selectivity across 88 streams (Fig. 1b) consistent with prior work [16, 52, 53]. Place-selective voxels were more prevalent in ventral 89 (t(7) = 21 .45 , Bonferroni-corrected p = 1 .1 → 10 →6) and dorsal ( t(7) = 6 .19 , corrected p = 4 .0 → 10 →3) cortex 90 than lateral cortex. Body-selective voxels were more prevalent in lateral than ventral ( t(7) = 13 .59 , corrected 91 

p = 2 .5 → 10 →5) and dorsal ( t(7) = 14 .87 , corrected p = 1 .3 → 10 →5) cortex, and face-selective voxels were more 92 prevalent in ventral ( t(7) = 8 .41 , corrected p = 5 .97 → 10 →4) and lateral ( t(7) = 8 .93 , corrected p = 4 .03 → 10 →4)93 cortex than dorsal cortex. Together, these analyses show that the NSD and fLoc provide functionally differentiated 94 brain responses across streams that will allow us to distinguish models of stream organization. 95 

Computational framework to instantiate hypotheses for stream organization using deep neural networks 96 We test which of two model families best predict functional organization into streams: multiple behaviors (MB) or 97 spatial constraints (SC). We instantiate the multiple behaviors hypothesis as the union of three different DANNs, 98 each trained for a stream-hypothesized task. We reason that training different DANNs for different tasks allows 99 optimal task training and encourages learning of maximally different representations suited for each behavior. We 100 use a ResNet-50 backbone [55], as larger models achieve better task performance than shallower models with 101 fewer parameters (e.g., ResNet-18), and previous research has shown a positive correlation between a model’s 102 task performance and its ability to predict neural responses in the ventral stream [17]. In the main multiple 103 behavior model (MB v1), we select tasks that are the computer vision equivalents of behaviors each stream is 104 commonly hypothesized to support - dorsal: object detection [56], lateral: action recognition [57], and ventral: 105 object categorization [55] (Fig. 2a). To test whether results depend on the specific stream functions we model, 106 we implemented two additional variants of stream-specific behaviors. In MB v2, we implemented a vision-language 107 model (CLIP [58]) instead of an action recognition model. This choice was motivated by evidence that the lateral 108 stream extends into posterior superior temporal cortex, which includes multi-modal and speech-/language-related 109 regions [59, 60, 61, 62, 63], recent findings that multi-modal models better capture high-level visual representations 110 than standard supervised networks [64], and theoretical work proposing that the lateral stream may be expanded 111 in humans beyond processing dynamic stimuli to support multi-modal processing such as language [12]. We also 112 changed the detection and categorization models to higher-performing architectures, using DETR [65] as a model for 113 detection and ConvNeXt-T [66] for categorization. In MB v3, we test models for other hypothesized functions of the 114 lateral and dorsal streams, while the ventral stream model remains an object categorization network (ResNet-50), as 115 categorization is the most established candidate function for the ventral stream [17, 39, 31]. For the lateral stream 116 model we implement a human pose-estimation network (DEKR-Pose) [67], motivated by evidence suggesting that 117 the lateral stream is involved in processing body form, motion, and social information [12, 13, 14, 18]. For the dorsal 118 stream model, we implement a monocular depth-estimation network (DepthAnythingV2) [68], motivated by the role 119 of dorsal stream in visually guided behaviors and spatial computations that depend on three-dimensional structure 120 [4, 69]. For all candidate MB models, units from the endpoint layer of the three constituent networks are pooled onto 121 a simulated cortical sheet using the same position-initialization procedure used for topographic models to enable 122 spatial comparison to brain data spanning the union of high-level regions spanning the three visual streams. 123 We instantiate the spatial constraints hypothesis using a topographic DANN (TDANN, [41, 70]), as it implements both 124 a self-supervised learning task (SimCLR [71], Fig. 2b) and spatial constraint during training and has been effective 125 in predicting the functional topography of the ventral stream [41]. Evaluating functional and spatial correspondence between models and the brain 142 We use a two-stage approach to evaluate functional and spatial correspondence. First, for each participant, we 143 use the Hungarian algorithm [54] and that participant’s unique image set from the NSD to derive a 1-to-1 mapping 144 between model units in the convolutional end layer and voxels in the union of anatomical ROIs corresponding to 145 the ends of each stream, based on their responses to the same images (Fig. 2c). This 1-to-1 mapping between 146 units and voxels not only provides a window into spatial organization but also provides a more stringent [74, 75] and 147 rigorous [76] testing of DANN models of the brain. Second, using this mapping, we assess spatial and functional 148 correspondence between the candidate model and the brain on an independent set of 515 held-out images seen by 149 all eight NSD participants (5-9% of the total images viewed, depending on the participant; Fig.2d-right). In total, we 150 evaluate 1312 model-to-brain mappings across candidate model types, model seeds, participants, and hemispheres. 151 We assess spatial correspondence both qualitatively and quantitatively. Qualitatively, we visualize where each model 152 unit maps onto its corresponding voxel on the cortical sheet (Fig. 2d-left). Quantitatively, we measure the similarity 153 between the topography of units on the simulated cortical sheet and that of their assigned voxels in cortex (Methods: 154 Evaluating spatial correspondence; Fig. 2d-right). The spatial similarity metric is the correlation between distances 155 between units on the simulated cortical sheet and distances among their corresponding voxels on the cortical sheet. 156 Spatial similarity is high if nearby units on the simulated cortical sheet are mapped to nearby voxels on the cortical 157 sheet. We assess functional similarity by measuring the correlation between each model unit’s response and that of 158 its corresponding voxel on an independent set of 515 held-out images seen by all participants (Fig. 2d-right). 159 To assess the maximal performance that models can achieve under this framework, we estimate the noise ceiling 160 of the brain data, hypothesizing that the best model for the human brain is another human brain [77]. We use the 161 Hungarian algorithm and 80% of the 515 shared images for finding the corresponding voxels across participants, and 162 evaluate the spatial and functional correspondence between participants’ brains as above using the remaining 20% 163 of shared images. Most voxels in a given stream of one participant were mapped to the corresponding stream in other 164 participants (Supplementary Fig. S3), validating the mapping procedure and providing stream-specific benchmarks 165 for both spatial and functional correspondence (horizontal gray bars throughout). 166

Crucially, 399 both ingredients are necessary: stream structure emerges only when the model combines a self-supervised learning 400 objective with an intermediate spatial weight. Moreover, the spatial constraint that produces the best model-to-brain 401 spatial correspondence also produces the best model-to-brain functional correspondence and most closely matches 402 the effective dimensionality of representations in the human visual system. Thus, a key new idea from our study is 403 that the spatial and functional dimensions of cortical organization are intertwined, and that a local spatial constraint 404 combined with general representation learning can percolate up and generate large-scale structure. Our data also 405 underscore the importance of modeling brain topography alongside function, rather than focusing on function alone, 406 as is done in most current DANN approaches [30, 17, 31, 32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38], because incorporating spatial 407 constraints yields models that better predict brain function. Taken together, our results suggest that the organization 408 of visual streams may reflect a tradeoff between learning general-purpose representations in a self-supervised 409 manner and conforming to the physical constraints imposed by the size and wiring limitations of the brain. 410 The success of our spatial constraints model builds upon recent studies of topographic map emergence in the ventral 411 stream [22, 40, 70, 41, 43, 42, 45], which suggest that wiring [22], smoothness [40], and the balancing of spatial 412 and functional constraints [41] can give rise to topographic organization, more energy efficient systems, and can 413 extend to other backbone architectures like visual transformers. Our findings also align with work in mice showing 414 that a dual-pathway model with a self-supervised predictive loss better fits visual cortex data than task-optimized 415 models [89]. At the same time, our results go beyond these studies by showing that explicit optimization of separate 416 pathways for different candidate visual behaviors is not required to reproduce the segregation of visual streams. 417 We were surprised to find that models based on the multiple behavioral demands hypothesis produced substantially 418 lower functional correspondence with human visual cortex than the spatial constraints model. Based on the cognitive 419 neuroscience literature [3, 4, 90, 12, 13, 14]) our expectation going into the study was that the multiple behavioral 420 demands model would serve as a powerful benchmark model. The performance of the MB models was especially 421 surprising because training tasks that yielded a higher functional match to cortical responses (e.g., categorization) 422 yielded better functional correspondence across all streams, rather than better matching functional responses in 423 their hypothesized cortical stream (e.g., the ventral stream [3, 4, 90, 12, 13, 14]). These findings highlight two 424 important insights. First, they underscore the value of computational modeling as a tool for testing assumptions that 425 may otherwise be taken for granted. Our results show that the common assumption that a model optimized for a 426 behavior associated with a particular cortical stream (e.g., categorization for the ventral stream) should preferentially 427 capture responses in that stream is not necessarily correct. Explicitly implementing and quantitatively testing such 428 theories with DNN models can therefore reveal unexpected outcomes and challenge prevailing intuitions about the 429 functional organization of the brain. Second, while a large body of research has shown that training on categorization 430 yields models that predict ventral stream responses [17, 31, 32, 34, 35, 36], our findings join a more recent body of 431 work demonstrating that training on a variety of other tasks [91, 39], including biologically plausible self-supervised 432 objectives [72, 41], can achieve comparable levels of neural predictivity in the ventral stream. 433 In contrast, units in the spatial constraints model developed distinct stream-specific functional signatures despite 434 being trained with a single self-supervised objective. This result suggests that functional differentiation can emerge 435 from the interaction of self-supervised learning and spatial constraints, without explicit optimization for separate 436 tasks. Several additional analyses sharpen this interpretation. First, the weaker correspondence of earlier, more 437 V1-like TDANN layers to higher-level visual cortex indicates that stream-like organization does not arise from local 438 smoothness alone. Instead, stream structure emerges in higher layers only once sufficiently rich mid- and high-level 439 visual features have developed. Second, the cross-weight CKA and effective dimensionality analyses indicate that 440 during training the spatial loss does more than impose a spatial arrangement of an otherwise fixed representation on 441 the cortical sheet. Rather, it changes the functional properties of the learned representations, altering their geometry 442 in ways that improve their correspondence with cortical function. 443 Our conclusions should be interpreted within the scope of the models and data tested here. We have not exhaustively 444 evaluated all possible implementations of the multiple-behaviors hypothesis, nor have we tested models against 445 neural data spanning the full range of human visual behaviors. The NSD data were collected during a single 446 recognition memory task while participants viewed images, thus, our findings should not be viewed as a definitive 447 refutation of all possible multiple-behaviors accounts. Rather, our data provide a proof of existence: explicit 448 optimization for several separate tasks is not necessary for the emergence of stream-like organization.
