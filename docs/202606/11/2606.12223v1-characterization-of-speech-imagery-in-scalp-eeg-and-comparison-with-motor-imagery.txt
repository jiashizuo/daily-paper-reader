# Characterization of Speech Imagery in Scalp EEG and Comparison with Motor Imagery 

Bob Van Dyck 1∗, Liuyin Yang 1, Qiang Sun 1, Ang Li 2, Marc M. Van Hulle 1

Abstract — Speech imagery is attractive as a brain-computer interface paradigm for communication because it is endogenous and intrinsically linguistic. Yet despite growing interest, its dom-inant scalp-EEG spatiotemporal characteristics remain poorly characterized. Here, we asked how speech imagery appears in scalp EEG and compared it against finger motor imagery. Using a within-subject dataset containing speech imagery, finger motor imagery, and no-task trials recorded under the same trial structure, we analyzed band-power dynamics across channels and time. Finger motor imagery showed the expected contralateral mu/alpha and low-beta desynchronization over sensorimotor areas, whereas speech imagery showed a weaker, more distributed alpha-dominant increase. After normalization to each condition’s own post-trial interval, the speech-related alpha increase changed only modestly after cue onset, indicating that much of the speech-versus-no-task difference was already present during the instruction period. A classifier discriminat-ing imagery from no-task reached mean balanced accuracies of 0.563 ± 0.072 for speech imagery and 0.718 ± 0.127 for motor imagery, with a stronger alpha/beta dependence for motor imagery than for speech imagery. Together, these results provide a clearer group-level characterization of speech imagery in scalp EEG and indicate that its dominant spatiotemporal pattern differs from that of finger motor imagery and is more consistent with substantial non-articulatory task-related contributions than with a clear articulatory-motor analogue. 

I. INTRODUCTION Speech imagery is attractive as a brain-computer interface paradigm for communication because it is endogenous and directly tied to language. This distinguishes it from reactive paradigms such as P300 and SSVEP spellers, which depend on external stimulation, and from motor imagery BCIs, which are endogenous but typically rely on non-linguistic surrogate control signals [1], [2]. Despite that appeal, speech imagery remains difficult to characterize in scalp EEG. Reviews of the field report weak and heterogeneous effects across datasets, prompts, preprocessing pipelines, and de-coding strategies [3], [2]. What remains unclear is not only whether speech imagery is decodable in EEG, but whether the dominant decodable signal reflects speech-related motor processes or broader condition-level processes associated with preparing for and maintaining the covert task. Finger motor imagery provides a useful reference be-cause canonical EEG motor imagery is associated with con-tralateral sensorimotor mu/alpha and beta desynchronization, with discriminative information classically concentrated over

Recent work instead frames speech imagery as an internal simulation process linking motor and perceptual systems, with task-dependent perceptual reactivation rather than a single overt-like motor state as the relevant conceptual framework [6], [7], [8]. Prior work also suggests that inner speech need not be purely abstract, even without overt output [9]. The question is therefore not whether speech imagery is motor-linked in any sense, but whether its dominant scalp EEG pattern resembles canonical hand motor imagery. We address this issue using a matched within-subject multi-paradigm EEG dataset in which the same participants performed speech imagery, imagined finger movement, and no-task trials under the same trial structure [10]. The goal is to characterize the dominant group-level pattern of speech imagery, test whether it resembles canonical motor imagery, and assess whether the decoding results align with the same interpretation. II. METHODS 

A. Dataset, task design, and preprocessing 

We analyzed a multitask EEG dataset acquired from 34 healthy right-handed adults (18 female), recorded with a Neuroscan SynAmps RT system using 64 scalp EEG channels and 4 electrooculography channels at a sampling rate of 500 Hz. Participants completed multiple BCI tasks within a single session, including imagined speech and imag-ined finger movements. The imagined-speech task comprised three navigation commands ( next , back , select ) and a no-task 

condition. Participants were instructed to imagine pronounc-ing the target cue, emphasizing an articulatory, production-like form of speech imagery. The imagined-finger movement task used three finger flexions ( thumb , index , point ), where point denotes flexion of all fingers except the thumb and index, also with a no-task condition. Trials consisted of a 1.2 s instruction period, followed by a 2 s execution period marked by the fixation-color change at 0 s, and jittered post-trial (0.5 to 0.7 s) and inter-trial (0.75 to 1.5 s) intervals (Fig. 1). For each participant, a total of 60 imagined-speech, and 60 imagined-finger movement trials were recorded per target. The study was approved by the Ethical Committee of UZ Leuven (S62547), and all participants provided written informed consent. Preprocessing included notch filtering, 0.5 to 100 Hz band-pass filtering, bad-channel interpolation, ICA-based artifact removal, and downsampling to 200 Hz. For the present analysis, epochs were extracted from -1.0 to 3.0 s relative to the go cue. We computed Welch power spectral density

B. Spatio-temporal cluster-permutation analysis 

For speech imagery, the three speech targets were averaged to form a single speech condition, and the corresponding contrast was computed by subtracting no-task. For motor imagery, the three finger-movement targets were averaged analogously, and the contrast was computed by subtracting no-task. We analyzed both raw task-versus-no-task contrasts and contrasts recentered to each condition’s own post-trial interval. Statistical significance was assessed separately within each frequency band using a spatio-temporal cluster-based permutation test on subject-wise task-minus-no-task differences, with 10,000 permutations and Holm-Bonferroni correction across the displayed bands [11], [12], [13]. As usual for cluster-based inference, significance is interpreted at the level of extended effects rather than exact onsets or precise locations [14], [15], [16]. 

C. Filterbank CSP for imagery detection 

To relate the spatio-temporal results to imagery detec-tion, we trained subject-specific filterbank Common Spatial Pattern (FB-CSP) detectors with shrinkage LDA on prepro-cessed epochs from 0 to 2 s after cue onset [5], [17]. The default model used six functional bands: delta, theta, alpha, beta, low gamma, and high gamma. Each epoch was decom-posed into these bands, CSP filters were learned per band using shrinkage covariance regularization, and band-wise CSP power features were concatenated for classification. For each task, we trained three pairwise binary detectors, each contrasting one imagery target against the matched no-task condition. Performance was evaluated with 5-fold cross-validation and summarized per subject by averaging balanced accuracy across the five folds and the three target-versus-no-task pairs within each task. Band contributions were probed with leave-one-band-out variants. For descriptive heterogene-ity analyses, we used a balanced-accuracy threshold of > 0.5and also report the stricter uncorrected subject-level binomial threshold. III. RESULTS 

A. Speech imagery shows weak, distributed alpha synchro-nization 

Figure 2 shows clearly different group-level task-versus-no-task contrasts for speech and motor imagery. Motor imagery produced the expected focal decrease in alpha and low-beta power over left sensorimotor area, consistent with canonical contralateral event-related desynchronization dur-ing right-hand imagery [4], [5]. This desynchronization was sustained across the execution period and remained visible into the post-trial window. Speech imagery, by contrast, was characterized by a weaker and more spatially distributed alpha synchronization, strongest near the start of execution and not sustained across the full trial. 

B. Speech imagery is harder to detect than motor imagery 

Imagery detection with a filterbank CSP classifier was comparatively straightforward for motor imagery, but weaker and less reliable for speech imagery. Across 34 subjects, mean balanced accuracy was 0.563 ± 0.072 for speech-versus-no-task detection and 0.718 ± 0.127 for motor-versus-no-task detection. The leave-one-band-out ablation analysis confirmed the spatio-temporal results (Table I). Removing alpha produced the largest drop for both tasks, but the effect was much stronger for motor imagery. Removing beta further reduced motor detection, whereas speech-imagery detection showed a weaker and less specific dependence on individual bands. Overall, motor-imagery detection was more accurate and more tightly linked to the expected alpha/beta structure, whereas speech-imagery detection was weaker and more spectrally diffuse.

DISCUSSION Speech imagery did not resemble the canonical motor-imagery pattern in scalp EEG. Finger motor imagery showed the expected contralateral alpha and low-beta desynchro-nization over sensorimotor scalp, whereas speech imagery showed a weak and distributed alpha synchronization. While contrast is compatible with the broader imagined-speech EEG literature, our within-subject design with a matched no-task condition makes it explicit under a common tim-ing structure, common preprocessing, and a direct motor-imagery reference [3], [2], [18]. More broadly, the observed pattern fits accounts in which speech imagery recruits inter-nal motor-linked and perceptual processes without reducing to the canonical sensorimotor profile of motor imagery [6], [8]. The low-frequency/alpha-heavy structure is also com-patible with imagined-speech work showing that decodable information is not restricted to high-frequency activity [19]. At the same time, alpha synchronization is consistent with broader accounts involving attentional gating, suppression, and controlled access under demanding task conditions [20], [21], [22]. Our results do not uniquely determine the mechanism underlying this effect. They do suggest that, at the group level, the observed scalp pattern is better explained by broader task-related contributions than by a straightforward articulatory counterpart of canonical hand motor imagery. V. CONCLUSION This study provides a clearer group-level characterization of speech imagery in scalp EEG using a matched within-subject design with speech imagery, finger motor imagery, and no-task trials. Finger motor imagery showed the ex-pected contralateral alpha and low-beta desynchronization over left sensorimotor scalp, whereas speech imagery instead showed a weaker and more spatially distributed alpha syn-chronization, although in the best-performing subjects this effect became more focal over the right frontocentral region. Notably, even when participants were instructed to imagine articulatory movements, the dominant scalp pattern did not resemble canonical motor imagery and was more consistent with broader task-related activity (e.g. attention) than with a straightforward articulatory-motor analogue.
