## Abstract 

Recent reasoning-centric Large Language Models (LLMs) have made signif-icant strides, yet they predominantly operate on a passive-inference pattern that assumes complete information. In contrast, real-world clinical intelli-gence is inherently an iterative investigative process requiring strategic evi-dence acquisition. To bridge this gap, we formalize medical diagnosis as an Iterative Evidence-Seeking Task. We leverage Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) to elicit intrinsic reasoning within a closed-loop environment, guided by a novel suite of rewards that enforce diagnostic precision and examination consistency. To facilitate this, we introduce the Retrieval-Augmented Generation-based Examination Simulator (RAGES), a high-fidelity clinical oracle that provides realistic, knowledge-grounded follow-up evidence. Empirical results across diverse datasets demonstrate that our framework enables LLMs to transition from passive responders to autonomous assistants. Notably, our model demonstrates comparable performance to larger and reasoning-enhanced baselines, while RAGES proves superior to vanilla LLMs in generating biologically plausible clinical feedback. 

## 1 Introduction 

The rise of reasoning-centric models like OpenAI o1 (Jaech et al., 2024) and DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) has shifted the LLM landscape toward sophisticated chain-of-thought capabilities. However, a gap remains between static benchmarks and the dynamic nature of real-world intelligence. True intelligence requires more than one-shot inference. It demands the autonomous capacity to actively seek and integrate missing information to re-solve ambiguity. This necessity is most acute in the medical domain, where evidence-based diagnosis serves as the gold standard for clinical safety (Emmi et al., 2023). In clinical prac-tice, diagnosis is inherently an active investigative process: clinicians start with incomplete profiles and must strategically order examinations to refine their hypotheses. Pathological diagnosis, in particular, represents this multi-turn diagnostic trajectory, requiring a seamless loop between hypothesis generation and evidence acquisition. For LLMs, mastering this process is non-trivial, as it demands both deep medical knowledge and strategic reasoning to prioritize examinations under uncertainty. Current medical LLM research, however, is largely confined to a passive-inference pattern, focusing on single-turn, multiple-choice benchmarks with complete information (Qiu et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2025). These settings fail to capture the challenges of partial observability. Furthermore, the inherent subjectivity of differential diagnosis complicates the objective evaluation. To bridge these gaps, we formalize medical diagnosis as an Iterative Evidence-Seeking Task . We employ Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards (RLVR) (DeepSeek-AI, 2025; Mroueh, 2025) to elicit intrinsic reasoning within a closed-loop environment driven by RAGES (Retrieval-Augmented Generation-based Examination Simulator). ## 2 Related Work 

2.1 Reasoning Capabilities of LLMs in Medical Diagnosis 

Early studies have shown that pretrained LLMs encode rich clinical knowledge and ef-fectively answer medical questions, for example, Flan-PaLM (Singhal et al., 2023) and MedFound (Liu et al., 2025c). Prompting techniques such as chain-of-thought (CoT) have proven effective for inducing reasoning (Wei et al., 2023; Besta et al., 2024; Yao et al., 2023). In medicine, structured prompting enhances diagnostic accuracy (Nori et al., 2023; Kwon et al., 2024; Savage et al., 2024). With the advent of OpenAI’s o1 model (Jaech et al., 2024), the focus shifts to the native reasoning capability of LLMs on medical tasks. Nori et al. (2024) evaluate o1-preview on medical challenge problems and find it dramatically outper-forms previous models with prompting. Sandmann et al. (2025) and Tordjman et al. (2025) both evaluate DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) on medical tasks and clinical reasoning, demonstrating the potential of reasoning models. Building on this paradigm, recent work has introduced frameworks designed for stepwise medical reasoning. HuatuoGPT-o1 (Chen et al., 2024) is a medical LLM trained via verifiable reasoning steps. Huang et al. (2025) focus on inference-time scaling of reasoning in the medical domain. MedS 3 (Jiang et al., 2025a) learns to reason about medical problems with a process reward model. Baichuan-M2 (Team et al., 2025) shows strong reasoning capability in medical tasks. 2Preprint. Under review. 

2.2 Reinforcement Learning with Verifiable Rewards 

Reinforcement learning for LLMs originates with reinforcement learning from human feedback (RLHF), which is introduced to better align model outputs with human preferences. More recently, with the advent of DeepSeek-R1 and group relative policy optimization (GRPO) framework, more attention has shifted toward rule-based reward functions as a means to elicit and strengthen the intrinsic reasoning capabilities of LLMs (Mroueh, 2025). In mathematics, functions primarily focus on correctness verification, as seen in Open-R1 (Hugging Face, 2025) and DeepScaleR (Luo et al., 2025). In the medical domain, several studies utilize closed-ended questions and also employ direct correctness verification (Qiu et al., 2025b; Zhang et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025a; Tarek & Beheshti, 2025; Zhu et al., 2025). However, for tasks where answers cannot be easily verified, LLM-as-a-judge has emerged as a practical alternative for reward evaluation (Guan et al., 2024). 2.3 Multi-Turn Diagnosis 

Clinical diagnosis is inherently iterative, involving hypothesis formation, information gathering, and refinement. Several studies simulate multi-turn doctor-patient interactions (Bao et al., 2023; Chen et al., 2023; Li et al., 2023; Toma et al., 2023; Liu et al., 2025d), including systems such as AMIE (Tu et al., 2024), AI Hospital (Fan et al., 2025), and MedAgentSim (Almansoori et al., 2025). APP (Zhu & Wu, 2025) explores patient-centered multi-turn consultations, while MedAgentBench (Jiang et al., 2025b) and MMD-Eval (Liu et al., 2025b) provide realistic simulation environments grounded in structured patient data. Other efforts focus on sequential stages of diagnosis. Sun et al. (2024) note that most LLM-based studies treat diagnosis as one-shot dialogue and propose a two-planner system for differential diagnosis and final prediction. Similarly, MAC (Chen et al., 2025) models two consultation stages and simulates multidisciplinary treatment. However, few studies tackle continuous multi-turn reasoning with additive evidence within a single LLM session. DiagGym (Qiu et al., 2025a) simulates the clinical environment by maintaining the most possible diagnosis and a test plan for the next action. 

## 3 Method 

We conceptualize medical diagnosis not as a static classification task, but as a dynamic, multi-turn decision process under partial observability. As illustrated in Fig. 1, our frame-work formalizes the diagnostic workflow as an Iterative Evidence-Seeking Loop. The model 3Preprint. Under review. is initially presented with a sparse observation O0 (clinical history and morphological find-ings). Rather than yielding a premature conclusion, the model must autonomously navigate the diagnostic state space by formulating a set of latent hypotheses D and strategically proposing auxiliary examinations T . As more observations Oi (i > 0) are acquired, the hypotheses gradually focus on the final, precise diagnosis. This trajectory is governed by three symbiotic modules: (1) an autonomous reasoning model trained via RLVR to optimize the diagnostic flow (Section 3.1); (2) RAGES, a high-fidelity clinical oracle that simulates deterministic laboratory feedback to close the reasoning loop (Section 3.2); and (3) a curated pathological corpus that provides the grounding for complex clinical scenarios (Section 3.3). 

3.1 Reinforcement Learning with Diagnostic Evidence-Seeking Rewards 

To elicit expert-level reasoning, we employ RLVR within the Group Relative Policy Opti-mization (GRPO) framework. We architect a tri-factor reward design that aligns the model’s trajectory with the nature of medical diagnosis. To ensure objective evaluation, we utilize a higher-capacity LLM as a reasoning verifier to assess the logical consistency of the disease hypotheses and proposed examinations.

3.1.3 Mitigating Strategic Reward Hacking 

A significant risk in medical RLVR is reward hacking (Tarek & Beheshti, 2025), where the model exploits ambiguities in medical terminology to inflate rewards. As illustrated in Fig. 1, we identify two primary failure modes: counterfactual hallucinations (e.g., gender-incompatible diagnoses) and informational vagueness (e.g., using overly broad categories). The first challenge arises from corner cases in the training data. LLMs systematically append rare diseases to every case to earn scores. The second challenge stems from ambiguities in evaluation. Verifier favors vague diagnostic descriptions that match many diseases. To mitigate these issues, we introduce a hacking penalty term Ph and improve the diagnosis verifying prompt with two mechanisms: conflict detection and differential-as-mirror .Conflict detection ensures consistency with the ground truth and basic medical knowledge, triggering the penalty if diagnoses contain factual errors or contradictions. Differential-as-mirror leverages historically referenced differential diagnoses from original cases. These diagnoses are closely related but wrong examples, serving as negative anchors. If a diagnosis matches most of these anchors, it is deemed overly vague and clinically uninformative. To facilitate the evidence acquisition phase without human-in-the-loop constraints, we propose RAGES (Retrieval-Augmented Generation-based Examination Simulator). It serves as a deterministic proxy for a pathology laboratory, bridging the gap between interactive LLM dialogue. To note, RAGES only simulates outcomes directly proposed by diagnostic models, rather than returning all the information it may exploit. More specifically, RAGES exploits the following mechanisms as in Algorithm 1: 

Faithful Recitement. The simulator first identifies the overlapped items requested by the models and reuses verified results from the case report ( Edirect ). However, given the 5Preprint. Under review. differences among the examination items, this step typically contributes only a portion of the desired results. 

Empirical Knowledge Grounding. To simulate the rest of the workup, RAGES leverages a structured knowledge base containing over 24,000 mappings between 1,629 diseases and 465 IHC markers. Each mapping records a specific probability association between diseases and test results. By retrieving relevant items ( Eretrieved ), the simulated evidence is not merely an LLM hallucination but is anchored in statistical clinical reality. 

Contextual Synthesis. A reasoning-enhanced LLM synthesizes these retrieved priors with the specific patient context (the ground truth diagnosis) to generate consistent, explainable, and biologically plausible test results with different confidence levels. A random drop of low-confidence outcomes mirrors failed examinations and improves faithfulness. 

3.3 Curated Pathological Corpus 

To support this diagnostic paradigm, we curated a high-quality corpus of complex patholog-ical cases. We compiled cases from publicly available sources, including academic journals and medical websites, and translated them into structured information. Specifically, each case is broken down into five components (Fig. 1): clinical history, microscopic findings, differential diagnosis, further examinations with their results, and the final diagnosis. The first two components provide the initial input for the first turn. The differential diagnosis is used as a differential mirror, while the examination results offer dependable evidence for constructing the second-turn input. Finally, the confirmed diagnosis is treated as the ground truth for evaluating model predictions. This structured decomposition allows us to sepa-rate the initial observation state from the acquired evidence state, providing the necessary scaffolding for training autonomous models in a partially observable environment. 

## 4 Experiments 

4.1 Implementation 

We use GPT-4 to extract key information from the raw case reports and manually verify the quality. DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI, 2025) is employed to perform RAGES. For model training, we adopt Qwen2.5-7B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024) as the base model and Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct as the reasoning verifier. The training dataset comprises 959 instances collected from DakaPath 1 and the Chinese Journal of Pathology, of which 287 contain real test results and 384 provide a referenced differential list. We use τ1 = 0.8 for cases with only initial information and τ2 = 1.5 for cases with additional test results. Pf , Ph, and Be are set to 0.5, 0.3, and 0.1, respectively. The training workflow is implemented using OpenRLHF (Hu et al., 2024) and runs on 8 H100 GPUs, with 4 GPUs dedicated to model training and the remaining 4 to the verifier. The training process takes about 40 hours, as discussed in Appendix A.6. Additional details are provided in Appendices A.9 and A.10.

The hit-at-one rate is used to measure diagnostic accuracy ( DxAcc ). To ensure a comprehensive and objective evaluation, we leverage three stronger LLMs from different sources than what we deployed during training as automatic verifiers to migrate hacking problems through merely model-to-model agreement: DeepSeek-R1 ( R1 ), Qwen2.5-Max ( QM ), and GPT-5-Minimal ( GPT-5 ). Prior work (McDuff et al., 2025) has demonstrated that LLM-based evaluation is highly consistent with human evaluation, making it a feasible surrogate. Meanwhile, we invite a human expert to evaluate a subset of 50 cases ( Human ), including 40 from the Chinese dataset and 10 from the English dataset. Notably, while our experimental evaluation utilizes a two-stage verification for empirical clarity, the architecture is inherently designed for scalable, multi-turn diagnostic rollouts. 5.3 The Fidelity of Clinical Inquiry 

A diagnostic model is only as reliable as the questions it asks. As shown in Fig. 4, we evaluate the Clinical Plausibility of the requested examinations. We use GPT-5-Minimal as an external evaluator. GPT-5 is tasked with assessing whether the suggested examination items are appropriate, given the model’s differential diagnosis. To ensure stability and reduce variance, each evaluation is repeated three times, and the pass rate is reported as the final metric. The moderate overall pass rates in current LLMs often stem from a thoroughness bias, where models request redundant examinations to verify each candidate in a broad differential list. Our SFT and RL models demonstrate superior alignment with expert-level examination protocols compared to their general-purpose baselines, reflecting a successful transition to a hypothesis-driven evidence-seeking mode. 

5.4 Ablation Study 

The ablation study investigates two key aspects: (1) the effectiveness of incorporating a rank-sensitive diagnosis reward with different τ values during RL (RL reward ablation), and (2) the contribution of the RAGES components (RAGES ablation). 

RL reward ablation. We evaluate four variants of the diagnosis reward. (1) Binary reward: where a hit receives 1 and otherwise 0, which can be seen as a limiting case when τ → 0. (2) Rank-sensitive reward with τ = 0.8 and (3) with τ = 1.5. (4) Rank-sensitive reward with dynamic τ values as the main setting. We assess performance using three metrics: differential accuracy (DiffAcc), final diagnosis accuracy (DxAcc), and the average length of initial differential lists (#DDx) to directly show how the reward function shapes the model’s diagnostic behavior. A lower τ effectively incentivizes a broader differential search, maximizing exploratory capability in the early stages. Conversely, a higher τ enforces clinical focus, compelling the model to commit to the most likely diagnoses. Our dynamic 

τ strategy outperforms all static configurations by effectively managing the uncertainty-precision trade-off. It allows the model to be exploratory with sparse information and decisive once evidence arrives, mirroring the cognitive shift of human experts. 

RAGES ablation. The efficacy of RAGES is validated in Table 5. We observe that the co-effect between faithful recitation and empirical knowledge is essential for closing the reasoning loop. The full configuration achieves the highest correctness (84.8% overall), confirming that grounding the simulator in a structured medical environment is superior to relying solely on an LLM’s raw internal knowledge. This reinforces RAGES’s role as a reliable Clinical Oracle for training and evaluating autonomous evidence-seeking models. 9Preprint. Under review. 

## 6 Conclusion and Discussion 

In this work, we have formalized and addressed a fundamental gap in current medical LLMs: the transition from passive inference to autonomous diagnostic agency. By framing diagnosis as an iterative evidence-seeking trajectory, we demonstrate that models can be trained to resolve clinical ambiguity through strategic information acquisition. Our framework, comprising the RLVR-based reasoning engine, the RAGES clinical oracle, and a curated pathological corpus, provides a robust blueprint for closed-loop medical reasoning. Empirical results show that providing a mechanism for active inquiry leads to a quantum leap in diagnostic fidelity, with our RL-trained models exhibiting a high degree of clinical priority and conciseness, even at a 7B-parameter scale. Despite these advancements, our work marks the beginning of a broader research agenda. Several high-impact directions remain. (1) While validated clinically, our rank-sensitive reward Rd is domain-agnostic. It is mathematically applicable to any hierarchical decision-making task requiring hypothesis prioritization. Future work will explore how this reward shapes exploration-to-decision transitions in broader non-medical contexts. (2) Though exploited as an offline simulator, RAGES could be integrated into online RL loops to enable multi-turn autonomous rollouts and long-term diagnostic look-ahead. Furthermore, expanding the framework to include radiology and oncology data will test its robustness in complex, multi-source informational environments. (3) Future alignment should shift from outcome-only metrics to process-based reward design. By aligning internal reasoning steps with established clinical guidelines, we can ensure the model reaches the correct diagnosis through a verifiable and trustworthy path. Overall, this work establishes a structured framework for interactive AI-assisted diagnosis. By empowering LLMs with the capacity for active information acquisition, we move closer to a future where AI serves not merely as a static encyclopedia but as a dynamic, evidence-driven partner in the clinical diagnostic process. 

## Ethics Statement 

This work leverages pathological case studies from multiple sources, raising two primary ethical considerations, i.e., patient privacy and data re-distribution. Regarding patient privacy, all case reports collected from public websites and journals were already anonymized at the source. We carefully removed any personally identifiable information from the in-house dataset, preserving only essential information such as age and gender for clinical reasoning. Regarding data distribution, we will not release our in-house dataset publicly due to institutional data protection policies. For externally sourced cases, we strictly adhered to the usage guidelines specified by each website or journal. To avoid unauthorized redistribution, we will release only the URLs linking to the original case sources, allowing other researchers to access the materials while respecting the original data ownership and licensing terms. The risk of this work may also lie in improper responses (including repetitive patterns, false information, and offensive output) since we do not specifically strengthen the safety of the model. The presented cases may contain content that is offensive.

A.6 Discussion on Efficiency 

In the main text, we report that the training process takes approximately 40 hours on 8 H100 GPUs. Time consumption arises from two main sources: the basic RL training (rollout generation, gradient computation, etc.) and the computationally intensive model-based verification process (diagnosis and examination assessment). We provide further details below. 

Inefficient GPU usage with LLM as Verifier. Using collocation and sleep strategies, Open-RLHF efficiently allocates GPUs across models with different roles. However, we could not identify a feasible interface to integrate an additional model for judgment that directly returns text. Consequently, we adopted a less efficient approach: we dedicated 4 GPUs exclusively to the reasoning verifier and relied on HTML to transfer information between models. 

LLM verifiers are computationally expensive. Beyond the framework overhead, the verifiers themselves incur significant cost. For each generation, the verifier is used twice: once for diagnostic evaluation to promote accurate diagnoses, and once for examination assessment to ensure reasonable test requests, effectively doubling the computational load. In a trial experiment without the examination judge, training time dropped to roughly 28 hours. Using a smaller verifier, such as Qwen2.5-7B, nearly halved the judging cost, but led to severe counting errors and incorrect judgments. Therefore, we ultimately hosted a Qwen2.5-32B model on 4 GPUs for the judging role, despite the inefficiency. Table 9: Inference efficiency of models. Hosted on 4 H100 GPUs with vLLM engine. A.8 Supervised Fine-tuning Details 

Following Huang et al. (2025), we create an SFT dataset generated by DeepSeek-R1 to activate the reasoning of existing LLMs on two-turn diagnosis. In the initial consultation , LLMs take clinical history and histological findings as input and think about possible differential diagnoses and appended examinations. Instead of directly giving the differential list in the original cases as a guide, we adopt a post-verification strategy. We only provide the input and generate it several times for each case. After gathering these trials, we use another LLM to judge whether the ground truth diagnosis appears in these trials, and retain the positive ones as SFT data. We chose this more complex strategy for two reasons. First, we do not expect LLMs to be bound by the original text, since different pathologists might derive different disease suspects from the same case. Second, when a differential list is provided, LLMs might have hallucinations like direct references to the original results. Based on the verified trials, we use the RAGES method to simulate the appended results. After collecting sufficient appended results, we can start the follow-up consultation . We provide LLMs with the response in the first turn and the acquired further examination 23 Preprint. Under review. results, and ask them to produce a final diagnosis after careful thinking. Also, we employ the post-verification strategy and retain as SFT data those trajectories that propose the true diagnosis. A total of 925 training samples related to initial consultations and 623 follow-up samples are constructed. For SFT, we adopt Qwen2.5-32B-Instruct (Yang et al., 2024) as the base model. We apply parameter-efficient Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2022) and enable bf16 precision to optimize training with our curated dataset. The training workflow is implemented using Llama-Factory (Zheng et al., 2024), and evaluation is conducted with vLLM (Kwon et al., 2023). We use LoRA with default hyperparameters, as α = 16 and 

r = 8. The initial learning rate is set to 5 × 10 −5 with cosine decay, and training is run for 15 epochs. The fine-tuning is carried out on 8 A100 GPUs, and the entire process completes in approximately 12 hours.
